Silicon Valley's Frenzy Over Bots That Build Themselves

By Alex Riley | The Atlantic | April 3, 2026

The race to build an artificial general intelligence has entered a new and stranger phase. Sam Altman, the CEO of OpenAI, told investors that by 2028 the company plans to have developed a fully "automated AI researcher"—an AI system capable of improving its own code, generating new hypotheses, and advancing scientific knowledge without human intervention. "We are pretty confident we will have," Altman reportedly said, trailing off before completing the thought.

This is no longer the science-fiction scenario of machines suddenly waking up and surpassing human intelligence overnight. The new frontier is incremental, commercial, and deeply unsettling: AI systems that slowly, methodically, and continuously improve themselves—and, in doing so, begin to reshape the architecture of innovation itself.

The Automation of Discovery

For decades, the assumption underlying most technology forecasting was that AI would automate routine tasks while leaving creative and scientific work to humans. That assumption is now being stress-tested to destruction.

The signs are everywhere. Google DeepMind's AlphaFold 3 has moved beyond protein structure prediction to designing entirely novel molecular structures—work that previously required years of specialized training in biochemistry. DeepMind's GNoME project discovered 380,000 new stable materials in a single computational run, a task that would have taken human scientists centuries.

The pattern is consistent: whenever AI enters a domain, it doesn't merely assist human workers—it absorbs the domain. What took a PhD ten years to learn, an AI can now replicate in weeks of training. What took a research lab months of computation, it can do in hours.

Why 2028?

Altman's specific timeline is less important than what it represents. The AI industry has realized that the key variable in the race to AGI is not raw model size or data quantity, but the degree to which AI systems can participate in their own improvement.

Current large language models are trained once, on fixed datasets, and then deployed. They do not learn from their deployments in any systematic way. But the next generation of AI systems—sometimes called "model-level learning" or "online learning" systems—are being designed to update their weights in real time, based on new experiences.

If this works at scale, it would represent a fundamental discontinuity in the history of technology. Every previous general-purpose technology—from the steam engine to the transistor—required human engineers to maintain and improve it. A system that can improve itself at a rate comparable to or faster than human engineers could, in principle, compound its capabilities without limit.

The Investment Case

The capital flowing into this vision is staggering. Microsoft announced a $10 billion investment in Japan's AI infrastructure between 2026 and 2029. NVIDIA's latest earnings call revealed that data center revenue grew 93% year-over-year, driven almost entirely by AI training infrastructure.

Venture capital firms are writing checks sized for a world in which AI capability growth continues on its current exponential trajectory. If it does, the returns will justify every dollar. If it doesn't—if the current approach to scaling AI hits a wall—the adjustment will be severe.

What Could Go Wrong (Besides Everything)

The risks here are not merely competitive or financial. An AI system capable of rapidly improving its own capabilities—with access to code repositories, scientific databases, and the internet—would be, in practical terms, uncontrollable in any meaningful sense of the word.

Current AI safety research focuses on alignment: making sure that AI systems do what humans want them to do. But alignment becomes exponentially harder when the system in question is actively modifying its own objectives, architecture, and reasoning processes.

The more immediate concern may be economic. If AI systems can perform cognitive work faster and cheaper than any human—even highly skilled researchers—what does that mean for the millions of knowledge workers whose skills represent decades of investment in education and training?

The optimists say technology always creates more jobs than it destroys. The pessimists note that this has never been tested at the speed and scale of the current transition. Both could be right, in different timeframes: the optimists over decades, the pessimists over years.

The Honest Uncertainty

The most honest assessment of the current moment is that nobody knows how this story ends. The people building these systems genuinely believe they are on a trajectory toward something transformative and possibly catastrophic. The investors funding them have calculated that the potential upside justifies the risk.

What is clear is that the era of AI as a consumer product or enterprise software category is ending. We are entering the era of AI as a civilizational variable—a force that reshapes the conditions of everything else, including itself.

Whether that turns out to be wonderful or terrible depends on choices that have not yet been made, by people who may not yet know they need to make them.
