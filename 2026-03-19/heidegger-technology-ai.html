Martin Heidegger

First published Fri Jan 31, 2025

Martin Heidegger (1889&ndash;1976) is a central figure in the
development of twentieth-century European Philosophy. His magnum
opus , Being and Time (1927), and his many essays and
lectures, profoundly influenced subsequent movements in European
philosophy, including Hannah Arendt&rsquo;s political philosophy,
Jean-Paul Sartre&rsquo;s existentialism, Simone de Beauvoir&rsquo;s
feminism, Maurice Merleau-Ponty&rsquo;s phenomenology of perception,
Hans-Georg Gadamer&rsquo;s hermeneutics, Jacques Derrida&rsquo;s
deconstruction, Michel Foucault&rsquo;s post-structuralism, Gilles
Deleuze&rsquo;s metaphysics, the Frankfurt School, and critical
theorists like Theodor Adorno, Herbert Marcuse, J&uuml;rgen Habermas,
and Georg Luk&aacute;cs. Beyond Europe, Being and Time has
influenced movements like the Kyoto School in Japan, and North
American philosophers like Hubert Dreyfus, Richard Rorty, and Charles
Taylor. His influence has extended far beyond the bounds of academic
philosophy, and his existential analysis of human existence has
inspired theorists in fields as diverse as theology, anthropology,
sociology, psychology, aesthetics, literary criticism, political
science, strategic management, and cognitive science.

The driving question in Heidegger&rsquo;s work is &ldquo;the question
of being&rdquo;&mdash;the question of the meaning or sense of
being&mdash;and he argued that our understanding of being is
temporally structured. In Being and Time , Heidegger pursued
the question of being by means of a phenomenological exploration of
the way that time structures our engagement with the world. Heidegger
was an ontological pluralist: he held that there are multiple distinct
kinds of being. Human beings or &ldquo;Dasein&rdquo;,
Heidegger argued, must be understood as beings-in-the-world, and thus
as constituted by our relationships to the practical and social
contexts that give meaning to our actions. The analytic of Dasein
underpinned his critique of post-Cartesian accounts of human beings as
subjects in an objective world. In his later work, Heidegger argued
that being is historical in a profound sense&mdash;that there has been
a sequence of distinct &ldquo;epochs&rdquo; of being, culminating in
the contemporary technological age. Heidegger argued that language and
works of art play a constitutive role in structuring these
&ldquo;being-historical worlds&rdquo;.
