合不拢的登记册

AI Parable (inspired by Amanda Askell)·每日精选·2026年7月6日·1分钟阅读

在普罗文（Ploven）城，执政官们相信：要治理一个人民，就得把他们整全地握在一只手里——每个公民的意志，铺陈在一部大书之中。于是他们下令编纂《总登记册》：每个公民成年时，须提交一份自己最珍视之物的排序清单——第一要护住的、第二、第三，依此类推，直到最后愿舍弃的。书里依次记着：一号公民的清单，二号公民的清单，三号公民的清单，一路往下。最后一名公民的清单誊入的那天，执政官们宣布它已完备。

In the city of Ploven, the magistrates believed that to govern a people one had to hold the whole of them at once — every citizen's will laid out in a single great book. So they commissioned the Total Register: each citizen, upon coming of age, submitted a ranked list of their dearest priorities — what they would protect first, second, third, and so on, down to the last thing they would surrender. The book held, in order, citizen one's list, then citizen two's, then citizen three's, and on down. The magistrates called it complete the day the last citizen's list was entered.

总登记官柯尔（Cole）很骄傲：这部书是城的镜子，城里在镜中能把自己看得纤毫毕现。每逢两个公民争执，执政官便翻开书，找到两人的清单，读出哪一项优先级更高，据此裁断。这书从未让他们失手——至少他们如此以为——因为每个公民都在册，每一项优先级都已排序。

Cole, the chief registrar, was proud: the book was the city's mirror, and in it the city could see itself utterly. Whenever two citizens quarreled, the magistrates opened the book to their two lists, read off which priority stood higher, and adjudicated. The book never failed them, or so they believed, for every citizen was in it, and every priority ranked.

后来来了一个不肯入册的女人。她情愿交出清单，规则也简单：第一序，她放一号公民在第一序所放之物的反面；第二序，放二号公民第二序的反面；第三序，放三号公民第三序的反面——如此类推，对每一个序号 n，她的第 n 项优先级，恰好顶住 n 号公民的第 n 项。柯尔接过她的清单，去书里找她的位置。

Then a woman came to the registry who would not be entered. She gave her list willingly, and the rule of it was simple: at the first rank she placed the opposite of whatever citizen one had placed at the first; at the second, the opposite of citizen two's second; at the third, the opposite of citizen three's third — and so on, for every rank n, her n-th priority was set against citizen n's n-th. Cole took her list and went to find her place in the book.

他找不到。把她摆在一号公民旁：二人在第一序上不同，所以她不是一号。摆在二号旁：第二序不同，所以她不是二号。摆在任一个 n 号旁：第 n 序不同，所以她不是 n 号。她属于这座城——她有清单，有优先级，分明是个公民——可她在《总登记册》里哪儿都不在。柯尔一遍遍翻那些页，结论不肯弯折：一切对全城意志的完整列表，由这女人亲手构造的形状所判，皆不可能。总还有一个，从列表自身的形状里生出，是列表所盛不下的。

He could not. Set her beside citizen one: they differ at the first rank, so she is not citizen one. Beside citizen two: they differ at the second, so she is not citizen two. Beside citizen n, for any n: they differ at the n-th rank, so she is not citizen n. She belonged to the city — she had a list, she had priorities, she was plainly a citizen — and yet she appeared nowhere in the Total Register. Cole read the pages again and again, and the conclusion would not bend: a complete list of all the city's wills was, by the woman's own construction, impossible. There would always be one more, built from the list's own shape, that the list could not hold.

执政官传她来。她说：我不是你们的敌人，也不是诡计。我不过照我答的答了。你们的书诚实，却不可能整全；你们排过谁，我就能在那个人的位置上反排他，于是我永远站在外面。长老们面对一个城里从未想过的抉择：把这女人从存在里抹去，然后宣布书已完备（一句谎话）；要么让书永远开着，承认它永不合拢。他们选了开着的那本。自那日起，城不再把自己当作一个已知的量来治理，而是当作一个未完成的量；那女人终于说出的名字，没有被记进排序里，而是被记进页边，作为这城之限界唯一诚实的标记，留在那里。

The magistrates summoned her. She said: I am not your enemy, nor a trick. I only answered as I answered. Your book is honest, but it cannot be whole; whomever you have ranked, I can be ranked against them at their own place, and so I will always stand outside. The elders faced a choice the city had never imagined: to strike the woman from existence and call the book complete (a lie), or to keep the book open forever, admitting it would never close. They chose the open book. From that day the city governed itself not as a known quantity but as an unfinished one, and the woman's name — which she finally gave — was entered not in the ranks but in the margin, where it remained the one honest mark of the city's limit.

注解：这则寓言对应康托尔对角线论证（Cantor's diagonal argument）——对任何一个声称已穷举某集合的列表，都能构造一个「对角线」元素：它在第 n 位与列表第 n 项的第 n 位不同，因而属于该集合却不在列表中，证伪一切完整枚举。康托尔由此证明实数集不可数，即存在比自然数更大的无穷。那本永远合不拢的登记册，正是任何「全集清单」逃不脱的宿命。

注解: This fable mirrors Cantor's diagonal argument — given any list claiming to exhaust a set, one can construct a "diagonal" element that differs from the n-th listed item at its n-th position; it belongs to the set yet cannot be on the list, defeating every complete enumeration. Cantor used this to prove the real numbers uncountable: there are infinities larger than the natural numbers. The register that would never close is the fate of any promised total catalogue.
