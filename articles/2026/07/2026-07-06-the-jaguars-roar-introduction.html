美洲虎的咆哮：导言

Juliana Barbassa·3:AM Magazine·2026年7月6日·8分钟阅读·原文

在圣保罗一座文化中心的白墙上，作为一场展示巴西五百年历史的展览的一部分，挂着两幅印第安儿童（一个男孩、一个女孩）的版画。他们的画像出自两位德国博物学家——卡尔·弗里德里希·菲利普·冯·马蒂乌斯（Carl Friedrich Philipp von Martius）与约翰·巴普蒂斯特·冯·斯皮克斯（Johann Baptist von Spix）——合著的一本书。1817 年在里约热内卢登陆后，他们用三年时间穿越全国，观察、编目并采集了数百件动植物标本，留待日后在欧洲进行分析与展示。

On the white wall of a cultural centre in São Paulo, as part of an exhibition showcasing five hundred years of Brazilian history, are lithographs of two Indigenous children, a boy and a girl. Their images were part of a book by two German naturalists, Carl Friedrich Philipp von Martius and Johann Baptist von Spix. After landing in Rio de Janeiro in 1817, they travelled across the country over the course of three years, observing, cataloguing, and collecting hundreds of specimens of flora and fauna for later analysis and display in Europe.

尽管这两位博物学家对旅途中所观察到的其他事物都做了详尽至极的笔记，但关于这两个孩子，他们除了记录其所属族群外几乎没留下任何信息。在书中他们正是这样被标注的：女孩是米拉尼亚人（Miranha），男孩是朱里人（Juri）。这些画像本身在巴西广为人知；学童也许会在教科书里偶然见到，又或者像作家米歇利尼·韦伦舒克（Micheliny Verunschk）那样，在圣保罗的这样一场展览中遇见它们，与该国繁茂自然景观和文化多样性的其他插画并列在一起。

Despite the naturalists’ painstaking notes on so much else they observed during their trip, they recorded very little information about the children other than their people of origin. And that is how they’re identified in the book: the girl a Miranha, and the boy a Juri. The images themselves are well known in Brazil; schoolchildren might come across them in textbooks, or, as was the case with the author Micheliny Verunschk, in an exhibition such as the one in São Paulo, alongside other illustrations of the country’s exuberant natural landscape and cultural diversity.

鲜为人知的是：这两个被剥夺了姓名与身世的孩子，正是科学家们带回欧洲作进一步研究的「样本」之一。他们的一生，以及他们抵达德国数月后的死亡，基本上已被抹去。《美洲虎的咆哮》（原书名 O Som do Rugido da Onça）正是从这一空缺中诞生的：源于韦伦舒克在展览中对那些版画的凝视，源于她对那个未曾被讲述的故事的追寻。这两个孩子的画像甚至在他们生前就已广为流传。马蒂乌斯、斯皮克斯及其一行人是首批深入巴西广袤内地的欧洲科学家。葡萄牙人此前一直将该国企图封锁于其他外国人之外，直到 1808 年才开放；这一政策曾令著名的德国博物学家亚历山大·冯·洪堡（Alexander von Humboldt）也吃尽苦头——他早在此前数年的 1799 年就已抵达西语南美，并对其发现著述甚丰。巴西相对的封闭状态，反倒在德国及其他地方激起了对斯皮克斯与马蒂乌斯考察成果的极大兴趣；这两个孩子及其他原住民的画像得以广泛传播，恰好满足了这种好奇。

What is not widely known: that these children, shorn of their names and their history, were among the samples the scientists carried with them back to Europe for further study. Their lives, and their deaths a few months after their arrival in Germany, have been largely erased. It is from that absence that The Jaguar’s Roar, originally published as O Som do Rugido da Onça, was born: from Verunschk’s visit to the lithographs in the exhibition, and from her search for the story not told. Images of the children became widespread even during their lifetime. Martius, Spix, and their party were among the first European scientists to explore Brazil’s vast interior. The Portuguese had kept the country closed to other foreigners until 1808, a policy that had hindered, among others, the well-known German naturalist Alexander von Humboldt, who had reached Spanish-speaking South America several years earlier, in 1799, and written extensively of his discoveries. Brazil’s relative isolation had helped feed a great interest in Germany and elsewhere for Spix’s and Martius’s findings; the dissemination of portraits of the children and other Indigenous people helped satisfy that curiosity.

对韦伦舒克而言，圣保罗展览上的那些画像成了一种执念。「那些形象对我来说再熟悉不过，」她在 2021 年的一次访谈中说，「但在那里——在原件中——与他们面对面，他们望着我，这让我很不安。」她在数年间无数次重访那场展览，试图理解这种不安的本质，追溯它的源头。她说，作为一名受过训练的历史学家，她的第一反应是展开研究，去挖掘一切能找到的关于这两个孩子的信息。但她同时也是诗人和小说家。既然几乎找不到关于二人的记录，她便开始书写他们的故事，将虚构与档案交织，塑造出一种关于「可能发生过之事」的版本——她自己的版本。

For Verunschk, the images in the São Paulo exhibition became an obsession. ‘The figures were very familiar to me,’ she said in a 2021 interview, ‘but finding them there, in the original, their faces looking at me, it was disturbing.’ She visited the exhibition countless times over several years, trying to understand the nature of this disturbance, its origin. As a trained historian, she said, her first impulse was to conduct research, to dig for whatever information might be available about the children. But she’s also a poet and a novelist. Not finding much recorded about the two, she began to write their story, weaving together the fictional and the archival to shape one version – her version – of what might have been.

小说以一段史实旅程为起点：1820 年，一个女孩和一个男孩被斯皮克斯与马蒂乌斯从巴西的亚马孙河流域带往德国的巴伐利亚宫廷。在这条叙事线索之上，韦伦舒克又编织了另一条故事线，它设定在当下的巴西，以若泽法（Josefa）为中心——她是圣保罗的一位年轻女子，在一场展览中偶然见到那两个印第安孩子的画像。这次相遇，尤其是与那女孩画像的对视，令若泽法深受震动。她开始反思那个孩子的命运，也开始审视自己的身份，最终动身前往德国寻找答案。

The novel takes as its starting point the historical journey of a girl and a boy who were taken from the Amazon River basin, in Brazil, to the Bavarian court, in Germany, by Spix and Martius in 1820. Into this narrative, Verunschk weaves another story, set in present-day Brazil and centred on Josefa, a young woman in São Paulo who comes across a portrait of the Indigenous children in an exhibition. The encounter, particularly with the image of the girl, leaves Josefa shaken. She begins to reflect on the child’s fate and on her own identity, eventually setting off for Germany in search of answers.

在韦伦舒克的小说里，这个女孩相信自己很小的时候就受到一个美洲虎神灵的庇护。美洲虎的形象在故事中充当一条贯穿始终的线索。作为这片大陆上最大的掠食者，美洲虎长久以来既是迷恋的对象，也是恐惧的源泉。它是原住民神话和巴西民间传说中的神话形象，常作为力量与知识的原型出现，有时愿意将其本领借予人类，为善或为恶。关于人变形为美洲虎以寻求复仇、施加暴行，或做出人类无法（或不肯）为之的行径的故事，流传于歌谣与传说之中，并被吸纳进文学，乃至一部广受欢迎的肥皂剧里。在欧洲度过的那几个月悲痛与疏离之中，反复出现的美洲虎形象成为女孩力量的源泉，也维系着她与故土的联结。最终，美洲虎成为她复仇的工具，将自己的声音与力量借予这个孩子，击碎了她被迫陷入的沉默——一个没有名字的女孩，墙上的一幅画像。

In Verunschk’s novel, the girl believes she had been taken under the protection of a jaguar entity when she was very young. The jaguar’s presence serves as a connecting thread in the story. As the largest predator on the continent, the jaguar has long been a source of fascination and terror. It is a mythical figure in Indigenous mythologies and Brazilian folklore, often appearing as an archetype of power and knowledge, willing at times to lend its skills to humans, for good or evil. Stories of people shapeshifting into jaguars to seek revenge, wreak violence, or act out in ways humans can’t (or won’t) appear in songs and stories, and have been incorporated into literature and even a popular soap opera. During the girl’s months of grief and alienation in Europe, the recurring figure of the jaguar serves as a source of strength and a connection to home. Ultimately, the jaguar becomes an instrument of her revenge, lending the child its voice and its strength, shattering the passivity she’d been reduced to – a girl without a name, an image on a wall.

通过这种方式，韦伦舒克直面了那些关于巴西自然与社会的观念，而这些观念在一定程度上可追溯到斯皮克斯与马蒂乌斯。时至今日，他们的目光仍在帮助定义巴西——即便对巴西人自己而言也是如此。

In her approach, Verunschk is confronting perspectives on Brazil’s nature and society that have their roots, in part, in Spix and Martius. Their gaze, even now, helps define Brazil, even for Brazilians.

返回欧洲后，两位博物学家出版了一部旅行记《巴西游记》（Travels in Brazil），其中收录了两个孩子的画像。书中也记载了科学家们对所遇原住民的评断：「印第安人的气质几乎完全未开化，表现为一种冷漠迟钝。心灵的一切力量，甚至感官中更为精微的愉悦，似乎都处于昏睡状态，」他们写道，「他们与殷勤、感恩、友谊、谦逊、雄心，以及一切装点人类社会的细腻而高贵的情感无缘；迟钝、缄默、对万事万物漠不关心，印第安人所运用的，不过是他天生的敏锐感官、他的狡黠，以及他持久的记忆罢了。」

Upon their return to Europe, the naturalists published an account of the journey, Travels in Brazil, that included the images of the children. It also included the scientists’ reflections on the Indigenous people they encountered: ‘The temperament of the Indian is almost wholly undeveloped, and appears as phlegm. All the powers of the soul, nay, even the more refined pleasures of the senses, seem to be in a state of lethargy,’ they wrote. ‘Strangers to complaisance, gratitude, friendship, humility, ambition, and, in general, to all delicate and noble emotions which adorn human society; obtuse, reserved, sunk in indifference to everything, the Indian employs nothing but his naturally acute senses, his cunning, and his retentive memory.’

1826 年斯皮克斯早逝之后，马蒂乌斯继续大量著述，整理二人的动植物学发现，并逐渐被视为有关该国的权威。1840 年，当巴西精英阶层苦于为这个新独立国家塑造身份认同之时，巴西历史地理学会设立一项奖项，征集最佳论文以确立研究本国历史的指导方针。获奖者不是别人，正是马蒂乌斯。

Following Spix’s early death in 1826, Martius continued to publish extensively, writing up their zoological and botanical finds and coming to be regarded as an authority on the country. In 1840, as the Brazilian elite struggled to shape the identity of the newly independent country, its Historical and Geographic Institute offered a prize for the best essay setting out guidelines for how to approach its history. The winner was none other than Martius.

他的论文为巴西勾勒出一种早期图景：一个由「独特的种族和谐」所定义的国家，借用了众水汇流成一河的隐喻，认为混合的种族之水汇成一条河流——尽管其中等级依旧存在，因为「葡萄牙血统的强劲河流理应吞没印第安人和埃塞俄比亚人种这两条细小支流」。马蒂乌斯笔下那个「三大人种并立共存、方式前所未见」的巴西形象，在随后的数十年间被进一步阐发并奉为圭臬，成为国家形象的一部分，从而对葡萄牙殖民计划的惨烈代价、对大陆原住民的屠戮，以及作为经济支柱的奴隶贸易，都轻轻一笔带过。

His essay framed an early view of Brazil as a country defined by a ‘peculiar racial harmony’, drawing on the metaphor of a single river forming from mixed racial waters, albeit one in which a hierarchy remained, since the ‘powerful river of Portuguese blood ought to absorb the small tributaries of the Indian and the Ethiopian races’. The idea of Brazil as a country in which ‘three human races live side by side, in a manner unheard-of,’ as Martius wrote, would be developed and embraced in the decades to come as part of a national image that shrugged off the terrible cost of the Portuguese colonial project, the slaughter of the continent’s Indigenous people, and the slave trade that was the backbone of the economy.

时至今日，斯皮克斯与马蒂乌斯穿越该国的航行仍被颂为那个时代最重要的科学考察之一。他们的成果仍定期现身于巴西最受推崇的文化机构：除在圣保罗的常设展中收入若干版画外，仅就近而言，2017 年里约热内卢办过一场展览，2021 年又有一场。而时至今日，他们对巴西、对其人民、对其山川景观的视角——更广泛地说，一种西方的、欧洲中心的视角——在该国依旧占据主导。

To this day, Spix and Martius’s voyage through the country continues to be celebrated as one of the most important scientific explorations of its time. Their work continues to appear regularly in Brazil’s most esteemed cultural institutions: beyond the inclusion of some prints in the permanent exhibition in São Paulo, there was an exhibition in Rio de Janeiro in 2017 and another in 2021, to mention some of the most recent. And to this day, their perspective on Brazil, its people, its landscapes – and a Western, Eurocentric perspective, more broadly – continues to hold sway in the country.

韦伦舒克把这部小说当作一面棱镜，借此折射巴西文化与历史的若干面向，尤其是那些揭示数百年来人们对原住民的态度、以及国家与其他势力对其所作所为的种种。她将关于原住民传说、民间传统以及若干历史片段的研究揉入笔端：1699 年王室军队对数百名帕亚库人（Paiacu）的屠杀；葡萄牙王室曾以保护土地为条件换取某一原住民群体助其征战，事后却拒不兑现承诺的一桩旧事。随后她又引入当代报纸上关于巴西原住民遭杀害的标题与引文，映照出多少事情至今未改。

Verunschk uses the novel as a prism through which to view aspects of Brazil’s culture and history, particularly elements that reveal attitudes toward the Indigenous and their treatment by the state and other actors over the centuries. Into her writing she folds research on Indigenous lore, folk traditions, and snippets of this history: a massacre of hundreds of Paiacu in 1699 by troops representing the crown; an episode when the Portuguese royal family offered another Indigenous group protection for their lands in exchange for help fighting a war, only to fail to deliver on that promise. She later brings in headlines and quotes from contemporary newspapers speaking of present-day killings of Indigenous people in Brazil, reflecting how much has not changed.

尽管小说的背景是历史性的，韦伦舒克的笔触却透出一种真切可感的紧迫。她在博物馆展览中所见到的那些版画，几乎未加任何背景说明便被陈列出来——这是一场绵延至今、仍在持续的抹除。她对此有切肤之痛，而她的文字也留下了这一痛感的痕迹。为抗衡这种抹除，小说将那些常常只被当作被发现、被描述、被研究对象之人的视角置于中心。它让孩子的声音与探险者、与资助此次远征的王室成员、与那座被带往的宫殿中的其他劳作者的声音并置——甚至与河流等地理特征的声音并置。

Although the backdrop of the novel is historical, there is a palpable urgency to Verunschk’s prose. She experienced the lithographs in the museum exhibition, presented as they were with little added context, as part of an enduring, ongoing erasure. She had a visceral reaction to them, and her writing bears traces of it. To counterbalance this erasure, the novel centres the points of view of people still so frequently rendered as objects of discovery, description, and study. It introduces the children’s voices alongside the voices of the explorers, of members of the royal family that funded the expedition, of others who worked in the palace where the children were taken – and even of geographic features like rivers.

《美洲虎的咆哮》屡屡触及讲故事与语言之于身份认同的重要性。一个当代的非原住民作家，凭什么自认能进入两个十九世纪初生于亚马孙的原住民孩子的视角——这一问题在小说开篇便被坦然承认。为这两个孩子写一部小说，难道不又是另一种窃取，另一种形式的抹除吗？故事伊始，叙述者便直面这一张力：「这是死者的声音，死者的语言，用死者的文字写成。这一切都满是裂痕，诚然如此，可除了透过裂缝讲述这个故事，我还能怎样？就像一株植物冲破砖的坚不可摧，它的根在黑暗中游走，那叶片的蓬勃强行塑造出新的景观，这个故事向着阳光伸展。」

The Jaguar’s Roar touches often on the importance of storytelling and of language as essential to identity. The question of how a present-day, non-Indigenous writer can presume to inhabit the perspectives of two Indigenous children born in the Amazon in the early nineteenth century is acknowledged early in the novel. Isn’t writing a novel about these children another theft, another erasure of sorts? Early on, the narrator addresses this tension: ‘This is the voice of the dead, the language of the dead, in the writing of the dead. All of it is riven with faults, true, but what can I do except tell this story through the cracks? Like a plant that breaks through the imperviousness of brick, its roots prowling the dark, the vigour of its leaves imposing a new landscape, this story reaches for the sun.’

韦伦舒克带着一个明确的意图来写这部小说：不让这段叙事仅以单一声音、仅扎根于某一特定地点、时间与世界观念来讲述。为融入更多视角，她做了大量研究，并把创作过程当作一次用心倾听，承认并吸纳那些纷繁多样的神话、那些理解世界的诸种方式，以及那些可能构成小说中心那一男一女两个孩子之世界的共同传说。她在 2021 年的访谈中说，她所追求的是一种坦然承认的集体作者性。「

Verunschk approached the novel with a clear intention that this narrative not be told in only one voice, rooted in one particular place, time, and world view. To fold in additional perspectives, she did extensive research and approached the creative process as an act of careful listening, acknowledging and folding in the many, variegated myths, ways of understanding the world, and shared tales that might have made up the world of the boy and the girl at the centre of the novel. What she aimed for is a sort of overtly acknowledged collective authorship, she said in the 2021 interview. ‘

「我是个讲故事的人，」她说，但「没有一个故事是孤立讲述的。每个故事都有一个先于它的故事。它有自己的丝线，把它与从前的一切、与先于它的人们、与其他的声音相连。」作为译者，我受到一种挑战的吸引——想要公正地对待韦伦舒克这部丰富的复调之作。《美洲虎的咆哮》的世界里，毁灭是科学探求的另一面，文化在此碰撞，常常伴随着流血。她的文字让多种语言——欧洲的与原住民的——的词汇，与一种充斥着地域色彩和时代错位、刻意口语化的葡萄牙语变体并存，甚至还有完全生造的词，例如美洲虎这一形象所用的某些词。河流会说话，它们的叙述在时间中回旋、打转、往复。有时，一串串词从书页上倾泻而下，例如林中动物的种类，或巴西用来指称美洲虎及其同类的诸多名字，仿佛以言语呈现自然与文化的丰饶：

I’m a storyteller,’ she said, but, ‘no story is told in isolation. Every story has a story that precedes it. It has strands, connecting it to what came before, to the people who came before, to other voices.’ As a translator, I was drawn to the challenge of doing justice to Verunschk’s richly polyphonic work. The world of The Jaguar’s Roar is one in which destruction is the flip side of scientific enquiry, where cultures collide, frequently amid bloodshed. Her prose allows vocabulary from multiple languages, European and Indigenous, to exist alongside variations of an aggressively colloquial Portuguese peppered with regionalisms and anachronisms, and even entirely made-up words, such as some used by the figure of the jaguar. Rivers speak, and their narratives eddy and swirl and loop through time. At times, lists of words tumble across the page, such as types of animals in a forest, or the many names used to refer to jaguars and their kin in Brazil, in a sort of verbal rendering of a natural and cultural abundance:

呼唤我吧，我将披着我众多的毛皮而来，以群星的伪装而来，suaçurana，我将前来。斑豹、美洲狮、黑豹。我会来的，jaguaretê，我会来。Acanguassú，我将前来。Acanjaruna，我将前来。Ianovare，我将前来。Jaguapinima，我将前来。Ñanguarichã，我将前来。Niguciédo-senjo，我将前来。Pacová-sororoca，我将前来。Mingoêdo-sengue，我将前来。Jagoareté-apiaba，我将前来。Tigre，我将前来。Canguçuzinho-do-campo，我将前来。Maracajá，我将前来。Jagoaracucu，我将前来。Jaguatirica，我将前来。Jaguapitangussu，我将前来。Iaguar，iauaretê，我将前来。Tipai uu，我将前来。我将前来，把你理当拥有的东西交还给你，你的美洲虎之皮。

Call me and I’ll come in my many pelts, in the guise of stars, suaçurana, I’ll come. Spotted leopard, cougar, black panther. I’ll come, jaguaretê, I will. Acanguassú, I’ll come. Acanjaruna, I’ll come. Ianovare, I’ll come. Jaguapinima, I’ll come. Ñanguarichã, I’ll come. Niguciédo-senjo, I’ll come. Pacová-sororoca, I’ll come. Mingoêdo-sengue, I’ll come. Jagoareté-apiaba, I’ll come. Tigre, I’ll come. Canguçuzinho-do-campo, I’ll come. Maracajá, I’ll come. Jagoaracucu, I’ll come. Jaguatirica, I’ll come. Jaguapitangussu, I’ll come. Iaguar, iauaretê, I’ll come. Tipai uu, I’ll come. I’ll come and I’ll give you what’s yours by right, your jaguar skin.

整部小说中，取自信件或日记的档案摘录、数百年前的科学或艺术论著，以及歌德（Goethe）或席勒（Schiller）的引文，与巴西民间传说元素及梦呓般的遁走曲并置，共同记录下那些被暴力与深重流离模糊了现实边界的人生。这种语言、语域与视角的碰撞，既具建设性又具毁灭性，折射出某种属于巴西的东西——无论昨日还是今日；在韦伦舒克的笔下，那些裂痕被一一曝露。

Throughout the novel, archival extracts from letters or diaries, scientific or artistic treatises from centuries past, and quotes from Goethe or Schiller, co-exist with elements of Brazilian folklore and dreamlike fugues that help chronicle lives in which violence and a profound dislocation have blurred the lines of reality. This clash of languages, registers, and perspectives, at once productive and destructive, reflects something of Brazil, then and now; in Verunschk’s writing, the fractures are exposed.

原文如此多语交错，以至于读葡萄牙语版的巴西读者也无法字字看懂。于是我选择将极少数词保留原貌。我希望这能让韦伦舒克文字中那份丰饶与有意的迷失感略略透出。例如，当一位米拉尼亚女子即将生下故事核心的那个女孩，在一座「maloca」——一种粗朴的棚屋——中待产时，我未将该词译出；又如德国的一条河流初次向小女孩自我介绍，低语着「fluss-fluss-fluss」——这个妙趣横生的德语拟声词意指河流——我也照旧保留。当一位未来的巴伐利亚国王致信表亲，求娶其女为妻时，他在恳求中夹进了法语。我同样原样保留：「我爱卡罗利妮（Karoline）公主，亲爱的表亲，更甚者，j'en suis fou。」在翻译那些带有强烈地域方言色彩、巴西读者一望可知但在英语中无对应物的段落时，我倚重语调乃至语序来传达这种转换，却避开了那些可能读起来像戏仿的变体拼写与缩合。在《美洲虎的咆哮》中，韦伦舒克创造了一部叙事，把不谐与疏离——以及一份美的尺度——还给了一个两百多年来被缩减为墙上一帧静默肖像的女孩的故事。正如韦伦舒克所承认的，这不全是她的故事；它是关于一个本质上属于巴西的故事的一个版本，属于她的版本。愿还有别的版本。

The text is so multilingual that even Brazilian readers of the Portuguese version wouldn’t have understood every word. So I opted to leave a select few words as I found them. I hope this allows some of the fecundity and the intentional disorientation of Verunschk’s prose to show through. For example, when a Miranha woman prepares to give birth to the girl who will be at the centre of the story in a ‘maloca’, a rustic hut, I left the word untranslated; and when a river in Germany first introduces itself to the young girl by whispering ‘fluss-fluss-fluss’ – the wonderfully onomatopoeic German word for river – I left that too. When a future Bavarian king writes a letter asking his cousin for her daughter’s hand in marriage, he peppers his plea with French. I let that stand as well: ‘I love the princess Karoline, dear cousin, better yet, j’en suis fou.’ When translating sections in a strongly regional dialect that would have been recognisable to a Brazilian reader, but has no equivalent in English, I leaned into cadence and at times word order to convey the shift, but eschewed alternate spellings and contractions that might read as parody. In The Jaguar’s Roar, Verunschk creates a narrative that returns dissonance and alienation, as well as a measure of beauty, to the story of a girl reduced for more than two hundred years to a silent portrait on a wall. As Verunschk acknowledges, this is not her story; it is one version, her version, of a quintessentially Brazilian story. May there be others.
