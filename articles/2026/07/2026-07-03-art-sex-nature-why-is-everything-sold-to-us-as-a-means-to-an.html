艺术、性、自然：为什么一切都被当作手段而非目的本身卖给我们？

Julian Baggini·The Guardian·2026年7月3日·13分钟阅读·原文

几十年来，米高梅（Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Studios）出品的电影都以咆哮的雄狮利奥（Leo）开场，它头戴花环，环绕着「ars gratia artis」的铭文：为艺术而艺术。鉴于米高梅是一头赚钱的巨兽，我们或许会怀疑这句高调言辞的真诚。不过，它确乎表达了人们制作电影为数不多的正当理由之一。为艺术之外的任何东西——利润、自我宣传、宣传鼓动——而艺术，根本算不上真正的艺术，至少不是其最纯粹意义上的艺术。

For decades, films out of the Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Studios have opened with Leo the roaring lion, garlanded with the motto ars gratia artis: art for art’s sake. Given that MGM is a money-making behemoth, we might doubt the sincerity of this high-minded sentiment. Still, it certainly expresses one of the few legitimate reasons why people should make movies. Art for the sake of anything else – profit, self-promotion, propaganda – isn’t really art at all, or at least not in its purest sense.

因此，当我看到英国国家艺术通行证（National Art Pass）近期的一则广告时，不免有些震惊。这张通行证让持卡者免费或打折进入英国各地的画廊和博物馆。其标语「See more. Live more」（看得更多，活得更丰富）听起来没错：艺术确实丰富我们的生活。但结果发现，这里的「更多」纯粹是量上的，而非质上的。主口号宣称「用艺术为你的生命添上几岁」，紧跟着一句：「在画廊和博物馆中度过时光，或许能帮你活得更久。」艺术不再为艺术而存在，而是为了你的心脏——而且是肉体那颗，不是精神那颗。这种围绕艺术的宣传已无处不在，英格兰艺术委员会（Arts Council England）也在推广这样的理念：「参与创意与文化活动，对个人和社区均有已证实的健康益处。」

It therefore came as a bit of a shock to see a recent advert for the National Art Pass, which gives holders free or discounted entry to galleries and museums around the UK. The tagline “See more. Live more” sounded right: art does indeed enrich our lives. But it turned out that the “more” here was purely quantitative, not qualitative. “Grow some years on to your life with art,” proclaimed the main slogan, followed by: “Spending time in galleries and museums could help you live longer.” Art not for art’s sake, but for your heart’s sake, the fleshy not the spiritual one at that. This messaging around the arts has become ubiquitous, with Arts Council England promoting the idea that “engaging in creative and cultural activities has proven health benefits for individuals and communities”.

我或许被这张海报震惊到了，却并不意外。很久以来，我一直在私下哀叹万物的工具化：似乎再没有什么是因其自身而有价值的了，一切只被看作服务于某种功利功能才有用。我最早嗅到这股可悲风气是在 2010 年，当时我不幸要评论格雷琴·鲁宾（Gretchen Rubin）的著作《幸福计划》（The Happiness Project）——一份对一年间锲而不舍追求幸福生活的记述。其中一段让我触动之深，至今几乎能逐字复述。某天与丈夫的相处开局不顺，但一道歉之后，鲁宾写道：「我们拥抱了——至少六秒钟，我恰好从研究中得知，这是促进催产素和血清素分泌所需的最低时长，而这两种化学物质能提升情绪、促进情感联结。那一刻的紧张便过去了。」

I may have been shocked by the poster, but I was not surprised by it. For a long time, I have been privately lamenting the instrumentalisation of everything: how nothing seems to be of value in itself any more but is only seen as useful in the service of some utilitarian function. I first got wind of this lamentable trend in 2010, when I had the misfortune to review Gretchen Rubin’s book The Happiness Project, an account of a year in relentless pursuit of the happy life. One passage struck me so hard I can almost recall it word for word today. A day with her husband gets off to a sticky start but, after an apology, Rubin writes: “We hugged – for at least six seconds, which, I happened to know from my research, is the minimum time necessary to promote the flow of oxytocin and serotonin, mood-boosting chemicals that promote bonding. The moment of tension passed.”

我由此得到一个令人心寒的画面：一个女人拥抱丈夫，不仅是出于爱或情意，还是为了释放激素、减轻压力。这些句子凸显出，她的幸福计划如何引导她做每件事时都把改善自身情绪记在心上。其余一切似乎都不那么重要，连真相也不例外。在结束她这年把自身当作「幸福机器」来操弄的实验之时，她反思了哪些变了、哪些没变。「也许我只是在看自己想看的东西，」她思忖道，旋即又补一句：「也许吧，可谁在乎呢？」管它真不真，只要让你感觉更好就行。

I was left with the chilling image of a woman holding her husband not only out of love or affection but in order to release hormones and reduce her stress. Those sentences highlighted how her happiness project had led her to do everything with the improvement of her mood in mind. Nothing else seemed to matter as much, even truth. At the end of her year-long experiment in treating herself as a felicific machine, she reflected on what had or had not changed. “Maybe I was seeing what I wanted to see,” she wondered, only to add: “Maybe, but who cares?” Whatever makes you feel better, true or not.

从目睹为幸福而拥抱，到目睹为长寿而创意，其间这些年，我见过无数类似的例子：生活中一切美好的事物，都不是因其自身被推崇，而是因它们带来的物质好处而被宣扬。这种工具化已如此阴险地成为常态，以至于我们甚至察觉不到它的怪异，更遑论它的错误。我们似乎也没意识到它究竟有多普遍。然而它的影响深远，让我们一次又一次地看不清生活中真正有价值的东西。

In the years between witnessing hugging for happiness and creativity for longevity, I have seen countless other examples of all the things that are good in life being promoted not for their own sake but for the material benefits they bring. This instrumentalisation has become normalised so insidiously that we don’t even notice that it is odd, let alone wrong. Nor do we seem to be aware of quite how pervasive it is. Yet its effects are profound, leading us to lose sight again and again of what is truly of value in life.

在为出了什么问题开出处方、又如何医治之前，我需要为「万物皆在被工具化」这一看似夸张的论断作一辩护。它或许像修辞上的夸大其词，但我着实想不出有什么有价值的东西，未曾被人因其功利好处、而非其内在价值而赞颂过。拿做礼拜来说。多数信教者认为崇拜是一项虔诚的本分，而非进入天堂的实用手段。然而如今，连基督徒也不时这样讲——比如黛博拉·詹金斯（Deborah Jenkins）在《首要基督教》（Premier Christianity）杂志上援引研究指出：「成为教会社群的一员，能延长寿命、减轻抑郁、促进心理健康。」

Before offering a diagnosis for what has gone wrong and how to cure it, I need to defend the seemingly hyperbolic claim that everything is becoming instrumentalised. It may seem a rhetorical exaggeration, but I genuinely find it difficult to think of anything worthwhile that hasn’t been celebrated by someone for its utilitarian benefits ahead of its intrinsic merits. Take churchgoing. Most believers hold that worship is a devotional duty rather than a pragmatic means of getting into heaven. Today, however, it is not uncommon to hear even Christians, such as Deborah Jenkins in Premier Christianity magazine, pointing to research that: “Being part of a church community can lengthen life, reduce depression and promote positive mental health.”

我翻过的一本书，以身体健康为由提倡祈祷，并援引一项研究，称其「发现每日礼拜祈祷对心血管系统、血液，以及肌肉和骨骼有显著的医学益处」。当然，若被质疑，没人会说这些才是修行的最佳理由。但这并不妨碍他们把这些理由当作很棒的理由端出来。况且，比起声称一位全然慈爱的造物主上帝当真在意你周日早晨如何度过，这些理由更可信，也确实更科学。

A book I flicked through advocated prayer for physical health, pointing to a study that “found significant medical benefits on the cardiovascular system, blood, as well as muscle and bone resulting from the solat daily prayer”. Of course, if challenged, none would say these are the best reasons to practise religion. But it doesn’t stop them offering these reasons as very good ones. Furthermore, they are more credible and certainly more scientific than claims that an all-loving creator god really thinks it’s important how you spend your Sunday mornings.

更俗世一些的，是连高潮都被给了工具化的理由。2015 年《电讯报》（Telegraph）一则标题——「科学家称：每日一次高潮，或可远离前列腺癌」——概括了一种如今广为流传的信念：男人做爱或自慰的最好理由之一，不是快感、亲密或释放性张力，而是保护健康。

More profanely, we are even given instrumental reasons to orgasm. A headline from the Telegraph in 2015 – “An orgasm a day could keep prostate cancer away, scientists claim” – summed up a now widely shared belief that one of the best reasons for a man to have sex or masturbate is not pleasure, intimacy or the release of sexual tension but to protect his health.

如果你想寻找那些人们因其自身而珍视的事物，指望找到一个未被颂扬过其对健康、财富或福祉有益的，恐怕要找很久。北方歌剧团（Opera North）的网站列举了唱歌的十大好处，其中只有一条——「让你表达自我」——与艺术和创造力有关。其余包括「让你感觉更好」、「增强肺功能」、「帮你战胜压力、放松身心」、「有助于改善记忆」、「在生活艰难时能帮上忙」以及「提升自信」。

If you were to search for things people value in their own right, in the hope of finding one that hasn’t been praised for its benefits for health, wealth or wellbeing, you’d be looking for a long time. The Opera North website lists 10 benefits of singing, and only one – it lets you express yourself – has anything to do with art and creativity. The others include “makes you feel better”, “enhances lung function”, “helps you beat stress and relax”, “helps improve memory”, “can help when life gets tough” and “boosts your confidence”.

许多倡导重新亲近自然的人，所持理由恰恰诉诸那种功利、以自我为中心的享乐主义，而正是这种主义让人类与大地失去联系。国民信托（National Trust）谈论「在自然中漫步如何有益于福祉」，而日益流行的「森林浴」（forest bathing）则鼓励我们把林地当作某种名副其实的「步入式诊所」。这些自命的自然代言者似乎漏掉了一个反讽：如果我们走进林间是因为树木能为我们做什么，那我们抱着的是和那些砍树者同样的榨取式、开发式心态。

Many people who advocate for reconnecting with nature do so with reasons that are designed to appeal to the very same utilitarian, self-centred hedonism that is to blame for humanity losing touch with the Earth in the first place. The National Trust talks of how “walking in nature can help wellbeing” while the growing popularity of “forest bathing” encourages us to use woodland as though it were a kind of literal walk-in clinic. These would-be advocates for nature seem to miss the irony that if we go out among the trees because of what they can do for us, we’re going with the same exploitative, extractive mindset as those who cut them down.

Shinrin-yoku，即「森林浴」，起源于日本，是一种疗法形式。摄影：Pamela Joe McFarlane/Getty连哲学这门无私求智之学，也未能逃过工具化的诅咒。大学仅说自己开设的项目能让你探询存在的一些最根本问题，已经不够了。如今的问题 decidedly 更加「底线化」：哲学如何帮你买房，或攒够养老金？哲学被例行当作「可迁移思维能力」的 trainers，而这些能力最容易迁移到哪里，也一目了然：职场。剑桥大学（University of Cambridge）哲学系就专门设了一整个网页，罗列它所教授的五项「简历友好」技能：智力、沟通、组织、人际与研究。

Shinrin-yoku, or ‘forest bathing’, originated in Japan as a form of therapy. Photograph: Pamela Joe McFarlane/GettyEven philosophy, the disinterested pursuit of wisdom, has fallen prey to the curse of instrumentalisation. It is no longer enough for universities to say that their programmes allow you to explore some of the most fundamental questions of existence. Now the questions are of a decidedly more bottom-line sort: how will philosophy help you buy a house or build your pension pot? Philosophy is routinely sold as a trainer of “transferable thinking skills”, and it’s clear where these most transfer to: the world of work. The Faculty of Philosophy at the University of Cambridge has a whole webpage devoted to five résumé-friendly skills it teaches: intellectual, communication, organisational, interpersonal and research.

工具化最致命之处，在于它作用于我们与他人一起做、或为他人做的事。康德（Immanuel Kant）将此视为「绝对命令」（categorical imperative）——道德的绝对要求——「无论在你自己身上还是任何他人身上，始终同时把人当作目的，而绝不仅仅当作手段」。我们用来描述对他人工具化的那些词，正映照出我们认为它有多腐蚀人心：非人化、物化、剥削。正因如此，对社会联结的工具化既是不道德的，也是自我挫败的。一旦我们开始凸显社会关系能为我们做什么，我们就把其中涉及的其他人当作了纯粹的自利工具。

Instrumentalisation is most pernicious when it applies to things we do with and for others. Immanuel Kant saw it as a “categorical imperative” – an absolute demand of morality – to “treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means”. The words we use to describe the instrumentalisation of others echo how corrupting we think it is: dehumanisation, objectification, exploitation. That is why the instrumentalisation of social connection is immoral as well as self-defeating. If we start to foreground what social relationships do for us, we treat the other people involved as mere tools for self-advancement.

我甚至还没穷尽那些已例行工具化的活动清单。还可以加上：园艺、运动、露营、游泳、参与社运、志愿服务、烤面包、做手工、写日记、欢笑、说「谢谢」。我们越来越多地问的不是它们本身有什么好，而是它们能为我们带来什么好处。而所谓「好」，我们指的是健康、财富与世俗的成功。

I haven’t even begun to exhaust the list of activities that have become routinely instrumentalised. Among those we could add are gardening, playing sport, camping, swimming, campaigning, volunteer work, baking bread, crafts, keeping a diary, laughing, saying “thank you”. We increasingly ask not what is good about them but what good they can do for us. And by “good” we mean health, wealth and worldly success.

那些因自然、艺术、学习、友谊等本身而爱它们的人，或许会觉得突出它们的工具性好处有些令人不快，可这样做又有什么坏处呢？毕竟，过着工具化生活的人与没有的人，可能在做的恰恰是同样的事。

Those who love nature, art, learning, friendship and so on for their own sake may find it distasteful to spotlight their instrumental benefits, but what is the harm in doing so? After all, someone living an instrumentalised life and someone who is not might be doing exactly the same things.

这一异议忽略了一个事实：好的生活不仅取决于我们做什么，更取决于我们怎么做。两个文化日程相同的人，可能去看同样的展览、看同样的电影、听同样的音乐，但如果他们的动机根本不同，那么他们栖居的世界也就根本不同。

This objection misses the fact that a good life does not only depend on what we do, but how we do it. Two people with the same cultural calendars may go to the same exhibitions, watch the same films and listen to the same music, but if their motivations are fundamentally different then so are the worlds they inhabit.

要明白为何如此，我们得回到这个根本问题：任何事物为何有价值。亚里士多德（Aristotle）是最早——但绝非最后——观察到这一点的人之一：我们做有些事是作为手段以达目的，另一些事则是作为目的本身。只有后者具有内在价值，而手段只具有外在价值。若问生命中终极价值何在，显然在那些具有内在价值的事物之中。

To understand why, we have to go back to the basic question of why anything has value. Aristotle was one of the first but certainly not the last to observe that we do some things as means to ends and other things as ends in themselves. Only the latter have intrinsic value, while means to ends have mere extrinsic value. If we ask where the ultimate value in life lies, it is clearly in things with intrinsic value.

这点智慧太无争议，几乎可被斥为老生常谈。但它值得在各时代、在人生各阶段反复重申，因为我们太容易被纯粹的工具性之物裹挟，偏离真正有价值的东西。金钱当然是最明显的例子。金钱之重要仅在于它能买到什么，可用来获取我们最看重的许多东西。然而，人似乎天生就想积攒越来越多，永远觉得不够，于是把本可与挚爱共度的时光、珍视的活动都挪作了他用。

This piece of wisdom is so uncontroversial it could be dismissed as platitudinous. But it merits repeating across the ages and across all our life stages because it is so easy to be sucked away from what has real value by purely instrumental goods. Money is of course the clearest example. Money is important only for what it can buy and can be used to obtain many of the things we most value. Yet it seems all too human to try to accumulate more and more of it, never believing one has enough, diverting us from time with loved ones and cherished activities.

追求外在之物而非内在之物，是相当常见的错误。但「万物工具化」更进一步。它不仅让我们分心于一切本身即好的事物；它还褫夺这些事物本身的内在价值，把它们变成纯粹的手段。更糟的是，这些目的本身甚至并不具有内在价值。

Pursuing extrinsic rather than intrinsic goods is a common enough mistake. But the instrumentalisation of everything takes it one step further. It doesn’t just distract us from all the things that are good in themselves; it strips these very things of their intrinsic value and turns them into mere means to ends. Worse, these ends are not even of value in themselves.

想想工具化所服务的都是什么：健康、财富与心理福祉。这些都如此显而易见地令人向往，以至于我们很容易忽略它们没有一个是具有内在价值的。财富显然如此，但身心健康同样如此。

Think about what instrumentalisation serves: health, wealth and psychological wellbeing. These are all so obviously desirable that it’s easy to miss the fact that none have intrinsic value. That is clearly true of wealth, but it is equally true of mental and physical health.

先说身体健康。我们常把它说得仿佛是最重要的事。正因如此，奥古斯丁·巴勒斯（Augusten Burroughs）那句「当你拥有健康，你就拥有了一切」才作为一种网络梗流传甚广。但我们并非为健康本身而看重健康。我们看重它有两个原因。其一，替代方案通常伴随着痛苦与受苦，而这些本身是坏的，应当回避。其二，有了健康，我们更能去做那些赋予生命意义的事。但一个没有爱、没有有意义的活动或经历的健康人生，会是空洞的。事实上，许多慢性病患者会让自己和旁人都吃惊地发现，原来健康并不是他们生命中最重要的事。

Take bodily health first. We often talk about it as if it were the most important thing of all. That’s why Augusten Burroughs’s quote “When you have your health, you have everything” has a healthy life as an internet meme. But we don’t prize health for its own sake. We value it for two reasons. One is that the alternative usually involves pain and suffering, which are bad in themselves and so to be avoided. The other is that, with health, we are more able to do the things that bring meaning to our lives. But a healthy life devoid of love, meaningful activity or experiences would be empty. Indeed, many people with chronic illness surprise themselves and others when they discover that, actually, their health is not the most important thing after all.

连心理健康本身也并不重要。心理疾病是内在地坏的，因为它是无所得的受苦。但良好的心理状态，正如良好的身体状态，只是更根本价值之物的一种赋能者。甚至某些心理苦痛也并非内在地坏。我们为之哀伤是件好事，因为这表明当我们在乎之人遭遇不幸时，我们的情感能正确地运作。

Even mental health is not important in itself. Mental illness is intrinsically bad, since it is suffering without gain. But being in good mental health, like being in good physical health, is just an enabler of what is more fundamentally valuable. Even some mental distress is not intrinsically bad. It is a good thing that we grieve, as it shows that our emotions are functioning correctly when bad things happen to the people who matter to us.

即便幸福——或许是工具化最常被宣称的好处——也不是内在之善。如果一个人因看到他所偏执仇恨之人受苦而感到快乐，这并非好事。在一片化学的极乐之云中度过一生、心满意足却与现实世界脱节，也并非好事。当你的伴侣在背后不忠，你却在「关系牢固」的幻觉中快活度日，这也并非好事。盲目的安之若素有时确比痛苦的知情更好，但这并不让它成为善。

Not even happiness, perhaps the most commonly claimed benefit of instrumentalisation, is an intrinsic good. It is not good if someone feels happy to see the suffering of people they prejudicially hate. It would not be good to live life in a chemical cloud of bliss, content but disengaged from the real world. It is not good to live happily in the illusion of a strong relationship when your partner is cheating behind your back. Blissful ignorance may sometimes be better than painful knowledge, but that does not make it good.

那么，若健康、财富与心理福祉都不是，究竟什么本身即是好的？哲学家们一再犯这样的错：试图把某一样东西指认为人类的「至善」（summum bonum）、「终极之善」。对亚里士多德而言，是理智的静观；对佛教徒而言，是消除苦难；对康德而言，是善良意志；对功利主义者而言，是幸福。但似乎没有理由非要把内在之有价值者收窄为某一种状态或活动。亚里士多德更切中肯綮之处，在于他把「繁荣」（flourishing）指认为人类最高之善；他之失误，只在于对繁荣所要求的内容过于武断。当我们的人生投身于那些因其自身、而非任何他者而有价值的事物时，我们便在繁荣。

So what is good in itself, if not health, wealth and mental wellbeing? Philosophers have repeatedly made the mistake of trying to identify one thing as the summum bonum, the “ultimate good” for humankind. For Aristotle, it was intellectual contemplation; for the Buddhists, elimination of suffering; for Kant, a good will; for utilitarians, happiness. But there seems no reason to try to narrow down what is intrinsically valuable to one state or activity. Aristotle was more on point when he identified flourishing as the highest good for humanity, erring only when he became too prescriptive about what flourishing demands. We flourish when our lives are ones of engagement with things that are valuable for their own sakes and not for any other.

塞西莉·布朗（Cecily Brown）的《A Round Robin》，展于伦敦蛇形画廊南馆（Serpentine South gallery）。摄影：Wiktor Szymanowicz/Future Publishing/Getty Images内在的人类之善，包括所有那些使生命值得一过、而无需任何进一步辩护的事物。要问它们：「有什么用？」本身就是没抓住要害。它们就是要害所在。我们无法给出论证证明它们为何有价值；我们只能描述是什么让它们有价值，并期望他人认同其可贵。例如，我们可以说，在森林中度过的一天，应当首先且最主要地因其让我们体认到生而为人的惊奇、并惊叹于自然世界而被珍视。参与或观看一项运动，是去参与或见证那种比生活其余时刻更精妙地把心灵与身体合而为一的拼搏与喜悦。学习一门外语，是通往另一种文化的入口，让你能与该文化中的人交流、触及它的文学与媒介。所有这些都丰富我们的生命、拓宽我们的经验，这本身就是有价值的，哪怕它不能为你的寿命多添一秒，也不能让痴呆迟来一天。如果你把它们看作未来时刻提升心智、情感或体能的手段，你就是把注意力从此时此地有价值之物上移开了。生命不是为未来的训练。它是一场已经开始的比赛，而时间正在流逝。

Cecily Brown’s A Round Robin at the Serpentine South gallery in London. Photograph: Wiktor Szymanowicz/Future Publishing/Getty ImagesIntrinsic human goods include all the things that make life worth living without need of any further justification. To ask of them: “What’s the point?” would be to miss the point. They are the point. We cannot give arguments for why they are valuable; we can only describe what makes them valuable and hope others recognise their worth. For example, we can say that a day spent in the forest should be appreciated first and foremost because it makes us recognise the wonder of being alive and marvel at the natural world. To play or watch a sport is to participate in or witness the struggle and delight of attempting to bring mind and body together more seamlessly than in the rest of life. Learning a foreign language is a gateway into another culture that allows you to communicate with members of it and access its literature and media. All these things enrich our lives and broaden our experience, which is valuable even if it doesn’t add a second to your lifespan or delay dementia by a day. If you see them as a means to boost your mental, emotional or physical strength for future times, you are taking your focus away from what is valuable here and now. Life isn’t a training for the future. It’s a game that’s already started, and time is running out.

内在之善与外在之善的区分，在概念上是清晰的，但在现实世界中很快就没那么泾渭分明了。工具化并不创造外在价值，它只是把外在价值抬到高于内在之价值的位置。

The distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic goods may be conceptually sharp, but in the real world it quickly becomes less clearcut. Instrumentalisation does not create extrinsic value, it merely elevates it above what is intrinsically valuable.

沉浸于一个好故事：从政治到心理，从饮食到科技，从文化到罪案

Lose yourself in a great story: from politics to psychology, food to technology, culture to crime

人有现实的需要，挣钱或劈柴有时比读一本小说或陪孙辈玩耍更重要。许多事不得不为工具性目的而做，若只投身于那些内在即有价值之事，会是一种非凡的特权、一种放纵，或兼而有之。

Human beings have practical needs, and it can be more important to earn money or chop wood than to read a novel or play with your grandchildren. Many things have to be done for instrumental purposes, and to occupy yourself only with what is intrinsically worthwhile would be an exceptional privilege, an indulgence, or both.

围绕「为艺术而艺术」与「艺术作为教化工具」之争，有时未免有些偏题。某些艺术，尤其是器乐和抽象绘画，只能因其自身而被欣赏。但许多文学、电影与戏剧能让我们洞察伦理、政治与人心。所有这类理解都帮助我们活得更好、更关注我们自身及他人生命中真正要紧之事。这样的艺术可被看作通往道德教化这一目的的手段，但在好的艺术中，手段与目的结合得如此紧密，以至两者的区分显得人为而造作。比如，任何对契诃夫（Anton Chekhov）何以成为伟大剧作家的论述，都无法把他高超的舞台技艺与它所呈现的人性分离开来。许多说教艺术的问题，不在于它含有教益，而在于传达得太拙劣。这样的作品不仅是坏艺术，也是蹩脚的教化工具。

The debate over “art for art’s sake” versus “art as a didactic tool” can be somewhat misguided. Some art, especially instrumental music and abstract painting, can be appreciated only for its own sake. But much literature, film and drama can give us insight into ethics, politics and the human heart. All such understanding helps us to live better and attend more to what truly matters, in our lives and those of others. Such art can be seen as a means to the end of moral education, but in good art, means and ends are so closely tied that the distinction seems artificial. For instance, any account of why Anton Chekhov was such a great playwright could not separate his stagecraft and the humanity of what it represents. The problem with much didactic art is not that it contains lessons but that they are conveyed so crudely. Such works are not just bad art but poor pedagogical tools.

内在价值与外在价值的关系是复杂的，而工具化的问题之一，恰恰在于它试图把这种关系压平、简化。它怂恿我们去识别最有用之物，然后把它从具有终极价值之物中分离出来，并置于其上。如此行事，它常常削弱乃至毁掉它本欲最大化的那些好处。

The relationship between intrinsic and extrinsic value is complex, and one of the problems of instrumentalisation is that it seeks to flatten and simplify it. It encourages us to identify what is most useful, and then separate it from, and prioritise it above, what is of ultimate value. In doing so, it often diminishes or destroys the very benefits it promises to maximise.

拿社会联结来说。我刚刚听说一项研究说，做任何事——哪怕是阅读——与他人一起做都比独自做对我们更有益。这一讯息如今被广为传播、广为人知，于是人们知道「共处之道」对身心健康很重要。但友谊与社群最有价值的特征之一，恰恰在于它让我们走出对自身的关切，让我们更意识到他人的需要。要从社交中获益最多，我们需要以正确的精神去做——选择与他人相处，是因为我们彼此在意、因为我们觉得他们令人振奋、因为我们享受成为某种共同经验或事业的一部分。所以，如果我们仅以个人福祉为由去交际，多半就得不到社交通常带来的那些益处。

Take social connection. I have just heard of a study that says that doing anything – even reading – is better for us when we do it with others than alone. This message is now widely broadcast and understood, so people know that conviviality is important for their mental and physical health. But one of the most valuable features of friendship and community is how they take us out of concern for ourselves and make us more aware of the needs of others. To get the most out of socialising we need to do it in the right spirit, choosing to be with other people because we care for them and they for us, because we find them stimulating, because we enjoy being part of a collective experience or endeavour. So if we choose to mingle only for reasons of our personal wellbeing, we are probably not going to get the benefits that socialising usually brings.

工具化给出了一种效率的幻觉，因为它鼓动我们直接去追求人人都想要的那些实际之物。但这往往适得其反。在多数情况下，如果获取某项活动所宣称的好处成了你的首要动机，你反倒会得不到这些好处。看似捷径，实为短路，反噬它本欲达成的目标。

Instrumentalisation offers the illusion of efficiency because it promotes the direct pursuit of practical things that we all want. But often this turns out to be counterproductive. More often than not, you will fail to get the claimed benefits of an activity if getting them becomes your primary motivation. What look like shortcuts turn out to be short circuits, undermining what they seek to achieve.

如果工具化是个如此深重的错误，我们为何还会犯下它？毕竟，我们并非有意要从最珍视的活动中褫夺意义。

If instrumentalisation is such a profound mistake, why have we made it? After all, we do not deliberately set out to strip meaning from our most valued activities.

工具化的根，扎在西方现代性的若干相互关联的特征里。启蒙运动（The Enlightenment）使一种「拥有主权的、自主的个人优先」的观念走向成熟，而这种观念在古典与基督教思想中早有深根。几个世纪下来，这种观念已成某种常识。每个人都被设想为自己命运的主宰、自身人生故事的作者。自我表达与自我决定被视为成为本真之自我的要件。

Instrumentalisation has its roots in several connected features of western modernity. The Enlightenment brought to fruition an idea of the primacy of the sovereign, autonomous individual, one that had deep roots in classical and Christian thought. Over the centuries, this idea has become a kind of common sense. Each person is supposed to be the master of their own destiny, the author of their own life story. Self-expression and self-determination are seen as essential for being an authentic self.

在一个权力由少数人凌驾于被压制的多数人之上的时代，启蒙思想家提倡更大的个人自由，他们是对的。但人也是社会性动物，永远无法完全自主。现代性之失，正在于对此视而不见，把全部重点放在个人自由上，而对我们的相互依赖着墨不够。这导致对自主之重要性的夸大，把对个性的推崇推得过远。其结果是原子化：一个我们与他人的分离已变得过分的世界。

Enlightenment thinkers were correct to promote greater individual freedom in an age when power was wielded by the few over a subjugated majority. But human beings are also social animals and can never be entirely autonomous. Modernity’s mistake is to lose sight of this, placing all the emphasis on personal liberty and not enough on our interdependence. This has led to an exaggeration of the importance of autonomy that has pushed the prizing of individuality too far. The result is atomisation: a world in which our separateness from others has become excessive.

这个原子化的世界有若干特征，全都助长工具化。其一，它催生一种掌控的幻觉。被鼓励着感到自主，我们便看不见有太多事物我们根本无能为力。世界自行展开，机遇与绊脚石以同样随机的频率出现。我们甚至对自己也无法完全掌控。我们对自己最根本的构成——性情、人格、禀赋与局限——都无从置喙。

This atomised world has several features, all of which encourage instrumentalisation. First, it promotes an illusion of control. Encouraged to feel autonomous, we lose sight of the fact that there is much over which we have no power. The world unfolds, opening up opportunities and throwing spanners in the works in equally random measure. We are not even in full control of ourselves. We had no say in our fundamental constitutions: our dispositions, personalities, gifts and limitations.

但被预设成「自由而自主」地去想自己，我们便想象能够操纵世界、达成所愿。幸福、健康与成功都唾手可得，只要我们做出正确的选择。于是世界变成一连串待拉的操纵杆、待按的按钮，一切都向我们的意志屈服。

But primed to think of ourselves as free and autonomous, we imagine that we can manipulate the world to achieve whatever we want. Happiness, health and success are all ours for the taking, just as long as we make the right choices. And so the world becomes a series of levers to be pulled and buttons to be pushed, all to yield to our wills.

我们自主的行动力，越来越多地通过我们作为消费者的身份来表达。自由首先成了「如何花钱」的选择，并附带着一项承诺：我们需要的一切都能以现金换得。消费者心态已影响我们与一切事物——不仅是所购之物——的关系。结果是，世界在本质上变成了交易性的，意味着一切都成了用来获取他物的工具。约会软件给人「我们在选购伴侣」的印象，绝非偶然，因为我们连人际关系也带着消费者的框架去接近。高等教育不再主要是拓展心智的机会，而成了对未来收入的投资，凡不能承诺良好财务回报的课程都被视为不划算。

Our autonomous agency has increasingly been expressed through our status as consumers. Freedom is above all the choice of how to spend our money, with the promise that everything we need can be obtained in exchange for cash. The consumer mindset has affected how we relate to everything, not just the things we buy. The result is that the world has become essentially transactional, meaning that everything is an instrument for getting something else. It is no coincidence that dating apps give the impression that we are shopping for partners because we approach even relationships with the consumer framing. Higher education is no longer primarily an opportunity to expand the mind but an investment on future earnings, with courses that do not promise a good financial return considered poor value.

工具化的另一深层文化根源，是那种从自然科学悄然渗入我们文化的还原论（reductionism）。还原论主张，理解事物如何运作的方法，是把它们拆解为构成性的部件。几个世纪以来，这个观念对自然科学大有裨益。但它局限性的线索，来自它在社会科学中的相对失败。经济、社会与心理无法用简单的机械过程来解释。我们已经懂得，即便在自然科学中，靠拆解事物所能解释的也有限，看清系统如何作为整体运作，同样——有时更加——重要。

Another deep cultural source of instrumentalisation is the reductionism that has surreptitiously seeped into our culture from natural science. Reductionism is the idea that the way to understand how things work is to break them down into their constitutive parts. It’s an idea that served natural science well for centuries. But a clue as to its limitations comes in its relative failure in the social sciences. Economies, societies and psychologies cannot be explained by simple mechanistic processes. We have learned that, even in the natural sciences, you can explain only so much by taking things apart, and that it is equally – sometimes more – important to see how systems work as a whole.

许多工具化的背后，是一种粗陋的还原论：它无视系统，只盯住系统内的元素。一段体验的丰厚——比如置身户外——被还原为促进血流或释放激素的手段。艺术本激荡起大量往往相互冲突的情感，却被仅因其唤起某些「好」情感的能力而看重。社会纽带本会带来痛苦与心碎，也同样带来欢愉，却被还原为情感支持的来源。

Behind much instrumentalisation is a crude reductionism that ignores systems and focuses on elements within them. The richness of an experience, such as being in the outdoors, is reduced to a means to stimulate blood flow or release hormones. Art, which stirs a large variety of often conflicting emotions, is prized purely for its capacity to evoke certain good ones. Social bonds, which cause pain and heartache as well as joy, are reduced to sources of emotional support.

把对个人自主的膨胀信念、交易性的消费者心态，与对事物如何运作的还原论态度合在一处，我们便不可避免地把世界当作一堆可供掠夺、以增进自身福祉的资源。悲剧在于，当我们如此行事，我们忽视而非满足了最深层的需要。

Combine an inflated belief in personal autonomy, a transactional consumer mentality and a reductionist attitude to how things work, and it is inevitable that we treat the world as a collection of resources we can plunder to promote our own wellbeing. The tragedy is that when we do so, we neglect rather than serve our deepest needs.

若要逆转万物的工具化，我们的文化会是什么样子？当然，我们仍会把许多事作为手段以达目的。我们也会乐于承认，生活中许多美好之物同样带来工具性好处。但我们会把这些看作可喜的副作用，而非它们的目的。一个去工具化的世界，将是这样一个世界：在其中，我们更专注于此时此地便有价值之物。

What would our culture look like if we were to reverse the instrumentalisation of everything? Of course, we would still do many things as means to ends. We would also be happy to agree that many of the good things in life bring us instrumental benefits too. But we would see these as welcome side-effects, not their purposes. A deinstrumentalised world would be one in which we would attend more to what is of value right here, right now.

拿友谊来说。我们从他人那里得到的个人益处是真实的，但它们不该是我们与之相伴的理由。关系之所以有价值，是因为我们看重这些人，而不是因为与他们共处的时光会在我们脑中释放内啡肽。休谟（David Hume）两个多世纪前就纠正过这一错误，他写道：「我因爱我的朋友而在为他行善中感到快乐；但我并非为了那份快乐而爱他。」

Take friendship. The personal benefits we get from others are real, but they should not be the reason for being with them. Relationships are valuable because we value the people, not because spending time with them releases endorphins in our brains. David Hume corrected this error more than two centuries ago when he wrote: “I feel a pleasure in doing good to my friend, because I love him; but do not love him for the sake of that pleasure.”

依事物自身的价值、而非它可能带给我们什么去欣赏事物，是令人解放的。它把我们从那种内心压力中释放出来——那种总得确保我们所做之事服务于某个更进一步目的的压力。充分地生活，意味着充分地体味生命所给予的，而非试图从中榨取可兑现的好处。它让我们得以认识到，好的生活是我们每天都能过的，既在小事、也在大事之中。最重要的是，它告诉我们：我们所爱与所珍惜的人与事，其本身即已足够，无需再服务于任何其他功能，才能证明为之倾注时间与关怀的正当。置身这世间，认取生命即是其自身的目的，乃是达致其丰盈的钥匙。

To appreciate things for their own value instead of what they might bring us is liberating. It frees us from the internal pressure always to make sure that what we are doing serves some further purpose. Living life to the full means fully appreciating what life brings, not trying to extract bankable benefits from it. It leaves us able to recognise that the good life is something we can live every day, in small ways as well as big. Most importantly, it tells us that the things and people we love are enough in and of themselves and don’t need to serve any further function to justify devoting time and care to them. To be in this world realising that life is its own end is the key to attaining its fullness.
