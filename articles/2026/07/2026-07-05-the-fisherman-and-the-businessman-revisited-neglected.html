渔夫与商人再考：一则流行寓言中被忽视的历史脉络与阐释

Benjamin George Coles·3:AM Magazine·2026年7月5日·16分钟阅读·原文

在2010年的一篇英文博客中，巴西小说家保罗·科埃略（Paulo Coelho）讲述了下面这则极短的故事，题作「渔夫与商人」（The Fisherman and the Businessman），仅附言：「经典的巴西故事，可能也见于其他文化。有人找到了英文版本，但我无法确认译者是谁。」

In a 2010 English-language blog post, the Brazilian novelist Paulo Coelho told the following very short story, giving it the title ‘The Fisherman and the Businessman’ and commenting only: ‘classic Brazilian story, probably also present in other cultures. Someone found the English version, but I could not identify the translator’.

曾经有一位商人坐在巴西一座小村庄的海边。他坐着的时候，看见一个渔夫划着小船靠岸，船里装着相当多的大鱼。商人很是赞叹，问渔夫：「你花多长时间能捕到这么多鱼？」渔夫答道：「哦，就一小会儿。」「那你为什么不在海上多待一会儿，捕更多的鱼呢？」商人十分诧异。「这些足够养活我全家了。」渔夫说。商人又问：「那么，你一天剩下的时间都做什么？」渔夫答道：「嗯，我通常一早起来，出海捕几条鱼，然后回去陪孩子们玩。下午，我和妻子一起小睡，到了傍晚，我就和村里的伙伴们一起喝酒——我们弹吉他、唱歌、跳舞，闹个通宵。」

There was once a businessman who was sitting by the beach in a small Brazilian village. As he sat, he saw a fisherman rowing a small boat towards the shore having caught quite a few big fish. The businessman was impressed and asked the fisherman, “How long does it take you to catch so many fish?” The fisherman replied, “Oh, just a short while.” “Then why don’t you stay longer at sea and catch even more?” The businessman was astonished. “This is enough to feed my whole family,” the fisherman said. The businessman then asked, “So, what do you do for the rest of the day?” The fisherman replied, “Well, I usually wake up early in the morning, go out to sea and catch a few fish, then go back and play with my kids. In the afternoon, I take a nap with my wife, and [when] evening comes, I join my buddies in the village for a drink — we play guitar, sing and dance throughout the night.”

商人向渔夫提了个建议。「我是企业管理学的博士。我可以帮你成为一个更成功的人。从现在起，你应该在海上花更多时间，尽可能多捕鱼。等你攒够了钱，就可以买一艘更大的船，捕更多的鱼。不久你就能买得起更多船只，成立自己的公司、自己的罐头生产厂和分销网络。到那时，你就会搬出这个村子，去圣保罗（Sao Paulo），在那里设立总部，管理你的其他分部。」渔夫接着问：「那之后呢？」商人哈哈大笑：「那之后，你可以在自家宅子里过上国王般的日子，等时机成熟，你可以上市，把股份放到证券交易所，你就会变得富有。」渔夫问：「那之后呢？」商人说：「那之后，你终于可以退休了，你可以搬到渔村旁的一栋房子里，一早起来，捕几条鱼，然后回家陪孩子们玩，和妻子舒舒服服地午睡，到了傍晚，你可以和伙伴们一起喝酒，弹吉他、唱歌、跳舞闹个通宵！」渔夫一脸困惑。「这不正是我现在过的日子吗？」

The businessman offered a suggestion to the fisherman. “I am a PhD in business management. I could help you to become a more successful person. From now on, you should spend more time at sea and try to catch as many fish as possible. When you have saved enough money, you could buy a bigger boat and catch even more fish. Soon you will be able to afford to buy more boats, set up your own company, your own production plant for canned food and distribution network. By then, you will have moved out of this village and to Sao Paulo, where you can set up HQ to manage your other branches.” The fisherman continues, “And after that?” The businessman laughs heartily, “After that, you can live like a king in your own house, and when the time is right, you can go public and float your shares on the Stock Exchange, and you will be rich.” The fisherman asks, “And after that?” The businessman says, “After that, you can finally retire, you can move to a house by the fishing village, wake up early in the morning, catch a few fish, then return home to play with [your] kids, have a nice afternoon nap with your wife, and when evening comes, you can join your buddies for a drink, play the guitar, sing and dance throughout the night!” The fisherman was puzzled. “Isn’t that what I am doing now?”

行为科学家保罗·多兰（Paul Dolan）援引科埃略的博客，在他2014年的著作《幸福的设计：在日常生活中寻找快乐与意义》（Happiness by Design: Finding Pleasure and Purpose in Everyday Life）中收录了这则故事。几年前读这本书时，我才第一次遇见这则故事。你大概也遇见过它——不过，我猜，是在不同的语境里，而且其中一两个细节略有出入。

The behavioural scientist Paul Dolan, citing Coelho’s blog, reproduces this story in his 2014 book Happiness by Design: Finding Pleasure and Purpose in Everyday Life. It was reading this book some years ago that I first encountered the story. You’ve probably encountered it too – though, I would guess, in a different context and with one or two of its details slightly different.

我第二次遇见这则故事，大约是在一年半以前，是在一段YouTube视频里——演员多米尼克·龙沃（Dominique Rongvaux）用法语独白，地点和渔夫被设定为墨西哥人，而商人则被设定为美国人。视频的标题其实是「美国人与墨西哥人」（L'Américain et le Mexicain），而龙沃——或许是在表达自己的同情所向——戴着一顶宽边草帽（sombrero）。稍作查考便知，这段视频是从龙沃2011年录制的单人秀《懒散颂》（Éloge de l'oisiveté，英译：In praise of idleness）中剪出的片段。出于探幽的好奇，我看了整场演出，幸好YouTube上也有。在演出中，龙沃对渔夫与商人故事的来历只字未提，但他却反复地、往往并不事先声明地大段引用哲学家伯特兰·罗素（Bertrand Russell）1932年的一篇文章，演出的标题也正是那篇文章的标题。于是我读了那篇文章。我得说，那是罗素最为迷人也最具挑衅色彩的笔触——他呼吁实行普遍的四小时工作制，并力挺一种大体如故事中渔夫所描述的那样的生活——尽管我感觉得到，他更乐于见到人们在由此腾出的时间里多做一些推动文明的思考，而且他确实指出，通常需要教育才能知道如何最好地利用闲暇。无论如何，尽管主题上如此贴近，他在那篇文章里并未讲述、也未曾提及渔夫与商人的故事。

The second time I encountered the story, about a year and a half ago now, it was in a YouTube video of a monologue in French by the actor Dominique Rongvaux, and the location and the fisherman were identified as Mexican, while the businessman was identified as American. Indeed the video was titled ‘L’Américain et le Mexicain’, and Rongvaux – perhaps expressing where his sympathies lay – was wearing a sombrero. A quick bit of research revealed that this video was a clip from Rongvaux’s one-man show Éloge de l’oisiveté (tr: In praise of idleness), recorded in 2011. In an exploratory mood, I watched the full show, helpfully also available on YouTube. In it, Rongvaux does not say anything about the origins of the fisherman and the businessman story, but he does, repeatedly, and often without announcing he’s doing so, quote at length from a 1932 essay by the philosopher Bertrand Russell, with the show’s title also the title of that essay. So I read the essay. And I’d say it finds Russell at his charming and provocative best, as he calls for a universal 4-hour working day and champions a life generally much like the one the fisherman in the story describes living – although I get the sense that he’d be happier to see a bit more civilisation-advancing thinking going on in the freed-up time, and he does suggest that education is normally necessary to know how to make best use of one’s leisure. Anyway, despite the thematic closeness, he does not, in the essay, tell or refer to the fisherman and the businessman story.

到这一步，我当然也试过简单地在网上搜一下，结果找到了数不清的版本——其中很多出现在养生、旅行或语言学习的博客里。细枝末节从一个版本到另一个版本仍在不断出入。在一个版本里，人物和背景全是意大利人；在另一个里全是法国人；还有一个则未指明任何国籍。在那些设定为巴西人的版本里，有时会把科埃略列为作者，尽管如前所述，他本人的说法似乎排除了他的作者身份。在大多数情况下，根本没有任何出处标注。它基本上就像一则现代民间故事。

By this point, I’d of course also tried a simple web search for it, and found endless versions – a lot of them in wellness or travel or language-learning blogs. Small details continued to differ from one version to another. The characters and setting were, in one case, all Italian, in another all French, and in another had no specified nationality. In cases where they were Brazilian, Coelho was sometimes cited as the author, though his own comments, just mentioned, seem to rule out his authorship. In most cases, there was no citation at all. It seemed a modern folk tale, basically.

我又做了一些查考，发现前哈佛商学院教授马克·阿尔比恩（Mark Albion）声称这则故事是他写的——他给它的标题是「好生活：一则寓言」（The Good Life: A Parable），他最早流传的几个版本并未提及任何国籍，但后来他确实把那座滨海村落和渔夫设定为墨西哥人，把商人设定为美国人。在他2008年的著作《不止于金钱：每个MBA都需要回答的问题》（More Than Money: Questions Every MBA Needs to Answer）中，阿尔比恩甚至解释了自己在90年代是怎样想出这则故事的：他度了一周假回来，那几天大多和一位无忧无虑的潜水教练在一座几乎无人居住的小岛上度过（我在别处找到他把这座岛指认为特克斯和凯科斯群岛〔Turks and Caicos Islands〕中的索尔特礁〔Salt Cay〕）。然而在一条脚注里，他承认：「后来我见过这则寓言的诸多形态，我的月度电子通讯读者也曾提醒我注意，所以很显然，别人也写过相似的故事。我所发现的最早一篇是『Anekdote zur Senkung der Arbeitsmoral』（降低工作伦理的轶事，Anecdote to Reduce the Work Ethic），写于1963年，作者是德国诺贝尔奖得主海因里希·伯尔（Heinrich Böll），几乎如出一辙，但用法不同。一个相似的故事似乎也见于一则佛教故事和俄罗斯民间传说。」

I’ve now done a bit more research and discovered that former Harvard Business School professor Mark Albion claims to have written the story – his title for it is ‘The Good Life: A Parable’, and his earliest circulated versions of it do not mention any nationalities, but he did later make his coastal village and fisherman Mexican and his businessman American. In his 2008 book More Than Money: Questions Every MBA Needs to Answer, Albion even explains how he came up with the story in the 90s after returning from a week’s vacation spent mostly in the company of a carefree scuba-diving instructor on a barely populated island (which I’ve found him elsewhere identifying as Salt Cay, in the Turks and Caicos Islands). In a footnote, however, he acknowledges: ‘I’ve since seen this parable in many forms to which my monthly e-newsletter readers have directed my attention, so it is clear that others have written similar stories. The earliest I have found is “Anekdote zur Senkung der Arbeitsmoral” (“Anecdote to Reduce the Work Ethic”), written in 1963 by the German Nobel Prize laureate Heinrich Böll, nearly identical but used differently. A similar tale apparently also appears in a Buddhist story and in Russian folklore.’

伯尔的版本，一旦知道该去找它，便很容易找到，故事发生在一处欧洲西海岸的港口，出场者并非严格意义上的商人，而只是一位衣着考究、咔嚓按快门的游客，他费了点力气（也许）才改用当地语言与衣着朴素的渔夫交谈，因为他不小心把对方从码头边的午睡中吵醒了。当然，在那些墨西哥—美国版里，想必也得有人切换语言，并应对由此带来的种种困难——而我猜想，从历史上看这更可能是商人，而如今同样也可能是渔夫。无论如何，伯尔那篇幅略长一些的版本（三页，而非三段）在我看来在比例上不那么喜剧、也不那么尖锐——尽管，呃，依然喜剧、依然尖锐。然而，它明显是同一个核心故事，相似到我不禁想，阿尔比恩是否在童年读过它，然后忘得一干二净。

The Böll version, easily found when you know to look for it, takes place in a harbour on the western coast of Europe, and features, instead of a businessman as such, merely a well-dressed, camera-clicking tourist, who, with some effort perhaps, switches to the local language to converse with the modestly attired fisherman, having accidently awoken this fellow from his quayside slumbers. Of course someone presumably has to switch language, and deal with any difficulties arising from that switch, in the Mexican-American versions too – and I guess that, while historically that would have more likely been the businessman, these days it would just as likely be the fisherman. Anyway, Böll’s slightly more detailed rendering (three pages, rather than three paragraphs) strikes me as, in proportion, less comic and less stark – though, yeah, still comic and still stark. It is, however, noticeably the same essential story, so much so that I wonder whether Albion for instance read it in his childhood and then forgot about it.

在查阅伯尔版本的过程中——它原来是德国文学中的一则经典，广泛出现在母语（L1）和二语（L2）课程的阅读书目上——我反复见到一种说法：它很可能受到一则对话故事的启发，即以刚烈著称的希腊将军、一度称王的皮洛士（Pyrrhus）与以睿智和雄辩著称的谋臣西尼亚斯（Cineas）之间的对话，见于普鲁塔克（Plutarch）的《希腊罗马名人传》（Parallel Lives）中「皮洛士传」一节，该节多半写于公元2世纪初。这里的结构和要旨同样十分眼熟，只是话题不再是捕鱼，而是军事征服：西尼亚斯追问其主公，若他如愿征服罗马人，将如何利用这场胜利，皮洛士答自然是要进而征服整个意大利，西尼亚斯又问他将如何利用那场胜利，皮洛士答他将进而攻取迦太基（Carthage）和利比亚（Libya），对话如此这般地推进，直到皮洛士承认，待彻底压服所有对手之后，「我们便可安享清闲，我的好人，我们每天痛饮满杯，彼此以知心之谈相欢」，而西尼亚斯随即反问：那为何不一开始就这么做呢。

Reading up on the Böll version – as it turns out, a classic of German literature, widely featured on the syllabi of both L1 and L2 courses – I found the recurring observation that it was probably inspired by the story of a conversation between the famously fierce Greek army general and sometime king Pyrrhus and his famously wise and oratorically gifted advisor Cineas in the ‘Life of Pyrrhus’ section of Plutarch’s Parallel Lives, most likely written early in the 2nd century AD. Here the structure and gist are again very familiar, but, instead of fishing, the conversation is of military conquest, with Cineas quizzing his master on how, if he succeeds in his plan of conquering the Romans, he will make use of this victory, and Pyrrhus answering that he will of course then conquer all of Italy, and Cineas then asking how he’ll use that victory, and Pyrrhus answering that he’ll then go for Carthage and Libya, and the conversation continuing in this vein until Pyrrhus grants that, upon securing complete domination over all adversaries, ‘We shall be much at ease, and we’ll drink bumpers, my good man, every day, and we’ll gladden one another’s hearts with confidential talks’, only for Cineas then to ask why they don’t just do that in the first place.

所以，总而言之，科埃略认为那则「经典的巴西故事」「也见于其他文化」，无疑是对的。我想起阿黛拉·佩耶娃（Adela Peeva）的纪录片《这是谁的歌？》（Whose Is This Song?），它追溯了一首流行巴尔干民歌的源流，以及该地区几乎每个国家的普通人和音乐学家对它的作者身份与归属权所作的情感化的声称。我很想知道更多关于渔夫与商人这则故事谱系的事，我也确信这一谱系还有远未穷尽的余地可挖，尤其是如果我们把它扩展到阿尔比恩提到（却未给出出处）的那则佛教和俄罗斯变身，以及其他在人物与主题上明显相似的经典民间故事，比如拉封丹（La Fontaine）以「鞋匠与金融家」（The Cobbler and the Financier）为题所讲述的那一则，以及格林兄弟（Brothers Grimm）以「渔夫和他的妻子」（The Fisherman and His Wife）为题所讲述的那一则。

So, in summary, Coelho was certainly right to think that that ‘classic Brazilian story’ was ‘also present in other cultures’. I think of Adela Peeva’s documentary Whose Is This Song?, exploring the history of one popular Balkan folk song and the emotional claims of authorship and ownership of it made by laypeople and musicologists alike in just about every country in the region. I would be fascinated to know more about the genealogy of the story of the fisherman and the businessman, and I’m sure there is far, far more of that genealogy to be uncovered, especially if we expand it to include those Buddhist and Russian incarnations that Albion alludes to (but does not provide citations for), and other classic folk tales with noticeably similar characters and themes, like the one La Fontaine tells as ‘The Cobbler and the Financier’ and the one the Brothers Grimm tell as ‘The Fisherman and His Wife’.

然而我更感兴趣的，是我所接触到的种种对这则故事的回应。多兰——正是他第一次把这则故事引荐给我——把它描述为「凸显了我们无止境的成就驱动中的种种悖论」，并进一步评论道：「渔夫被指望去追求的许多东西，他现在就已拥有。这则故事的后果，事实上可能比循环往复更糟，因为渔夫在向上、向外攀爬的路上会失去朋友。他也可能对自己的身份生出疑虑。这正是许多出身贫寒的奖学金孩子不如同等高成就的富家子弟快乐的原因之一。」

Of even greater interest to me though are the responses to the story that I’ve encountered. Dolan, who I can credit with first introducing me to it, describes it as ‘highlighting the paradoxes of our relentless drive for achievement’ and further comments: ‘Much of what the fisherman is meant to aspire to he has now. The consequences of this tale could, in fact, turn out to be worse than circular, as the fisherman loses friends on the way up and out. He could also develop doubts about his identity. This is one reason why many of the scholarship kids from poor backgrounds are not as happy as their equally high-achieving peers from wealthier backgrounds.’

同样，龙沃似乎也明显地站在渔夫一边。我提到了那顶草帽，但更要紧的是他的演绎——他似乎把商人塑造成笑料，赋予他一种偏执、自欺、居高临下的腔调。另外，别忘了，还有那标题、那罗素式的灵感来源，以及他所收入这则故事的整场演出的总体精神。

Similarly, it appears that Rongvaux sides very much with the fisherman. I mentioned the sombrero, but, far more importantly, there’s his delivery – he seems to play the businessman for laughs, giving him a monomaniacal, deluded, condescending tone. Plus, lest we forget, there’s the title, the Russellian inspiration and the general ethos of the show he features the story in.

就连身为商业导师的阿尔比恩也说，这则故事「讲的是那些往往被视作理所当然的价值——当物质世界及其同伙『贪婪』把你从自我身边拽走时——在多数商学院的情境里被放大了」。他用这则故事来帮助阐发他所谓的「MBA 陷阱」（the MBA trap）这一概念，该陷阱「在你仅以金钱衡量成功时就会出现」。商人在这则故事里充当这一陷阱的某种代言人。阿尔比恩解释道：「以有意义的人生而非金钱为目标，你对安全与风险的界定就会改变：你此时认识到，一个『安全』的职业选择，是你相信它位于你的命运之路上的选择；而一个『有风险的』选择，则是不是如此的选择。」他总结道：「为何不像那位墨西哥渔夫那样，守着好生活呢？为何不把目标定为：在你所属之地，与你所关心之人一道，做有意义之事地工作与生活呢？」

Even Albion, the business guru, says that the story is ‘about values often taken for granted as the material world and its partner “greed” pull you away from yourself, magnified in most business school settings’. He uses the story to help illustrate his concept of ‘the MBA trap’, which ‘occurs when you measure success just by money’. The businessman functions in the story as a kind of mouthpiece for this trap. Albion explains: ‘With a meaningful life as your goal instead of money, your definitions of safe and risky change: you now recognize that a “safe” job choice is one that you believe is on your destiny path, and a “risky” choice is one that is not.’ He concludes: ‘Why not stay with the good life, like the Mexican fisherman? Why not focus on achieving a life of working and living in a place you belong, doing work that matters with people you care about?’

阿尔比恩声称伯尔的版本「用法不同」，在我看来，这必定主要基于伯尔的标题，因为阿尔比恩觉得自己的版本并非针对工作伦理本身。顺便说一句，我不太确定阿尔比恩想引用的是哪个译本，也不确定他所给出的标题译文是他自己的还是某个翻译工具的产物。我想，它比这则故事大概算标准英译本的标题略字面一些——该译本出自莱拉·芬内维茨（Leila Vennewitz）之手，题为「关于降低生产力的轶事」（Anecdote Concerning the Lowering of Productivity）。我估摸阿尔比恩同样会觉得自己的版本并非针对生产力本身。

Albion’s claim that the Böll version of the story is ‘used differently’ must, it seems to me, be based mainly on Böll’s title, with Albion feeling his own version is not about the work ethic as such. Incidentally, I’m not sure which translation of the story Albion means to cite, or whether the translation he gives of the title was his own or that of a translation tool. It is, I think, slightly more literal than the title of what’s probably the standard English translation of the story, which is by Leila Vennewitz: ‘Anecdote Concerning the Lowering of Productivity’. I suppose Albion would equally feel that his version is not about productivity as such.

无论如何，伯尔的版本对那位游客的企业家式提议作出了完全明确的负面评判，可以说从标题起就是如此，尤其体现在其全知叙述者的结语之中。按芬内维茨的译文：「这位由此受到启发的游客若有所思地走开了，因为他也曾一度努力工作，指望有朝一日不再工作；他对衣衫寒酸的渔夫已无半分怜悯，只剩一丝艳羡。」

The Böll version, in any case, is totally explicit in its negative judgement on the tourist’s entrepreneurial proposal, arguably from the title on, and especially in the concluding lines uttered by its omniscient narrator. As the Vennewitz translation has it: ‘the thus enlightened tourist walked pensively away, for at one time he too was working so as someday not to have to work any more, and no trace of pity for the shabbily dressed fisherman remained in him, only a hint of envy.’

而普鲁塔克那则原始文本，则并未如此明确地谴责穷兵黩武的皮洛士，但它让他听起来像是某些心理学家所说的「战斗成瘾」（Combat Addiction）的受害者：「经西尼亚斯这番推论，皮洛士与其说是被说服，不如说是被搅扰；他清清楚楚地看见自己将抛在身后的那种巨大幸福，却无法舍弃自己对所热切渴望之物的盼望。」

The Plutarchian urtext, meanwhile, is not quite so explicitly damning of the war-mongering Pyrrhus, but it leaves him sounding like a victim of what some psychologists have called Combat Addiction: ‘By this reasoning of Cineas Pyrrhus was more troubled than he was converted; he saw plainly what great happiness he was leaving behind him, but was unable to renounce his hopes of what he eagerly desired.’

至于我读到的那无数转载渔夫与商人故事的博客文章，它们也几乎无一例外地表达了对这则故事本质上相同的回应，所基于的也本质上相同的诠释：这个商人是个蠢人，他的愚蠢是我们许多人易犯的那种，也是我们经济/生活方式很大程度上所赖以建立的那种，而于我们有损。

As for the endless blog posts I found reproducing the fisherman and the businessman story, they too, almost invariably, expressed the same essential response to the story, based on the same essential interpretation: this businessman is a fool, and his foolishness is of a kind that many of us are prone to, and that much of our economy / way of life is based on, to our detriment.

我对这种种情绪不止是略表同情。只是，对这则故事的一种全然不同的诠释在我看来同样可信，甚至更可信。我说过，刊载这则故事的博客文章几乎无一例外地表达对它本质上相同的回应。在数小时通读此类博客文章之后，我找到了一个例外。那是内森·巴里（Nathan Barry）所写的一篇，他是ConvertKit——「一家面向创作者的邮件营销公司」——的创始人兼CEO。巴里在博文中说，他为ConvertKit定下了一个目标：年营收达到1亿美元——从当前的2600万美元起算。挺让人赏心悦目的，对吧？这十足就是商人在回应渔夫与商人的寓言。他也是美国人！

I’m more than a little sympathetic to such sentiments. It’s just that a completely different interpretation of this story seems to me as credible, if not more so. I said that blog posts featuring the story almost invariably express the same essential response to it. In several hours of reading through such blog posts, I found one exception. That was by Nathan Barry, the founder and CEO of ConvertKit, ‘an email marketing company for creators’. Barry says in his blog post that he has a goal for ConvertKit of $100 million in annual revenue – up from its current $26 million. Delightful, right? This is very much the businessman responding to the parable of the fisherman and the businessman. He’s American too!

巴里并未评论故事中的商人是否是个蠢人。相反，在承认这则故事能帮助一些人反思自己真正想要什么的同时，他批评了他从中察觉到的一个想法：「问题在于：如果你抄近路直奔终点，你就不会经历真正的转变。」

Barry does not comment on whether the businessman in the story is a fool. Instead, while acknowledging that the story can help some people reflect on what they really want in life, he critiques an idea that he detects in it: ‘here’s the problem: If you shortcut to the destination you won’t undergo real transformation.’

他说他之所以定下那个1亿美元的目标，「是因为我知道它足够大胆，以至于我需要经历一场转变才能实现它。8年前创办ConvertKit的那个23岁的年轻人，还无力打造出一个能触达数百万人的产品、团队和公司。他还无力招募、激励并带领一支团队去攻克必将到来的无数障碍。如今30岁的我也还未到那一步。但我定下这个目标，是因为它召唤我去克服那些真实的挑战，而我相信这些挑战会把我塑造成为一个更好的人。更具体地说，我相信如果我在与我价值观一致的方向上追求这一目标，我就会成为一个更好的父亲、队友、领导者、丈夫和朋友。目标未必非得关乎金钱或商业，那只是我所选择的。」

He says that he decided on his $100 million goal ‘because I know it’s audacious enough that I need to undergo a transformation in order to achieve it. The 23 year old who started ConvertKit 8 years ago isn’t capable of building a product, team, and company to reach millions of people. He isn’t capable of recruiting, inspiring, and leading a team to navigate the countless obstacles that will need to be overcome. Today at 30 years old I’m still not there. But I set the goal because it calls me to overcome real challenges that I believe will shape me to be a better man. More specifically I believe that if I pursue the goal in alignment with my values, I’ll be a better father, teammate, leader, husband, and friend. The goal doesn’t have to be about money or business, that’s just what I chose.’

巴里并未解释，为何在所有可能的目标之中，他偏偏认为1亿美元这一目标尤其适合让他成为一个更好的父亲、队友、领导者、丈夫和朋友。我真诚地很有兴趣听到那个解释。

Barry does not explain why he thought the $100 million goal – of all the possible goals out there – would be particularly suited to making him a better father, teammate, leader, husband and friend. I would be sincerely interested to hear that explanation.

巴里也未触及一个相关的问题：他是否会料想故事中的渔夫是个尤其糟糕的父亲、队友等等，或者至少比商人明显糟糕得多。既然渔夫似乎把自己与家庭和社群的关系描述为一种和谐而满足的状态，那么巴里是不是就得反过来指认这则故事不切实际？或指认渔夫自欺？又或者，假如巴里并不会抱有那种预期，那他又设想渔夫本可如何避免这些弱点呢？

Barry also does not address the related question of whether he’d expect the fisherman in the story to be a particularly bad father, teammate, etc., or at least a significantly worse one than the businessman. As the fisherman seems to describe a state of harmony and contentment in his relations with family and community, would Barry then have to call the story out as unrealistic? Or the fisherman as deluded? Or equally, if Barry wouldn’t have that kind of expectation, how does he imagine the fisherman could have avoided such weaknesses?

我对巴里的不少说法心存怀疑，但当他问「如果过程本身才是要紧的呢？」并补充说「旅程与苦难塑造我们」时，我认同他的要点。我也想到，例如，在一项伟大而费力的工程——或哪怕只是一段辛勤工作——的尽头、并由之挣得的那种轻松与闲适，与毫无此种根基而享有的轻松与闲适，感受起来颇为不同。也许，若我们设想渔夫生活中匮乏费力的、具挑战性的活动，那是对他的不公，但这一点至少并不清楚。

I am doubtful about much of what Barry says, but I take his point when he asks, ‘what if the journey is what matters?’ and when he adds ‘journeys and hardships shape us.’ It also occurs to me that, for instance, ease and restfulness at the end of – and earnt by means of – a great and effortful project, or even just a period of hard work, feel quite different from ease and restfulness enjoyed without any such foundation. Maybe we’d do the fisherman a disservice if we imagined that there’s a dearth of effortful and challenging activity in his life, but that’s at least not clear.

而标准诠释中让我觉得奇怪的，是它们如何理解商人那时的心思——当他向渔夫描绘一番成功企业生涯那辉煌的收尾阶段可能等待着什么时，他几乎一字不差地重复了渔夫方才所说的自己当下的生活。我们是觉得这商人老糊涂了吗？是觉得他患有严重的短期失忆吗？我们难道不会料想，商人若真诚回答，会提到众多经典的、需要一点金钱的令人愉悦或满足之事——比如旅行（他眼下大概正是在做这件事），或美食盛宴，或在需要之处建一所学校或一座剧院？在我看来，更现实的诠释是：商人完全清楚自己在重复渔夫所说，并且他这样做时面带微笑。也就是说，他在开玩笑。但玩笑背后有个严肃的论点。那就是：你为何总是问「那之后呢？那之后呢？」你 surely 知道，有些事物本身就是有价值的。企业经营的历险本身就是一件惊心动魄而又回报丰厚的事！一如你目前倾注毕生去做的那些事，它们本身就是有价值的事物。

What anyway strikes me as odd about standard interpretations of the story is how they understand what is going on in the mind of the businessman when he, describing to the fisherman what might await him in the glorious concluding phase of a life of successful enterprise, repeats, almost word for word, what the fisherman has just said he is currently doing with his life. Are we thinking this businessman is senile? Are we thinking he suffers from severe short-term memory loss? Mightn’t we expect the businessman to, if answering the question sincerely, mention one of the many classic enjoyable or satisfying things to do that requires a bit of money – travelling, say, as he’s presumably doing now, or gourmet dining, or having a school or a theatre built where there is need of one? It seems to me that the more realistic interpretation is that the businessman is fully aware that he’s repeating what the fisherman said, and that he’s doing so with a smile. That is to say, he’s joking. But there is a serious point behind the joke. Which is: why do you always ask ‘and after that? And after that?’ Surely you know that some things are valuable in themselves. The adventure of enterprise is a thrilling and rewarding thing in itself! Much as these things you currently dedicate your life to are things of value in themselves.

在问「那不就是我现在已在做的吗？」时，渔夫于是既没能领会那个玩笑，也没能领会其背后的严肃论点。他一刻也没有想到，企业的历险本身可以是令人愉悦或有所回报的。

In asking ‘Isn’t that what I’m doing already?’, the fisherman is then totally failing to grasp both the joke and the serious point behind it. It doesn’t occur to him for a second that the adventure of enterprise could be enjoyable or rewarding in itself.

按此诠释，这则故事所呈现的并非一位天生睿智的渔夫与一位愚蠢的商人之间的互动，而只是两个在若干重要方面颇为不同之人之间的互动——其中渔夫若要说的话，是两人中较为不自知的那一个。

According to this interpretation, the story is of an interaction not between a natively wise fisherman and a foolish businessman, but just between two people who are, in important ways, quite different – with the fisherman, if anything, the less aware of the two.

诚然，渔夫的生活方式对大多数人而言或许是较为健康的那种。在我看来，要说它对所有人都较为健康，则至少更可商榷。我们或可采取的另一种看法是：渔夫与商人在此代表着一道光谱的两端，而我们大多数人介于其间——也许存在某种这样的多样性也是件好事。如前所言，我同情于这样一种想法：当今我们许多人本可过得更像渔夫一些。我甚至相信，基本的环境与文明可持续性正系于此。另一方面，我远未信服：身边有一些更靠近商人那一端的人是不值得的。我的意思是，即便就故事自身的措辞而言：如果一个人捕鱼多过养家所需，那 surely 意味着社群里的其他人——也许是那些没那么热衷、也没那么擅长捕鱼的人——可以少捕些鱼，因为他们转而可以通过向他购买或与他以物易物来获得鱼，而这反过来很可能意味着他们有了空余时间和额外的动机去发展与练习其他技能——这些技能或许随之造福渔夫乃至整个社群；而如果渔夫当真进一步发展出一门大型而成功的捕鱼生意，那或许会，比如说，在歉收之年对困扰内陆社群的饥饿与营养不良问题发挥重要作用。

Now, it may be that the fisherman’s way of life is the generally healthier one for most people. To my mind, the case that it’s generally healthier for all people is at least a more doubtful one. Another view we might take is that the fisherman and the businessman here represent opposite ends of a spectrum, and most of us are somewhere in between – and maybe the existence of some such diversity is a good thing too. As already indicated, I’m sympathetic to the idea that many of us these days could do with living more like the fisherman. I’m even persuaded that basic environmental and civilisational sustainability are at stake in this. On the other hand, I’m far from convinced that having some people about who are closer to the businessman end of the spectrum is not desirable. I mean, even just in the terms of the story: if one guy fishes more than feeding his family requires him to, that will surely mean others in the community, perhaps less keen and able fishers, can fish less, as they can instead acquire fish by purchasing from or bartering with him, and this in turn might well mean that they have time free and an additional incentive to develop and practise other skills – skills that might then benefit the fisherman and indeed the whole community; and if the fisherman does go on to develop a large, successful fishing business, that might, for instance, play an important role in addressing problems of hunger and malnutrition besetting in-land communities in years of bad harvests.

出于这些理由，我颇感欣慰的是，我所见过的这则故事的所有版本，都能容纳这一较少偏袒的诠释。

For these kinds of reasons, I find it very satisfying that all of the versions of the story I’ve seen can accommodate this less partisan interpretation.

诚然，伯尔版本和普鲁塔克版本乍看之下似乎并非如此。它们全知叙述者的那番评论——前文已引——似乎横亘其间。在普鲁塔克那里，更有皮洛士那行关于其事业终极目标的台词并无西尼亚斯任何类似台词在前作铺垫的问题，更遑论在多数人看来，那项事业比捕鱼要成问题得多。

Granted, the Böll version and the Plutarch version might, at first glance, seem not to. The comments of their omniscient narrators, quoted above, might seem to get in the way. In the Plutarch, there’s even the matter of the Pyrrhus line about the ultimate goal of his enterprise not being preceded by any similar line from Cineas, to say nothing of the matter of that enterprise being, in most people’s reckoning, a far more problematic one than fishing.

先看伯尔一例。游客到末了「受到启发」，不再有怜悯，只剩对渔夫「一丝艳羡」。然而，此处的「启发」并不必然关乎如何最好地度过一生，那艳羡也并不必然基于觉得渔夫过着更好的生活。「游客曾一度努力工作，指望有朝一日不再工作」——这想必是在他真正投入自己的工作、开始至少部分地为工作本身而珍视它之前。我倒认为，游客有点艳羡渔夫——而且据说只是「一点」——艳羡他看似更简单的生活，或至少他与工作之间更简单的关系、他更易达成的工作目标。只要不必为此牺牲太多，游客大概是乐得拥有那份简单的。而我认为，游客到末了之所以「受到启发」，在于他理解到渔夫与他在这点上不同——并非人人都与他一样。

Take the Böll case first. So the tourist is ‘enlightened’ at the end and feels no pity anymore but just ‘a hint of envy’ towards the fisherman. This enlightenment, however, does not necessarily concern how best to live one’s life, and the envy is not necessarily based on the feeling that the fisherman is living a better one. ‘At one time [the tourist] too was working so as someday not to have to work any more’ – this, presumably, was before he got properly into his work, started valuing it at least partly for its own sake. I would think the tourist envies the fisherman a little – and only, it is said, a little – for his apparently simpler life, or at least his simpler relationship with work, his more easily achieved work goals. The tourist would like that simplicity, perhaps, if he didn’t have to sacrifice anything much for it. And I would think that the tourist is enlightened at the end in that he understands that the fisherman is not like him in this way – not everyone is.

在普鲁塔克一例中，我们至少可以问：西尼亚斯此处所展示的，究竟是他在修辞上的本领和有据可查的伊壁鸠鲁式（Epicurean）趣味，还是他的睿智？尤其因为，皮洛士到末了本可给他一个显而易见的答案，却没有给（也许是因为被西尼亚斯的论证手法绕晕了，没想到要去给）—— namely，西尼亚斯所建议他可即刻享用的那种好生活，只要他们的对手仍在外逍遥，对他或他所关切、所负有责任的其他人而言，就并非可容忍地安稳。皮洛士给出这一答案是否合理，至关紧要地，只是一个历史偶然性的问题。而这一点或许不那么显而易见，但对在后续版本中取代其位置的那些渔夫而言，此种偶然性同样是一个巨大因素。我的意思是，简单地设想一下，渔夫或他的某个孩子患上了一种只有昂贵治疗才能治愈的重症——并设想这确是90年代的墨西哥或巴西，或任何不存在强有力全民医保体系的地方。他当初不抓住机会积攒些财富的决定，倏然之间就得换个角度看。这一决定让他和他的家人更暴露在命运的风向之下。渔夫的生活也许是天堂般的，但那是一座脆弱的天堂，而一段哪怕只有一点像商人之所议那样的创业之旅，本可让它更安稳一些。

In the Plutarch case, we can at least ask whether Cineas is here demonstrating his oratorial skills and also-documented Epicurean tastes as much as his wisdom, not least as there is, very possibly, an obvious answer that Pyrrhus could give to him at the end, but doesn’t (perhaps because, bamboozled by Cineas’s argumentative manoeuvre, he doesn’t think to) – namely that the good life Cineas suggests he might enjoy already would not be tolerably secure, for him or for others he cares for or is duty-bound to, as long as their adversaries are at large. Whether or not it would be reasonable for Pyrrhus to give that answer is, crucially, just a matter of historical contingency. And it might not be so obvious, but such contingency is also a huge factor for the fishermen who take his place in subsequent versions of the story. I mean, suppose simply that the fisherman or one of his children develops a severe health problem, curable only with expensive treatment – and suppose this is indeed 90s Mexico or Brazil, or anywhere where there is not a strong universal healthcare system. Suddenly his decision not to take the opportunity to amass some wealth has to be seen in a slightly different light. It has left him and his family more exposed to the winds of fortune. Paradisical the fisherman’s life may be, but it’s a delicate paradise, and an entrepreneurial journey even a little like the one the businessman proposes might have made it more secure.

在查阅皮洛士与西尼亚斯故事的过程中，我发现自己在这一点上有西蒙娜·德·波伏娃（Simone de Beauvoir）为同盟——她第一部出版的非虚构著作于1944年问世，但直到2004年才被译成英文，题为《皮洛士与西尼亚斯》（Pyrrhus and Cineas），正是以那则故事为出发点。或者说，以它的某个版本为出发点。波伏娃远未得出皮洛士是蠢人或瘾君子的结论，而是充满激情地站在他这一边。她如此反驳西尼亚斯的诘问：「这往往是少年人初醒反思之声时的念头。孩提时，他像皮洛士一样：奔跑、玩耍而不自问，他所创造之物在他看来仿佛具有绝对的存在。它们自身就携带着自己存在的理由。」她暗示，这才是健康而正当的活法；西尼亚斯式的思维则源于「一阵坏情绪或幼稚的亢奋」。

Reading up on the Pyrrhus and Cineas story, I found that I have an ally here in Simone de Beauvoir, whose first published nonfiction book, released in 1944 but not translated into English until 2004, is titled Pyrrhus and Cineas and takes its starting point from that story. Or a version of it, anyway. Far from concluding that Pyrrhus is a fool or an addict, de Beauvoir sides passionately with him. She objects to Cineas’s queries thus: ‘Such are often the thoughts of the adolescent when the voice of reflection awakens in him. As a child, he was like Pyrrhus: he ran, he played without asking himself questions, and the objects that he created seemed to him endowed with an absolute existence. They carried within themselves their reason for being.’ This is the healthy and right way to live, she implies; Cineas-style thinking arises from ‘a fit of ill humour or puerile exaltation’.

然而显眼的是，波伏娃把皮洛士那串「战争与征服到足够之后他将做什么」的清单如此转述：「我将歇息。」这一对所设想状态的掏空，使她得以质问：「为何……要以虚无的宁静为尺，来谴责人类的事业？」这并不像它看似的那般是个诡诈的招数。西蒙娜·德·波伏娃并非在攻击普鲁塔克。她编排了自己那个简化版的故事以服务于她的目的，她的目的其实只是提出、进而回答这样一个问题：我们如何能对自己的事业与关切的那些任意的或半任意的限度感到满足。（「所有人类的事业……似乎都荒谬，因为它们唯有通过给自己设限才得以存在，而人总能越过这些限度，自嘲般地问道：『为何到此为止？为何不再走远些？有何用处？』」）

Conspicuously though, de Beauvoir paraphrases Pyrrhus’s list of things he will do when he’s warred and conquered enough thus: ‘I will rest’. This emptying of the envisioned state enables her to demand, ‘why […] condemn human projects by comparing them with the tranquillity of nothingness?’ This is not the devious move it perhaps seems. Simone de Beauvoir is not attacking Plutarch. She’s composed her own simplified version of the story to suit her purpose, which is really to ask and then answer the question of how we can be content with the arbitrary or semi-arbitrary limitations of our endeavours and concerns. (‘All human projects […] seem absurd because they exist only by setting limits for themselves, and one can always overstep these limits, asking oneself derisively, “Why as far as this? Why not further? What’s the use?”’) .

可在普鲁塔克的版本里，皮洛士说的是饮酒与深谈，而我们大可推测，他所指远不止于此——他所指的大致就是他那些渔夫后裔所描述的、他们度日的方式。当然，在友谊与婚姻之中，在抚育孩子之时，在采集并享用饮食的盛宴之中，在奏乐与通宵起舞之中，都有着大量正经的事业。我也不认为波伏娃会对此有异议。到她的书末尾，她似乎在说，她的西尼亚斯所主张跳过去径直抵达的那种「歇息」、那种「虚无」，只能是反思的一个虚构，无法在现实中被经验。（「凡有 viewpoints 之处，便不存在虚无。」「每一种思想、每一种感受都是事业。」「我们从未摆脱那一人类境况，我们也无从从外部设想它以作评判。」）

But Pyrrhus, in the Plutarch version, speaks of drinking and deep conversations, and we can surely surmise that he means a lot more than that – he means something like what his fishermen heirs describe doing with their days. And of course in friendships and marriages, in bringing up children, in gathering and feasting on food and drink, and in making music and dancing into the night, there are plenty of decent projects. Nor do I think that de Beauvoir would dispute this. By the end of her book, she seems to be saying that the ‘rest’, the ‘nothingness’ her Cineas advocates skipping to can only ever be a figment of reflection and cannot be experienced in actuality. (‘Wherever there is a point of view, there is not nothingness.’ ‘Every thought, every feeling is project.’ ‘We never escape from that human condition, and we have no way to envision it from the outside in order to judge it.’)

如此看来，波伏娃似乎终究算不上同盟。但她的确是同盟——在于她欣赏本真生命事业的多样性与本质上自我证成的性质。我觉得颇为有趣——也颇有违她的一贯作风——的是，为了论证她的形而上学论点，她把渔夫的生活误判为虚无，恰如商人或许所做的那样；与此同时，她所瞄准的，则有点像渔夫对商人生活的误判——把它判为毫无意义。我敢打赌，若把她这部以之命名的故事的后世诸版本一一呈现在她面前，她不会选边站。

It might seem then that de Beauvoir is not such an ally after all. But really she is an ally in her appreciation of the diversity and essentially self-justifying nature of authentic life projects. It seems to me funny – because out of character – that she, in order to make her metaphysical argument, mischaracterises the life of the fisherman as nothingness, much as the businessman perhaps does; meanwhile her target is something like the fisherman’s mischaracterisation of the businessman’s life as pointlessness. I’d hazard a guess that, presented with all these descendants of her book’s namesake story, she would not take sides.

我终归在想，该如何理解我所看到的这种把这则故事诠释为不选边的可能——尽管它的许多讲述者与再讲述者似乎都聚焦于一种相反的诠释。一方面，我倾向于认为，我们在此所见，是真理之韧性的一个明证：讲一个能自圆其说的故事，那一方程式里便会蕴含种种更深层的真理，无论你在讲述和/或诠释它时是否辨认出、并串联起那些特定的点。我此处在指的主要真理，是关于人类多样性之广度的那种——须说明白。另一方面，我又怀疑我们在此所见的，会不会其实更近乎文本之诠释弹性的一种明证——那种我们往往都能从中各取所需的东西。

I wonder how, in the end, to understand the possibility I see of interpreting the story as not taking sides, despite so many of its tellers and re-tellers seemingly focusing on an opposite interpretation. On the one hand, I’m inclined to think that what we have here is a demonstration of the resilience of truth – tell a story that minimally adds up, and in that equation there will be all sorts of deeper truths, whether or not you, in telling and/or interpreting it, discern and join those particular dots. The main truth I’m in this case referring to is that of the extent of human diversity, to be clear. On the other hand, I wonder if what we have here mightn’t actually be more of a demonstration of just the interpretive flexibility of texts, the way that we can often all find in them what we want to.
