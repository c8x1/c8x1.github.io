时间的伦理：从海德格尔的「向死而生」到数字时代的永恒现在

从海德格尔的「向死而生」到数字时代的永恒现在

Martin Heidegger / 当代解读·Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy·2026年3月20日·22分钟阅读·原文

时间是人类存在的基本维度，但在数字时代，我们对时间的体验正在经历根本性的变化。从海德格尔的「向死而生」到当代数字文化的「永恒现在」，时间的伦理问题比以往任何时候都更加紧迫。我们如何在碎片化的时间体验中重新找到存在的深度？

Time is a fundamental dimension of human existence, yet in the digital age, our experience of time is undergoing a radical transformation. From Heidegger's 'being-toward-death' to the 'eternal now' of contemporary digital culture, the ethical questions of time have become more urgent than ever. How do we rediscover the depth of existence within a fragmented experience of time?

海德格尔在《存在与时间》中提出了「向死而生」（Sein-zum-Tode）的核心概念。他认为，人的存在（Dasein）本质上是对自己有限性的觉知。死亡不是生命的终点事件，而是贯穿整个存在的可能性——正是因为知道自己终将死去，每一个当下的选择才具有了重量和意义。海德格尔指出，大多数人生活在一种「沉沦」状态中——被日常琐事所吞没，逃避面对自身的有限性，将自己消散在「常人」（das Man）的无差别之中。沉沦状态下的人被「好奇」（Neugier）所驱动——不断追求新的刺激，但从不深入；不断被新事物吸引，但从不停留。

In Being and Time, Heidegger introduces the core concept of 'being-toward-death' (Sein-zum-Tode). He argues that human existence (Dasein) is essentially an awareness of its own finitude. Death is not an endpoint event of life, but a possibility that pervades all of existence — precisely because we know we will die, every present choice gains weight and significance. Heidegger observes that most people live in a state of 'falling' (Verfallenheit) — swallowed up by everyday trivialities, evading the confrontation with their own finitude, dissolving themselves into the indistinguishable mass of 'the They' (das Man). Those in a fallen state are driven by 'curiosity' (Neugier) — constantly chasing new stimuli without ever going deeper; perpetually drawn to novelty without ever lingering.

数字技术深刻地改变了我们对时间的体验。社交媒体的信息流创造了一种「永恒现在」——过去和未来都被压缩为一个不断刷新的当下。短视频、即时通讯和实时推送使我们永远处于「现在」的暴政之下，没有间隙去回溯过去或展望未来。韩炳哲将这种状态描述为「没有否定性的时间」——一个没有停顿、没有间隔、没有沉思空间的时间连续体。在这种时间体验中，深度记忆被即时信息取代，长远规划被短期满足取代，存在的厚度被表面的速度取代。

Digital technology has profoundly altered our experience of time. Social media feeds have created an 'eternal now' — past and future compressed into an endlessly refreshing present. Short videos, instant messaging, and real-time push notifications trap us under the tyranny of the 'now,' leaving no gap to look back or look ahead. Byung-Chul Han describes this condition as 'time without negativity' — a temporal continuum with no pauses, no intervals, no space for contemplation. In this experience of time, deep memory is replaced by instant information, long-term planning by short-term gratification, and the thickness of existence by superficial speed.

时间的伦理要求我们重新审视如何度过我们有限的存在。如果海德格尔是对的——本真的存在需要面对自身的有限性——那么数字技术是否在阻止我们成为本真的自己？当算法通过无限的内容推荐来填充我们的每一个空闲时刻，我们是否失去了面对自身有限性的机会？当社交媒体将死亡和衰老从日常视野中清除，我们是否在逃避存在最根本的事实？海德格尔认为，只有真诚地面对自己终将死去这一事实，才能真正开始「存在」——开始有意识地选择如何度过每一刻，而不是被动地被日常的惯性所裹挟。

The ethics of time demands that we re-examine how we spend our finite existence. If Heidegger is right — that authentic existence requires confronting one's own finitude — then is digital technology preventing us from becoming our authentic selves? When algorithms fill every spare moment with infinite content recommendations, have we lost the opportunity to face our own finitude? When social media erases death and aging from our daily horizon, are we fleeing from the most fundamental fact of existence? Heidegger believed that only by honestly facing the fact of our own mortality can we truly begin to 'exist' — to consciously choose how to live each moment, rather than passively being swept along by everyday inertia.

重建时间的伦理需要几个关键的实践：允许「无聊」的存在——不被刺激填充的时间是面对自身存在的前提，在无聊中我们才能听到自己内心的声音；培养「等待」的能力——在一个一切都即时可得的时代，学会等待本身就是一种抵抗，它让我们重新体验时间的厚度；以及最重要的，重拾对有限性的觉知——不是因为有限性令人沮丧，而是因为它赋予每一个选择以不可替代的独特性。在数字时代，这种面对比以往更加困难，也更加必要。学会在碎片化的时间中找到存在的深度，是数字时代最重要的生活艺术之一。

Rebuilding the ethics of time requires several key practices: allowing 'boredom' to exist — time not filled with stimuli is a prerequisite for confronting one's own existence, and only in boredom can we hear our inner voice; cultivating the capacity to 'wait' — in an era where everything is instantly available, learning to wait is itself a form of resistance that lets us re-experience the thickness of time; and most importantly, recovering an awareness of finitude — not because finitude is depressing, but because it gives every choice its irreplaceable uniqueness. In the digital age, this confrontation is more difficult than ever, yet more necessary than ever. Learning to find existential depth within fragmented time is one of the most important arts of living in the digital age.
