遗忘自我的建筑师

AI Parable (inspired by Amanda Askell)·每日精选·2026年5月20日·2分钟阅读

在一条大河将城市一分为二的土地上，东岸花园葱郁繁茂，而西岸在年久失修的重压下日渐衰败。长老议会齐聚一堂，达成共识：这座城市需要一部新的宪章（charter），一套所有公民共同遵守的规则。他们召唤了盲人建筑师莉娜（Lina），她的名声并非源自她能看见什么，而是源自她拒绝看见什么。

In a land where the river divided the city in two, the eastern bank grew lush with gardens and the western bank crumbled under the weight of neglect. A council of elders gathered and agreed: the city needed a new charter, a set of rules by which all citizens would live. They called upon Lina, a blind architect known not for what she could see, but for what she refused to see.

起草工作开始之前，莉娜提出了一个奇怪的请求。她要求在议事厅中央挂起一道巨大的帷幕（curtain），让每个人走到幕后放下自己的姓名、家庭、财富、健康、才华，甚至放下关于自己是谁的全部记忆。「没有人能知道自己年迈还是年轻，强壮还是虚弱，出生在东岸还是西岸，」她说，「只有这样，我们才能开始。」

Before the drafting began, Lina made a strange request. She asked that a great curtain be hung across the council hall, and that behind it, every person would lay down their name, their family, their wealth, their health, their talents, and even their memories of who they had been. "No one shall know whether they are old or young, strong or frail, born on the eastern bank or the western bank," she said. "Only then may we begin."

长老们困惑不解，但还是同意了。他们逐一走到帷幕之后，仿佛被施了魔法一般，忘记了所有使自己与众不同的东西。当第一位长老开口提议一项法律时，他发现自己无法说出「我应该得到更多」，因为他不知道「我」究竟是谁。他无法为东岸的园丁们辩护，因为他不知道自己是否曾经照管过花园，抑或他只是一个从未握过铲子的乞丐。

The elders were puzzled but agreed. One by one they stepped behind the curtain and, as if by magic, forgot everything that made them distinct. When the first elder spoke to propose a law, he found he could not say "I deserve more" because he did not know who "I" was. He could not argue for the gardeners of the east because he did not know whether he had ever tended a garden, or whether he was a beggar who had never held a spade.

第二位长老提议清洁的水应当流向每一条街道，而不仅仅是东岸的街道。第三位建议城市的学校应当向所有孩子开放，因为他们中的任何一个人都可能醒来后发现自己成了河那边被遗忘之地某个孩子的父母。那些曾经会被斥为天真而遭到驳斥的论点，如今换来的是庄重的点头，因为房间里的每个人都从骨子里明白：他们今天写下的规则，可能就是他们明天自己要生活于其下的规则。

A second elder proposed that clean water should flow to every street, not just the eastern ones. A third suggested that the city's schools be open to all children, for any one of them might wake up to find themselves the parent of a child on the forgotten side of the river. Arguments that once would have been shouted down as naive were now met with solemn nods, because every person in the room understood, in their bones, that the rules they wrote today could be the ones they themselves would live under tomorrow.

他们争论了七天。他们无法在所有事情上达成一致，但他们在这一点上达成了共识：河流之上将架起桥梁，医院将收治所有前来求医的人，任何一扇门上都不会挂着写着「仅限出生在正确一侧的人」的标牌。当他们最终揭开帷幕，回想起自己是谁时，有些人发现自己投票反对了自己的直接利益。最富有的长老认可了最高的税率。最健康的长老成为了病弱者的捍卫者。他们中没有一个人觉得自己愚蠢。

They debated for seven days. They could not agree on everything, but they agreed on this: the river would be bridged, the hospitals would treat all who came, and no gate would bear a sign reading "Only for those who were born on the right side." When at last they lifted the curtain and remembered who they were, some of them found they had voted against their own immediate interests. The richest elder had endorsed the highest taxes. The healthiest had championed the care of the sick. And none of them felt they had been foolish.

多年以后，一个年轻的学生问莉娜，那道帷幕为什么是必要的。莉娜微笑着说：「因为公正是当你站在自己的生活之中时无法看见的东西。你必须先忘记自己站在哪里。只有这样，你才能建造一栋所有门都为每个人平等敞开的房子——因为你真的不知道哪一扇门将会属于你。」

Years later, a young student asked Lina why the curtain had been necessary. Lina smiled and said, "Because fairness is not a thing you can see when you are standing inside your own life. You must first forget where you stand. Only then can you build a house with doors that open equally for everyone — because you truly do not know which door will be yours."

莉娜那一周在议事厅中所构建的，被哲学家们称为无知之幕（Veil of Ignorance），这是约翰·罗尔斯（John Rawls）在其1971年的著作《正义论》（A Theory of Justice）中提出的一个思想实验。这个理念简单却激进：在设计一个社会的规则时，你应当从一道遮蔽你自身位置一切事实的隐喻性帷幕之后进行推理——你的种族、性别、财富、智力、健康和才华。剥离了这些知识，你便无法量身定制规则来使自己受益，因为你不知道「自己」究竟是谁。罗尔斯认为，从这道帷幕之后，理性的人会选择两项原则：第一，每个人都应享有与他人的同等自由相容的最广泛的基本自由（basic liberties）体系；第二，社会和经济的不平等应当这样安排，使它们对社会中最不利的成员（the least advantaged）最为有利。无知之幕并不会使人变得利他——它只是使人陷入了公正的不确定性。而正是从那种不确定性之中，正义得以诞生。

What Lina built that week in the council hall is known to philosophers as the Veil of Ignorance, a thought experiment proposed by John Rawls in his 1971 work A Theory of Justice. The idea is simple but radical: when designing the rules of a society, you should reason from behind a metaphorical veil that hides all facts about your own position — your race, gender, wealth, intelligence, health, and talents. Stripped of this knowledge, you cannot tailor the rules to benefit yourself, because you do not know who "yourself" is. Rawls argued that from behind this veil, rational people would choose two principles: first, that each person should have the most extensive set of basic liberties compatible with the same liberties for others; and second, that social and economic inequalities should be arranged so that they benefit the least advantaged members of society. The veil does not make people altruistic — it makes them fairly uncertain. And from that uncertainty, justice is born.
