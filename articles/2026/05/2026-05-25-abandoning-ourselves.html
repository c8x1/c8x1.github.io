放弃自我：选择、遗憾与我们未能度过的人生

从萨特到克尔凯郭尔：自由的重负如何塑造我们

Daily Curated Editorial·每日精选·2026年5月25日·10分钟阅读·原文

此刻，一个选择正摆在你面前，而且这不是一个微不足道的选择。你可以继续阅读这篇文章，也可以关掉页面去做完全不同的事情。那个你不会采取的行动——那个备选项——已经有了一个轮廓和分量。它代表了你这个下午一个永远不会发生的版本，一条思想和情感的轨迹，在你决定继续阅读的那一刻，它便不再是一种可能性，而彻底归于虚无。这就是人类生活中那种奇特的、通常不可见的结构：我们所做的每一个选择，同时也是我们所放弃的一整套选择，从某种意义上说，我们始终在成为某个我们永远不会成为的人。

There is a choice standing before you right now, and it is not a small one. You could continue reading this essay, or you could close the page and do something else entirely. That alternative action -- the one you will not take -- already has a shape and a weight. It represents a version of your afternoon that will never come to pass, a trajectory of thought and feeling that will simply cease to exist as a possibility the moment you commit to reading on. This is the strange, usually invisible architecture of human life: every choice we make is also a collection of choices we abandon, and we are, in a sense, always in the process of becoming someone we will never be.

诗人罗伯特·弗罗斯特对此深有体会——当他站在黄色树林的分岔路口，走上了那条人迹更少的路。然而，让他的诗久久萦绕不去的，并非他选择了的那条路，而是他没有走的那条——那条"同样美丽"的路，那条"渴望被踩踏"的路，那条他告诉自己也许有一天会回来的路，尽管他心知肚明自己永远不会回来。这首诗并非真正关于勇气或个人主义。它关于的是未选择之物的忧郁，是那种萦绕不去的意识：选择任何东西，都意味着让某种珍贵之物悄然流逝。

The poet Robert Frost understood this when he stood at the fork in the yellow wood and took the road less traveled by. But what makes his poem linger is not the road he chose. It is the road he did not -- the path that was "just as fair" and that "wanted wear," the one he tells himself he might return to someday, knowing full well he never will. The poem is not really about daring or individualism. It is about the melancholy of the untaken, the haunting awareness that to choose anything at all is to let something precious slip away.

哲学为这种处境赋予了一个名字，尽管在日常对话中鲜少使用：自由的苦恼。让-保罗·萨特认为，人类"被判定为自由的"——我们无法逃避选择的负担，因为即使拒绝选择，本身也是一种选择。不存在中立地带，不存在一个可以让我们审视所有选项而尚未做出承诺的立场。对萨特而言，这不仅仅是一个心理学观察，而是人类存在的根本结构。我们并非生来就拥有固定的本性或本质。我们通过自己所做的选择成为我们所是的人，而每一个选择都携带着我们放弃的所有替代可能性的幽灵。

Philosophy has a name for this condition, though it rarely uses it in ordinary conversation: the anguish of freedom. Jean-Paul Sartre argued that human beings are "condemned to be free" -- that we cannot escape the burden of choosing, because even the refusal to choose is itself a choice. There is no neutral ground, no position from which we can survey our options without already committing to one. For Sartre, this is not a merely psychological observation. It is the fundamental structure of human existence. We are not born with a fixed nature or essence. We become what we are through the choices we make, and each of those choices carries with it the ghost of every alternative we declined.

正因如此，对萨特来说，自由并非被体验为解放，而是被体验为苦恼。当你站在一个重大决定的门槛上——是否接受一份在另一个城市的工作，是否结束一段关系，是否要孩子——你不仅仅是在权衡各种选项。在非常真实的意义上，你在决定你将成为什么样的人。而苦恼源于一种认知：没有任何权威、任何道德准则、任何外部力量能够替你做出这个决定。正如萨特所说，你"无可推诿"。宇宙不会提供一个正确答案。你必须通过选择来创造正确答案，然后承担选择的重量。

This is why freedom, for Sartre, is experienced not as liberation but as anguish. When you stand at the threshold of a significant decision -- whether to accept a job in a new city, whether to end a relationship, whether to have children -- you are not simply weighing options. You are, in a very real sense, deciding who you will become. And the anguish arises from the knowledge that no authority, no moral code, no external force can make this decision for you. You are, as Sartre puts it, "without excuse." The universe does not provide a right answer. You must create the right answer by choosing it, and then bear the weight of having chosen.

设想一个具体的例子。一位二十多岁的女性获得了一份驻外记者的职位。这是她从大学时代就梦寐以求的工作——那种如果出现就只会出现一次的机会。但接受它意味着离开她的伴侣、她年迈的父母、她用了近十年时间建立起了归属感的城市。如果她留下，她保住了这一切，但那份工作将永远消失，随之消失的是她生命中一个特定的弧线：深夜从不熟悉的城市发回的报道、身处事件中心的兴奋、那种只有远离家乡才能慢慢积累起来的专业技能与自立精神。如果她离去，她获得了这一切，却失去了与所爱之人朝夕相伴的温暖、在同一座城市生活的静默满足、根须逐渐深扎大地的踏实感。

Consider a concrete case. A woman in her late twenties is offered a position as a foreign correspondent. It is the job she has wanted since university -- the kind of opportunity that comes once, if it comes at all. But accepting it means leaving behind her partner, her aging parents, the city where she has built a community over nearly a decade. If she stays, she keeps all of that, but the job vanishes, and with it a particular arc of her life: the late-night dispatches from unfamiliar cities, the exhilaration of being at the center of events, the slow accumulation of a certain kind of expertise and self-reliance that only distance from home can produce. If she goes, she gains all of that, but loses the warmth of daily proximity to the people she loves, the quiet satisfactions of a life lived in the same place, the gradual deepening of roots.

没有任何计算方法能够解决这个困境。它不是一个等待被发现答案的数学问题。无论她选择什么，她都在选择一个自我，同时放弃另一个自我——一个她可能成为的人，拥有不同的记忆、不同的遗憾、不同的喜悦。萨特的观点是，这不是理性的失败。这正是理性面对人类自由这一赤裸事实时的本来面目。她所感受到的苦恼不是系统中的漏洞，而是系统本身。

There is no calculus that can resolve this. It is not a math problem with a solution waiting to be discovered. Whatever she chooses, she will be choosing a self, and abandoning another self -- a person she might have been, with different memories, different regrets, different joys. Sartre's point is that this is not a failure of reason. This is what reason looks like when it encounters the raw fact of human freedom. The anguish she feels is not a bug in the system. It is the system.

索伦·克尔凯郭尔比萨特更早地看到了这一点，也许看得更深。在萨特写作的一个世纪之前，克尔凯郭尔就坚持认为，人类存在的核心任务不是发现自己，而是选择自己。这是一个微妙却至关重要的区分。浪漫主义传统倾向于将自我视为埋藏在我们内心深处的东西，等待着被发掘——一件一旦被找到就能解决所有不确定性的宝藏。克尔凯郭尔完全拒绝了这幅图景。不存在隐藏在社会规训层之下的真实自我。存在的只有你通过自己的选择正在创造的自我，而最根本的选择——那个支撑所有其他选择的选择——就是承担对这个创造过程的责任。

Soren Kierkegaard saw this even earlier, and perhaps even more deeply. Writing a century before Sartre, Kierkegaard insisted that the central task of human existence is not to discover yourself but to choose yourself. This is a subtle but crucial distinction. The Romantic tradition tends to treat the self as something buried inside us, waiting to be unearthed -- a buried treasure that, once found, will resolve all our uncertainties. Kierkegaard rejects this picture entirely. There is no true self hidden beneath layers of social conditioning. There is only the self you are in the process of creating through your choices, and the fundamental choice -- the one that underlies all others -- is the choice to take responsibility for that process.

在克尔凯郭尔的意义上选择自己，就是站在你人生各种开放的可能性面前说：这是我的。不是因为它不可避免，不是因为它是一切可能路径中最好的，而是因为你选择了它，充分地、自由地选择了它，并且你愿意承担它。这就是克尔凯郭尔所说的"跳跃"——不是逻辑上的跳跃，不是到达某个结论的跳跃，而是跳入自身存在的跳跃，是对成为你所是的那个特定之人的承诺，而非你可能成为的无限多的可能性。

To choose yourself, in Kierkegaard's sense, is to stand before the open possibilities of your life and say: this is mine. Not because it was inevitable, not because it is the best of all possible paths, but because you have chosen it, fully and freely, and you are willing to own it. This is what Kierkegaard calls "the leap" -- not a leap of logic, not a leap to a conclusion, but a leap into one's own existence, a commitment to being the particular person you are rather than the infinitely many persons you might have been.

当然，困难在于，你原本可能成为的那无限多的人并不会因为你做出了选择就简单地消失。它们徘徊不去，萦绕不去。豪尔赫·路易斯·博尔赫斯在他的短篇小说《小径分岔的花园》中美妙地捕捉了这种萦绕——故事中，一个人构想了一部小说，也构想了一个宇宙，在这个宇宙中，每一个决定的每一个可能结果都被同时实现。在这个分岔的花园中，没有任何选择是最终的选择，因为每一条路径都被走过了。选择去参战的人和选择留下来的人都同样存在。接受那份工作的女性和拒绝它的女性都同样真实。没有任何东西被失去，因为没有任何东西被排除在外。

The difficulty, of course, is that the infinitely many persons you might have been do not simply disappear once you have made your choice. They linger. They haunt. Jorge Luis Borges captured this haunting beautifully in his short story "The Garden of Forking Paths," in which a man imagines a novel -- and a universe -- in which every possible outcome of every decision is realized simultaneously. In this garden of forking paths, no choice is ever final because every path is taken. The man who chooses to go to war and the man who chooses to stay home both exist. The woman who accepts the job and the woman who declines it are both real. Nothing is lost because nothing is excluded.

乍听之下，这像是一个天堂。如果我们能够过每一种生活、探索每一个分岔、体验我们自身每一个可能的版本呢？那难道不会消解选择的苦恼吗？难道不会将我们从未选择之路的暴政中解放出来吗？

This sounds, at first, like a paradise. What if we could live every life, explore every fork, experience every possible version of ourselves? Would that not dissolve the anguish of choosing? Would it not free us from the tyranny of the untaken road?

但想一想会失去什么。在博尔赫斯的花园中，没有哪条单独的路径具有意义，因为每一条路径都被走过了。参战的勇气毫无意义，如果怯懦之路也同样真实。留下的忠诚空洞无物，如果离去的冒险也在同时被经历着。选择本身——赋予人类生活以质地和重量的那个行为——变得毫无重量。如果每一个结果都被实现了，那么没有任何结果是有意义的。如果你不会失去任何东西，那么你所拥有的一切都不珍贵。

But consider what would be lost. In Borges's garden, no single path has meaning, because every path is taken. The courage of going to war is meaningless if the coward's path is equally real. The fidelity of staying is empty if the adventure of leaving is also, simultaneously, lived. Choice itself -- the very act that gives human life its texture and its gravity -- becomes weightless. If every outcome is realized, then no outcome matters. If you cannot lose anything, then nothing you have is precious.

这就是遗憾核心处的悖论。我们感到遗憾，是因为我们失去了某样东西——一种可能性、一种未来、一个永远不会成真的自我版本。然而，正是这种失去，赋予了我们实际的生活以意义。如果我们能够过上所有可能的生活，选择将变得空洞。正是选择的不可逆性——我们无法回头、那条未选择的路将永远未被选择这一事实——使我们的决定具有意义。意义并不脱离失去而独立存在。意义从失去之中涌现。

This is the paradox at the heart of regret. We feel regret because we have lost something -- a possibility, a future, a version of ourselves that will never come into being. And yet it is precisely this loss that gives our actual lives their meaning. If we could live all possible lives, choice would be empty. It is the irreversibility of choosing -- the fact that we cannot go back, that the road not taken remains forever untaken -- that makes our decisions meaningful. Meaning does not float free of loss. Meaning emerges from loss.

这样来想。一幅画的力量不仅来自画布上的内容，也来自画家选择留在外面的东西。每一笔触也是一个不做另一种笔触的决定。每一种颜色是对所有其他颜色的拒绝。这种约束不是对艺术的限制，这种约束就是艺术本身。没有约束，就没有构图、没有形式、没有美——只有一切可能组合的无形蔓延，而这，就意味着一无所有。

Think of it this way. A painting gains its power not just from what is on the canvas but from what the artist chose to leave out. Every brushstroke is also a decision not to make a different brushstroke. Every color is a rejection of every other color. The constraint is not a limitation on the art. The constraint is the art. Without the constraint, there would be no composition, no form, no beauty -- only a formless sprawl of every possible combination, which is to say, nothing at all.

人的生活也是如此。你所成为的那个人，和你没有走上的路一样，被那些你没有走的路径所塑造。那个本可能成为音乐家的医生，将那种未被经历的生命携刻在体内，它影响着一切：她聆听音乐的方式，她思考纪律与创造力的方式，她理解承诺之意义的方式。被放弃的可能性并非简单地消失了。它变成了一种内在的指南针，一个参照点，她所有实际的选择都以此作为衡量。有时它使她更加感恩现有的生活。有时它隐隐作痛。但无论哪种情况，它都赋予她的存在一种否则不可能拥有的深度与共鸣。

Human lives are like this. The person you are is shaped as much by the paths you did not take as by the one you did. The doctor who might have been a musician carries that unlived life inside her, and it colors everything: the way she listens to music, the way she thinks about discipline and creativity, the way she understands the meaning of commitment. The abandoned possibility is not simply gone. It becomes a kind of inner compass, a point of reference against which all her actual choices are measured. Sometimes it sharpens her gratitude for the life she has. Sometimes it aches. But in either case, it gives her existence a depth and resonance that would be impossible without it.

正因如此，毫无遗憾地生活这一幻想——只做"正确"的选择、从不回头的幻想——归根结底是一种没有深度的生活的幻想。没有遗憾的人生，是从未有任何东西处于危险之中的人生。是一个从未对自己的选项投入足够关怀、以至于感受不到所放弃之物之损失的人的人生。而一个没有任何东西处于危险之中的人生，按任何有意义的尺度来衡量，都算不上真正的人生。

This is why the fantasy of living without regret -- the fantasy of making only the "right" choices and never looking back -- is ultimately a fantasy of living without depth. A life without regret is a life in which nothing was ever at stake. It is the life of someone who never cared enough about their options to feel the loss of the ones they let go. And a life in which nothing is at stake is, by any meaningful standard, not really a life at all.

哲学家米兰·昆德拉在《不能承受的生命之轻》中触及了这一点，他将德国哲学家尼采的永恒回归理念——即每一个事件都无限次地重演——与只发生一次的生命的"轻盈"进行了对比。如果一切都在重演，那么每一个选择都承载着无限的重量；我们被困在后果的永恒之中。但如果一切都只发生一次，如果没有重复也没有第二次机会，那么我们的选择似乎就脱离了意义——它们变得"轻盈"、无锚，仿佛我们所做的一切最终都无关紧要，因为它们永远不会被拿来与任何东西比较。

The philosopher Milan Kundera touched on this in "The Unbearable Lightness of Being," where he contrasts the German philosopher Nietzsche's idea of eternal return -- the notion that every event recurs infinitely -- with the "lightness" of a life that happens only once. If everything recurs, then every choice carries infinite weight; we are trapped in an eternity of consequence. But if everything happens only once, if there is no repetition and no second chance, then our choices seem to float free of significance -- they are "light," unmoored, as though nothing we do ultimately matters because it will never be compared to anything.

昆德拉的洞见在于，这两种状况——永恒回归的不可承受之重和单一生命的不可承受之轻——都令人难以承受。但轻盈最终更难承受。我们需要那种重量。我们需要那种感觉——我们的选择是不可逆的，它们在存在的肌理上刻下了无法抹去的印记。没有那种重量，我们将像幽灵一样漂泊于生活之中，触碰不到任何东西，也不被任何东西所触碰。

Kundera's insight is that both of these conditions -- the unbearable weight of eternal return and the unbearable lightness of a single life -- are difficult to bear. But the lightness is, in the end, harder. We need the weight. We need the sense that our choices are irreversible, that they carve something into the fabric of existence that cannot be un-carved. Without that weight, we would drift through life like ghosts, touching nothing and being touched by nothing.

这一切并不是在说遗憾是一种美德，或者说我们应该刻意培养它。持久的、腐蚀性的遗憾——那种使人瘫痪而非照亮人心的遗憾——是一种真正的痛苦，哲学不应该将其浪漫化。但是，被遗憾吞噬和承认遗憾是一种完全人生不可逃避的维度，这二者之间有着本质的区别。前者是一座牢笼。后者不过是诚实。

None of this is to say that regret is a virtue, or that we should cultivate it. Prolonged, corrosive regret -- the kind that paralyzes rather than illuminates -- is a genuine suffering, and philosophy should not romanticize it. But there is a difference between being consumed by regret and acknowledging that regret is an inescapable dimension of a fully human life. The first is a prison. The second is simply honesty.

诚实地活着，就是在觉知那些已经失去之物的状态下活着。就是接受每一个选择都是一种死亡——一个可能自我的死亡——而这种死亡，矛盾地，恰恰是任何真正活着的条件。那个拒绝选择的人、那个无限期地保持所有选项开放的人，并没有避免失去。他只是一下子失去了一切，因为一种无限可能性的生活是一种没有任何事情真正发生的生活。

To live honestly is to live in the awareness of what has been lost. It is to accept that every choice is a kind of death -- the death of a possible self -- and that this death is also, paradoxically, the condition for any genuine living. The person who refuses to choose, who holds all options open indefinitely, does not avoid loss. They simply lose everything at once, because a life of infinite possibility is a life in which nothing ever actually happens.

有一个故事，或许是杜撰的，关于哲学家维特根斯坦。据说在临终的病榻上，他对朋友们说："告诉他们，我度过了美妙的一生。"那些了解他生活细节的人对此感到惊讶。他一生痛苦、难相处、经常不快乐。他散尽家财，多次放弃学术哲学，长年独居。然而最终，他宣称这是美妙的。不是因为它无痛、不是因为它是最优的、不是因为它是所有可能人生中最好的，而是因为那是他的人生。他选择了它——或者至少，他通过一次又一次的选择走进了它。而在这选择之中，连痛苦也成为了某种可以被肯定的东西的一部分。

There is a story, perhaps apocryphal, about the philosopher Wittgenstein. On his deathbed, he is said to have told his friends: "Tell them I've had a wonderful life." Those who knew the details of his life found this astonishing. He had been tortured, difficult, often unhappy. He had given away a fortune, abandoned academic philosophy multiple times, and spent years in isolation. And yet, at the end, he claimed it as wonderful. Not because it was painless or optimal or the best of all possible lives. But because it was his. He had chosen it -- or at least, he had chosen himself into it. And in the choosing, even the suffering had become part of something that could be affirmed.

这是关于选择的哲学所能提供的最深刻的教诲。我们并未因那些我们没有过上的生活而缩减。我们恰恰是由它们所构成。每一个被放弃的可能性都是一根织入我们自身纹理的丝线，赋予其质地、张力和深度。选择的苦恼不是需要被克服的东西。它是一种需要被栖居其中之物——它标志着我们活着、我们自由、在我们短暂而不可重复的时光中，有些东西是攸关紧要的。

This is the deepest lesson that the philosophy of choice has to offer. We are not diminished by the lives we do not live. We are constituted by them. Every abandoned possibility is a thread woven into the fabric of who we are, giving it texture and tension and depth. The anguish of choosing is not something to be overcome. It is something to be inhabited -- the sign that we are alive, that we are free, that something is at stake in the way we spend our brief and unrepeatable time.

那条未选择的路并非虚无。它是你所选择的路投下的影子，没有那影子，你脚下的路将不可见。你将在行走中浑然不觉自己正在行走。而这，也许才是唯一真正令人遗憾的事——不是我们失去了种种可能性，而是我们可能注意不到，正是那种失去，使整个旅程成为真实的。

The road not taken is not nothing. It is the shadow cast by the road you chose, and without that shadow, the road beneath your feet would be invisible. You would walk without knowing you were walking. And that, perhaps, is the only truly regrettable thing -- not that we lose possibilities, but that we could fail to notice that the losing is what makes the whole journey real.
