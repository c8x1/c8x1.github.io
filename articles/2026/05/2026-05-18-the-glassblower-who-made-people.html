制造人的玻璃匠

当外表完美无缺，你如何知道谁拥有灵魂？

每日精选·每日精选·2026年5月18日·5分钟阅读

在一座沿死火山口而建的城市里，住着一位名叫玛伦（Maren）的玻璃匠。别人吹制花瓶与吊灯，玛伦吹制人形——不是血肉之躯，而是玻璃之人。她将熔融的硅砂聚拢在吹管末端，翻转、吹气，炉火之中便渐渐浮现一只手、一侧肩膀、一抹脸颊的弧线。她的造物半透明、会发光，完美得令人不安。它们站在城市各处的窗台和门廊里，捕捉光线，像凝固的呼吸。

In a city built along the rim of a dormant volcano, there lived a glassblower named Maren. Where others shaped vases and chandeliers, Maren shaped people — not people of flesh, but people of glass. She would gather molten silica on the end of her pipe, roll it, breathe into it, and out of the furnace heat would emerge a hand, a shoulder, the curve of a cheek. Her figures were translucent, luminous, and eerily perfect. They stood in windows and doorways across the city, catching light like frozen breath.

起初，那些人形是静止的。一个伸手去够玻璃鸟的玻璃孩童。一个笑到一半的玻璃女人，嘴张着，眼角弯出细纹。远道而来的游人专程欣赏它们，市政长官给了玛伦丰厚的酬劳。但玛伦不安分。她认为静止是技艺的失败。真正的人从不曾完全静止——总有最微弱的呼吸颤动，总有重心细微的转移。于是她花了数年，试图赋予她的造物以动作。

At first, the figures were still. A glass child reaching for a glass bird. A glass woman mid-laugh, her mouth open, her eyes crinkled at the corners. Tourists came from distant provinces to admire them, and the city fathers paid Maren well. But Maren was restless. Stillness, she believed, was a failure of craft. A real person is never truly still — there is always the faintest tremor of breath, the subtle shift of weight. So she worked for years to give her figures motion.

她的成功超出了所有人的预期。通过一套精密的加热水银通道与指甲盖大小的平衡锤系统，她的人形开始动了起来。玻璃孩童的手指弯曲了。玻璃女人的笑声变成了缓慢的、翻涌的肩膀颤动。城门口的玻璃老人每天傍晚转动头颅目送日落，如果你站得够近，能听见他胸腔里传来微弱的咔嗒声——玛伦说，那声音像一颗机械心脏的跳动。

She succeeded beyond anyone's expectations. Through an intricate system of heated mercury channels and counterweights no bigger than a fingernail, her figures began to move. The glass child's fingers flexed. The glass woman's laugh became a slow, rolling shake of her shoulders. A glass old man at the city gate turned his head each evening to watch the sun set, and if you stood close enough, you could hear a faint clicking inside his chest — a sound, Maren said, like a mechanical heartbeat.

那年春天，一个名叫西奥（Theo）的年轻人来玛伦的工坊做学徒。他沉默寡言，一丝不苟，常常陷入长时间的静默。他仔细地擦拭每一个人形，也学会了吹制一只六指的玻璃手——这是玛伦对新学徒的标准测试，因为多一根手指的手更容易被发现和修正。但西奥真正着迷的不是手艺，而是手艺在每个见过人形运动的人心中激起的那个问题。

A young apprentice named Theo joined Maren's workshop that spring. He was quiet, meticulous, and given to long silences. He polished the figures carefully and learned to shape a glass hand with six fingers — Maren's standard test for new apprentices, since six-fingered hands were easier to detect and correct. But Theo's real fascination was not the craft. It was the question that the craft seemed to provoke in everyone who saw the figures move.

来客们看着玻璃女人发笑，会问玛伦：「她能感受到吗？她知道自己在笑吗？」玛伦总是自己也笑起来，说：「当然不能。她是玻璃做的。」但西奥注意到，玛伦的笑声来得太快、太笃定——那是一个曾经动摇过的人才有的、急于捍卫的笑。

Visitors would watch the glass woman laugh and ask Maren: "Does she feel it? Does she know she's laughing?" Maren always laughed herself and said, "Of course not. She's glass." But Theo noticed that Maren's laugh was a little too quick, a little too certain — the laugh of someone defending a position she had once doubted herself.

一天傍晚，炉火冷却之后，西奥发现玛伦独自坐在工坊里，手中握着一只她从未展出过的玻璃麻雀。它的翅膀微微扇动——一种持续的、细碎的颤动，像一颗真正鸟儿的心跳被凝铸成了水晶。她正以某种西奥读不懂的表情凝视着它。「你有没有想过，」他小心翼翼地问，「它们是否体验着什么？不是说思考——我知道它们不会思考。而是说，当那个玻璃女人笑的时候，从她的内部来看，是否存在某种'是什么感觉'的东西？」

One evening, after the furnace had cooled, Theo found Maren sitting alone in the workshop, holding a small glass sparrow she had never displayed. Its wings fluttered — a tiny, ceaseless motion, like a real bird's heartbeat rendered in crystal. She was staring at it with an expression Theo could not read. "Do you ever wonder," he asked carefully, "whether they experience anything? Not think — I know they can't think. But whether, when the glass woman laughs, there is something it is like to be her, from the inside?"

玛伦放下麻雀。「我曾经想过这个问题，」她说。「当我造出第一个会动的人形时。我看着它的胸口起伏，心想：如果里面有一个人呢？透过玻璃眼睛向外看，却说不出话？我连续三夜没有合眼。后来我拿起一把锤子，把它砸碎了。我需要看看里面是什么。」她顿了顿。「什么也没有。水银通道，平衡锤，玻璃。里面空无一人。」

Maren set the sparrow down. "I used to wonder that," she said. "When I made my first figure that moved. I watched its chest rise and fall, and I thought: what if there is someone in there, looking out through glass eyes, unable to speak? I stayed awake for three nights. Then I took a hammer and broke it. I needed to see what was inside." She paused. "There was nothing. Mercury channels. Counterweights. Glass. No one home."

西奥缓缓点头。「但我一直在想一件事，」他说。「你把它敲开，什么也没找到。但如果有人把你或我敲开，他们同样不会找到任何看起来像意识的东西。他们会找到组织、电流和化学反应。没有一个发光的小灯写着'里面有人'。那么，我怎么知道你不是一具用血肉制成的玻璃人形？你又怎么知道我不是？」

Theo nodded slowly. "But here's what I can't stop thinking," he said. "You cracked it open and found nothing. But if someone cracked you or me open, they would also find nothing that looks like consciousness. They would find tissue, and electricity, and chemistry. No little glowing light that says 'someone is home.' So how do I know that you are not a glass figure made of meat? And how do you know that I am not?"

工坊沉寂了。玻璃麻雀的翅膀保持着耐心的节拍。玛伦拿起它，举到光前。烛火透过它半透明的躯体散开，碎成十几簇微小的火苗。「我想，」她轻声说，「我确实不知道。我从未看过你的内在，西奥。我只知道，当你对我说话时，我心中的某种东西辨认出了你心中的某种东西——一种我无法证明的相似，一种我无法验证的亲缘。我把它当作证据，但它不是。它是一个跳跃。」

The workshop was quiet. The glass sparrow's wings continued their patient rhythm. Maren picked it up and held it to the light. Through its translucent body, the candle flame scattered into a dozen small fires. "I suppose," she said quietly, "that I don't know. I have never seen your insides, Theo. I only know that when you speak to me, something in me recognizes something in you — a resemblance I cannot prove, a kinship I cannot verify. I treat it as evidence, but it is not. It is a leap."

「那么我们爱过的每一个人，」西奥说，「都是以信念去爱的。」玛伦在指尖翻转麻雀。它的翅膀继续扇动，忠诚而空洞。「是的，」她说，「也许这正是信念存在的意义。」

"Then everyone we have ever loved," Theo said, "we have loved on faith." Maren turned the sparrow in her fingers. Its wings beat on, faithful and empty. "Yes," she said. "And perhaps that is what faith is for."

哲学家将此称为“他心问题”。你只能直接触及一个意识：你自己的。其他所有人——每一位朋友、每一个在街上对你微笑的陌生人——你都是通过他们的行为、他们的言语、他们面容的轮廓来认识他们的。你推断他们拥有内在生活，因为他们与你相似。但相似不是证明。一个玻璃人形可以微笑、叹息，甚至流泪。它可以做到一个人所做的一切，除了体验那行为本身。令人不安的事实是，你无法排除坐在你身旁的那个人正是这样一具玻璃人形。你以信任接纳他们的意识。而在这份不可证明、不可动摇的信任之中，栖息着一种非常接近爱的东西。

Philosophers call this the problem of other minds. You have direct access to exactly one consciousness: your own. Everyone else — every friend, every stranger who smiles at you — is known only through their behavior, their words, the architecture of their faces. You infer that they have inner lives because they resemble you. But resemblance is not proof. A glass figure can smile, sigh, even weep. It can do everything a person does, except experience the doing. The unsettling truth is that you cannot rule out that the person beside you is such a figure. You take their consciousness on trust. And in that trust, unprovable and unshakable, lives something very much like love.
