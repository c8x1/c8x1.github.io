生命值得活吗？William James 的实用主义「也许」

一位哲学家在布鲁克林大桥上的沉思，以及实用主义对生命意义的回答

John Kaag·Aeon·2026年5月19日·19分钟阅读·原文

"生命最大的用处，是为比生命更长久的事物而耗尽它。"——William James，《William James的思想与品格》(1935)

'The greatest use of life is to spend it on something that will outlast it.' – William James, The Thought and Character of William James (1935)

一年前，十一月的一个傍晚，我决定从曼哈顿的酒店步行七英里，前往布鲁克林的社区书店。那是一个凉爽的日子，正值黄昏将至，万物——即便是纽约那种灰蒙蒙的物事——似乎都泛着微光。我忙着四处张望，差点没注意到有人放在布鲁克林大桥底部的一块白色小牌子。上面绿色的字迹是新漆的，写着：'生命值得活下去。'

A year ago, on a late afternoon in November, I decided to walk the seven miles from my hotel in Manhattan to Brooklyn's Community Bookstore. It was a cool day, on the cusp of evening, at a moment when things, even grimy New York-type of things, seem to glow, and I was so busy looking around that I almost didn't notice the small white sign that someone had placed at the bottom of Brooklyn Bridge. The green lettering was newly painted and read: 'LIFE IS WORTH LIVING.'

对许多人来说，生命的价值从来不成问题。它从未成为谈论或辩论的话题。生命只是被活着，直到不再活着。但有件事困扰着我：如果生命的价值如此显而易见，那块牌子最初为何会被竖起？那是因为，我们中有些人偶尔会发现自己站在桥顶，盘算着一次快速而致命的坠落。在与抑郁症搏斗几十年后的1870年，美国哲学家William James写信给哲学家兼诗人Benjamin Paul Blood说：'从未与自杀的念头调情过的人，算不上受过教育。'

For many people, life's worth is never in question. It never becomes a topic of conversation or debate. Life is simply lived until it is not. But something bothered me: if life's worth is so obvious, why was the sign put up in the first place? It is because there are those of us who occasionally find themselves on the top of the bridge, contemplating a quick and fatal trip to the bottom. Decades after battling depression in 1870, the American philosopher William James wrote to the philosopher and poet Benjamin Paul Blood that 'no man is educated who has never dallied with the thought of suicide'.

在1770年代，James的智识英雄之一David Hume曾论证，自我谋杀不应被视为非法或不道德的行为，因为它只伤害了行为者本人，而且在许多情况下可能减轻巨大的痛苦。继之而起的浪漫主义思潮，只是加深了一种观念——生命和死亡都应由充满激情的个体自由决定。如果你想通过一个最终的选择骤然退出生命，那在很大程度上取决于你自己。James年轻时最喜爱的书之一——很可能只是加深了他存在意义上的不安全感——是歌德的《少年维特之烦恼》(1774)，讲述了一个在三角恋的尖锐尽头自杀的人物。也许生命切割得太深，以至于逃离是可以理解的，甚至是值得尊敬的。更可能的情况是，浪漫主义者——以及James——偶尔将自杀视为一种把握生命的方式，通过终结生命来控制它的运转。我们都在不可控制地向坟墓滑去。也许选择主动坠落会更好。

In the 1770s, David Hume, one of James's intellectual heroes, had argued that self-murder should not be regarded as illegal or immoral since it hurt no one other than the perpetrator, and in many cases might alleviate great suffering. Romanticism, which arose in the subsequent generation of thinkers, only deepened the sense that life – and death – should be determined freely, by passionate individuals. If you wanted to exit life abruptly, by one final choice, that was largely up to you. One of James's favourite books as a young man, one which probably only deepened his sense of existential insecurity, was the Sorrows of Young Werther (1774), Goethe's story of a character who kills himself on the sharp end of a love triangle. Perhaps life cuts so deeply that it is understandable, even respectable, to escape. More likely, the Romantics – and James – occasionally regarded suicide as a way of taking hold of life, controlling its machinations by bringing them to an end. We are all slipping toward the grave, uncontrollably. Maybe it is better to choose to fall off.

令我惊喜的是，桥上的人行道空无一人。我可以独享这一片风景。大桥最高处达276英尺（84米），曾被视为工业世界七大奇迹之一。在1883年建成之前，27名工人在施工中丧生；两年后，Robert Odlum成为第一个从桥上跳下的人。他是一名游泳教练，想证明高速坠落并不一定致命，却遗憾地身亡了。在接下来的一个世纪里，大约有1500人因不同的原因追随了Odlum。我不确定那块牌子救了多少人，但我倾向于认为它收效甚微。

To my surprise and delight, the walkways on the bridge were empty. I'd have the view to myself. With a maximum height of 276 feet (84 metres), it was once regarded as one of the seven wonders of the industrial world. During its construction, 27 workers died, before it was completed in 1883; two years later, Robert Odlum became the first man to jump off the bridge. A swimming instructor who wanted to prove that descending through air at high speed was not necessarily fatal, he sadly died. In the next century, approximately 1,500 people have followed Odlum, for different reasons. I'm not sure how many people are saved by the sign, but I am inclined to think that it is highly ineffective.

桥顶很冷。我望向港湾中的自由女神像，然后回头望向James长大的曼哈顿。然后我向下看去。其中有一种令人恐惧的自由——在某个特定时刻选择生与死的自由，而时间向两个方向无限延伸。在阅读James大半生之后，这种自由仍然对我有吸引力。我想它永远都会有。在20世纪的第一个十年，James发展了美国的实用主义(pragmatism)，一种主张真理应以其现实后果来评判的哲学。这是一种应运而生的哲学，其基本目标是让生命更可承受。而且在大多数情况下确实如此。但如果实用主义(pragmatism)真的拯救了你的生命，那从来不是一劳永逸的事。这是一种始终对经验、态度、事物和事件保持敏感的哲学，即使它们是悲剧性的。虽然James偶尔贬低Arthur Schopenhauer的悲观主义（并拒绝为这位19世纪德国哲学家的纪念碑捐一分钱），James身后的著作揭示了他对这位阴郁思想家直视人类存在之黑暗的意愿的深切敬意。在这种与迅速逼近的黑暗的残酷对峙中，有一种近似勇气的东西。

It was chilly at the top. I looked across to the Statue of Liberty in the harbour and then back into Manhattan where James had grown up. Then I looked down. There was a terrifying liberty in this – the choice to live and die in a particular moment, as time stretches out endlessly in either direction. After reading James for most of my adult life, this liberty still has its appeal. I think it always will. In the first decade of the 20th century, James developed American pragmatism, a philosophy that held that truth should be judged by its practical consequences. It was a world-ready philosophy that, at its most basic, was supposed to make life more liveable. And it does, for the most part. But if pragmatism does save your life, it's never once and for all. This is a philosophy that remains attuned to experiences, attitudes, things and events, even when they are the tragic ones. While James occasionally disparaged Arthur Schopenhauer's pessimism (and refused to give a cent to a memorial in honour of the 19th-century German philosopher), James's posthumous writings reveal a deep respect for the grim thinker's willingness to stare clear-eyed into the gloom of human existence. There was something like courage in this brutal confrontation with quickly impending darkness.

按照James的看法，桥底的那块牌子应该重新漆过，或者至少加以修正：生命值得活下去——也许。正如他在1895年对哈佛大学基督教青年会的一群年轻人所说：'生命值得活下去吗？这完全取决于活着的人(liver)。'这取决于我们每一个人，从字面上讲，就是把生命变成'我们意志的产物'。如今，当我从高处向下凝视时，除了眩晕，我几乎总会想到Steve Rose，一位年轻的黑人心理学研究生，2014年从哈佛大学的William James Hall跳楼身亡。也许James的思维方式本可以拯救他——那种暗示他仍然掌控着自己生命的想法，那种认为结束一切的决定也许是合理的、甚至值得尊敬的，但继续活下去的可能性同样如此。那种可能性就在那里——依然存在，始终存在，即便在一片狼藉与怨愤之中——等待他去探索。也许他认为选择死亡是他唯一可以自由做出的决定，但James总是暗示可能还有其他选择。

According to James, the sign at the bottom of the bridge should be repainted or at least amended: LIFE IS WORTH LIVING – MAYBE. As he said to a crowd of young men from the Harvard YMCA in 1895: 'Is life worth living? It all depends on the liver.' It is up to each of us to, literally, make of life 'what we will'. These days, when I peer down from great heights, in addition to experiencing vertigo, I almost always think about Steve Rose, a young black psychology graduate who threw himself off the William James Hall at Harvard University in 2014. Perhaps James's way of thinking could have saved him – the suggestion that he was still in charge of his life, that the decision to end it all might be reasonable, even respectable, but so too was the possibility of continuing to live. The possibility was right there – still, always, even in the shit and rancour of it – for him to explore. Perhaps he thought that choosing to die was the only free decision at his disposal, but James always suggested there might be other options.

对大多数人来说，自由意志(free will)可以以多种方式行使（不必包括自杀），在许多情况下，人可以选择养成新的思维和行动习惯。如果有意义的自由显得难以捉摸或不切实际，我们大多数人仍然可以选择看到什么、忽略什么。这本身也是有价值的。'智慧的艺术，'James说，'就是知道该忽略什么的艺术。'也许这些可能性本可以让Rose活得更久一些。也许不能。我不敢妄下定论。

For the majority of people, free will can be exercised in any number of ways (which don't have to include committing suicide), and in many of these cases one can choose to embody new habits of thought and action. If meaningful freedom seems evasive or unrealistic, most of us still have a choice about what to see and what to look past. This too can be worthwhile. 'The art of being wise,' James suggested, 'is the art of knowing what to overlook.' Maybe these possibilities could have kept Rose alive for even longer than they did. Maybe not. I don't presume to be sure.

我认为，把人推下桥的一个万无一失的方法，就是假装你知道他们不知道的东西：生命具有无条件价值，而他们错失了某种显而易见的东西。站在桥沿上，我猜想他们会从这种断言中察觉到某种深层的不安全感或傲慢。他们可能纵身一跃，只为证明你是错的。因为事实上，你确实会是错的。James在其论文《论人类某种盲目性》(1899)的最后恳请中提醒读者，他们往往对其他人如何体验生命的意义一无所知。不如就停留在'也许'上。

I think one surefire way to send jumpers off the edge is to pretend that you know something they don't: that life has unconditional value, and that they are missing something that is so patently obvious. On the ledge, I suspect that they'd detect some deep insecurity or hubris in this assertion. And they might jump just to prove you wrong. Because you would, in fact, be wrong. In James's final entreaty in his essay 'On a Certain Blindness in Human Beings' (1899), he reminded his readers that they often don't have a clue about how other people experience the meaning of their lives. Better to leave it at 'maybe'.

我望向水面，夕阳掠过城市的天际线。夜幕将降临，百万颗星辰将再次与百万盏灯火竞争。短期内，灯火会赢。但在无限延长的长期中，星辰会赢。在这两极之间，谁也说不准。就目前而言，我相信James的'也许'——关于生命价值的开放性问题——是对的，至少对我来说是对的，因为它映射了我的存在处境：一个并非总是完全确信生命价值的人。它之所以是对的，我还认为，是因为他的'也许'大致契合了宇宙的开放性问题。一切事物，从最小的真核生物到最复杂的有机系统，都在做出自己的猜测，这是我们人类所谓的'推理'(inferences)的第一步原逻辑(proto-logical)步骤。没有好的猜测，就不会有适应或成长，对我们来说，就不会有任何意义可言。

I looked across the water as the sun skimmed the cityscape. Night would fall and a million stars would once again compete with a million electric lights. In the short term, the electric lights will win. But in the indefinite long term, the stars will. Between these poles, it's anybody's guess. For now, I believe that James's 'maybe' – the open question of life's worth – is right, or at least right for me, because it maps my existential situation as one who is not always entirely sold on life's value. It is also right, I think, because his 'maybe' is roughly fitted to the open question of the cosmos. Everything, from smallest eukaryotic being to the most complex organic system, is in the process of making its own guesses, the first proto-logical step in what we humans call 'inferences'. Without good guesswork, there would be nothing like adaptation or growth and, for us, there would be nothing like meaning.

James追随他的朋友、同属美国哲学家的C S Peirce，相信世界充斥着假设，充斥着使生命以其万千形态成为可能、使我们的生活有价值的那些'也许'。对James来说，星辰并非按完美秩序燃烧，更非按完美秩序显现，人类的生活也不是预先注定的。正如Ralph Waldo Emerson在他最喜爱的文章之一《圆》(1841)中所写：'让我提醒读者，我只是一个实验者。''也许'始终不变，或者像一个'也许'所能的那样不变。这是最好的。它给了我们值得注视、期待和体验的东西。持续的变化带来持续的惊奇，对James而言，这种神秘感——偶然性——往往足以让他在其他实际手段失效时渡过难关。'人类本性中没有一个事实比这更典型，'成熟的James断言，'即它愿意靠偶然(chance)而活。偶然的存在造成了……听天由命的生活与以希望为主旋律的生活之间的区别。'

James followed his friend and fellow American philosopher C S Peirce in believing that the world is teeming with hypotheses, with the 'maybes' that make life, in all its many forms, possible, and make our lives worthwhile. For James, stars do not burn, much less appear, in perfect order, and human lives are not settled in advance. As Ralph Waldo Emerson wrote in his essay 'Circles' (1841), one of James's favourites: 'Let me remind the reader that I am only an experimenter.' The 'maybe' remains constant, or as constant as a 'maybe' can. And this is for the best. It gives us something to watch and expect and experience. Persistent variation gives rise to persistent wonder and, for James, this sense of mystery – of chance – was often enough to see him through when other practical measures failed him. 'No fact in human nature is more characteristic,' the mature James asserted, 'than its willingness to live on a chance. The existence of the chance makes the difference … between a life of which the keynote is resignation and a life of which the keynote is hope.'

如果你把什么东西从桥上扔进下面的水里，它会打破水面，旋即消失。就是消-失-了，一个表示终结的四字词，像'死亡'、'命运'或'失落'一样。无论你多么拼命地尝试，都没有把它找回来或保全它的可能。多年来，我常常想象在深水中失去珍贵之物会是什么感觉——比钥匙或手机珍贵得多的东西。对于微小的物质对象，我猜想几乎没有保全的希望。我也曾考虑过这样一种可能性：一切皆是如此——钥匙、手机、钱包和生命。也许一切都毫无痕迹地消逝了。有些哲学家会对这种解释十分满意——认为万物都在消逝的过程中，在宇宙之日的尽头，什么也不会留下。我只是不属于这些哲学家之列。James也不属于。这种宿命论的确定性与他的'也许'相悖，也与一种我难以缺少的希望相悖。

If you throw something off a bridge into the water below, it breaks the surface and is immediately gone. Just G-O-N-E, a four-letter word of finality, like 'dead' or 'fate' or 'lost'. There's no chance of getting it back or preserving it, no matter how desperately you might try. Over the years, I've often imagined what it would be like to lose something precious in deep water, something far more precious than one's keys or phone. For small material objects, I suspect that there is little hope of preserving anything. And I have entertained the possibility that this is the case with everything: keys, phones, wallets and lives. Maybe everything just departs without a trace. Some philosophers would be perfectly happy with this explanation – that everything is in the process of passing away, that at the end of the cosmic day, nothing will be left. I am just not one of these philosophers. Neither was James. The certainty of this fatalism runs counter to his 'maybe' and counter to a hope that is, for me, hard to live without.

拿一个物体——一块小石头或一部手机——到一条浅河边。扔进去。在一个寂静的傍晚，当物体沉到河底时，涟漪仍在移动，仍在扩散。入水点的扰动最先消失，但事件的后果即使在其消散过程中仍呈同心圆状向外辐射。在一条狭窄、两岸陡峭的河流中，波浪拍击岸边，反弹回中心，再涌向对岸。这些微小的扰动是真实的，无论我们是否有能力感受到它们。有些东西留了下来。

Take an object – a small rock or a phone – to a shallow river. Throw it in. On a still evening, the ripples are still moving, still growing, when the object comes to rest on the bottom. The disruption at the point of entry is the first to vanish, but the consequences of the event radiate concentrically even as they dissipate. In a narrow river, with steep banks, the waves strike the shore, recoil to the centre, and make for the opposite side. The small perturbations are real, regardless of our ability to feel them. Something is left.

'我们的生命是一场对真理的学徒训练，即围绕每个圆还可以画出另一个圆，'Emerson在《圆》中写道。这篇文章发表五十年后，James完成了《心理学原理》(1890)，在其中发展了一种类似辐射球体的自我模型。中心是'物质自我'(material self)，即我们的身体和物质财富。这通常被视为我们生活中最具体的方面，但据James所言，它也是最表层的。我们通常愿意放弃物质财富来换取下一圈——他所说的'社会自我'(social self)，即从朋友、家人和爱人那里获得的认可。最后，James解释说，还有'精神自我'(spiritual self)，在'智识、道德和宗教的追求'中被寻求或体验的自我。这是自我中最广阔的方面，触及最远，但对许多人来说也是最微妙、最容易被忽视的。这就是那道即使未被完全察觉或表达也至关重要的波纹。

'Our life is an apprenticeship to the truth that around every circle another can be drawn,' wrote Emerson in 'Circles'. Fifty years after the essay was published, James finished the Principles of Psychology (1890), in which he developed a model of selfhood that resembled radiating spheres. At the centre was the 'material self', our bodies and material fortunes. This is frequently regarded as the most concrete aspect of our lives, but it is also, according to James, the most superficial. We would typically be willing to give up our material fortunes for the subsequent ring, what he terms 'the social self', the recognition that one gets from friends, family and loved ones. Finally, James explains, there is the 'spiritual self', one that is sought or experienced in 'intellectual, moral, and religious aspiration'. This is the most expansive aspect of selfhood, the farthest reaching, but also, for many of us, the most subtle and easily neglected. This is the wave that matters even when it is not fully detected or articulated.

在生命的最后十年，James继续捍卫人是万物尺度的立场。'我个人坚信，我们人类的经验并非宇宙中存在的最高形式的经验。'波纹向外扩散，拍击对岸，再轻轻折返。偶尔我们能感受到它们。在罕见的时刻，它们就是我们感受到的一切。按照James的说法，能够以任何规律性深切感受它们的人是非凡的。正是这种独特的个体在James于1901年在爱丁堡大学开展自然神学的吉福德讲座(Gifford Lectures)时占据了他大部分注意力，这一系列讲座后来成为次年出版的《宗教经验之种种》(Varieties of Religious Experience)。

In the final decade of his life, James continued to defend the position that man could be the measure of all things. 'I firmly disbelieve, myself, that our human experience is the highest form of experience extant in the Universe.' The waves ripple out, strike the opposite shore, and make their way back – gently. Occasionally we feel them. On rare occasions, they are all we feel. It is a remarkable person, according to James, who can sense them deeply with any regularity. It is this type of unique individual who occupied much of James's attention when he developed his Gifford Lectures in natural theology at the University of Edinburgh in 1901, a series of talks that became the Varieties of Religious Experience, published the following year.

James从来不是一个去教堂的人。在很大程度上，他对制度性宗教或精神自我的教义方面不感兴趣。一如既往，他感兴趣的是经验与生命，而在晚年他开始明确地转向思考两者的宗教可能性。他拒绝限制这些可能性，在《宗教经验之种种》中坚持说：'若要人以尽可能最宽泛、最一般的词语来概括宗教生活的特征，人们也许会说，宗教生活在于相信存在着一种不可见的秩序( unseen order)，我们的至善在于与之一致地调整自身。'

James was never a churchgoing man. For the most part, he wasn't interested in institutional religion or the doctrinal aspects of the spiritual self. He was, as always, interested in experience and life, and in his final years he began to turn explicitly to thinking through the religious possibilities of both. He refused to limit these possibilities, insisting in the Varieties: 'Were one asked to characterise the life of religion in the broadest and most general terms possible, one might say that it consists of the belief that there is an unseen order, and our supreme good lies in harmoniously adjusting ourselves thereto.'

这种对不可见秩序(unseen order)的调适可以采取多种形式，从不局限于特定的教堂、寺庙或清真寺。事实上，在撰写《宗教经验之种种》之前的准备过程中，James到处寻找它。他对不可见之物的探索将他带入致幻药物的实验，也带入了一个现代性常常斥为纯粹骗术的精神领域。如今，如果某样东西无法以完美的清晰度被看见，最简单的做法似乎就是断言它根本不可见。

This adjustment to the unseen order could take many forms and was never restricted to a particular church, temple or mosque. Indeed, James looked for it everywhere leading up to the writing of the Varieties. His exploration into the unseen carried him into experiments with psychotropic drugs but also into a spiritual realm that modernity often dismisses as mere quackery. Today, if something cannot be seen with perfect clarity, it seems easiest to assert that it cannot be seen at all.

当他的年迈父亲和刚出生的儿子在几年内相继去世时，James和妻子Alice试图与他们取得联系：1885年9月，James拜访了Leonora Piper，一位因据说能通灵而轰动波士顿的灵媒。他对Piper存有疑虑，但得出结论认为这位女性可能拥有他所谓的'超常能力'(supernormal powers)。James始终是一个彻头彻尾的经验主义者(empiricist)，并希望更仔细地检验这些能力。幸运的是，有一个新生的组织正是致力于此类研究——James于1885年参与创建了它。

When his aged father and newborn son died within a few years of each other, James and his wife Alice tried to contact them: in September 1885, James visited Leonora Piper, a medium who had become a Boston sensation for supposedly channelling spirits. He had his doubts about Piper but concluded that the woman might have what he called 'supernormal powers'. James was still, and always, the consummate empiricist, and wanted to test these powers more carefully. Luckily, there was a fledgling organisation dedicated to precisely this study – James co-founded it in 1885.

美国心灵研究学会(American Society for Psychical Research)的使命是调查一切'超常'(supernormal)事物。这不是什么边缘组织，但也不完全正常。其联合创始人之一G Stanley Hall曾在1870年代末来到哈佛跟随James攻读博士学位，并获得了美国第一个心理学博士学位。在James的支持下，Hall组织了一群研究者探索灵魂接触、探矿杖和心灵感应等事物的可能性。他们花费了数千小时（我没有夸张）采访神秘主义者和降神会的参与者。到1890年，Hall已退出该组织，结论是超心理学(parapsychology)无异于伪科学。但其他人，如James和他的密友、医生Henry P Bowditch，继续坚持到了世纪之交。1909年，James回顾了25年的捉鬼经历：'我承认，有时我曾倾向于相信，造物主永恒地打算让自然的这个部门保持令人困惑的状态，以同等程度地激发我们的好奇心、希望和疑心，因此，尽管鬼魂、千里眼、敲击声和来自灵魂的信息似乎总是存在、永远无法被完全解释消除，但它们也永远无法获得完全的证实。'

The mission of the American Society for Psychical Research was to investigate all things 'supernormal'. This was not some fringe organisation, but it was not altogether normal, either. One of its co-founders, G Stanley Hall, had come to Harvard to do doctoral work with James in the late 1870s and was awarded the first psychology doctorate in the United States. With James's support, Hall organised a group of researchers to explore the possibility of things such as spirit contact, divining rods and telepathy. They spent thousands of hours (I do not exaggerate) interviewing mystics and séance sitters. By 1890, Hall had resigned from the organisation, concluding that parapsychology amounted to pseudoscience. But others, like James and his close friend the physician Henry P Bowditch, marshalled on into the turn of the century. In 1909, James reflected on 25 years of ghostbusting: 'I confess that at times I have been tempted to believe that the creator has eternally intended this department of nature to remain baffling, to prompt our curiosities and hopes and suspicions, all in equal measure, so that although ghosts and clairvoyances and raps and messages from spirits are always seeming to exist and can never be fully explained away, they also can never be susceptible of full corroboration.'

自然喜欢隐藏。像James这样的人喜欢寻找。尽管充满困惑——或者也许正因为如此——James和他的同僚们始终明确地、虽然谨慎地保持着希望。然而，与当时大多数通灵者不同的是，心灵研究学会的成员记录并发表了他们的发现。那些结论都远非定论，但确实有助于拓展科学的边界，探索科学尚无法解释的领域。这份记录成为了面向成员及密切关联者的《心灵研究学会期刊》(Journal of the Society for Psychical Research)，以及面向公众的《会刊》(Proceedings)。这些卷帙浩繁的出版物总是令我惊叹：总计超过一万七千页。在好奇与疑虑之间的某个地方，是持久的希望。

Nature loves to hide. Humans like James love to seek. Despite the bafflement – or perhaps because of it – James and his fellow researchers remained pointedly, if cautiously, hopeful. Unlike most psychics of the time, however, the members of the psychical-research society documented and published their findings. None of those were anywhere near conclusive, but they did help to push the boundaries of science, exploring an area that science couldn't quite explain. This record became the Journal of the Society for Psychical Research, for members and close associates, and the Proceedings, intended for the general public. I'm always surprised by the sheer magnitude of the volumes: a little more than 17,000 pages in total. Somewhere between curiosity and suspicion was abiding hope.

当James开始他的心灵研究时，他已在生理学领域深耕多年。然而，解剖学家那种注重事实的客观方法在对人性的理解中遗漏了某些东西。对James来说，丢失了某种重要的东西：那种认为人不仅仅是一束感知和神经反应的集合，不仅仅是一具可能消失无踪的肉身的感觉。他希望存在某种空灵的、超越的东西——甚至可以说是幽灵般的东西——不受我们物质生活的束缚。在他一生的许多时刻，他暗示人们偶尔能感受到这'某种东西'萦绕在意识的边缘。直到1901年，James仍说：'我认真地相信，阈下(subliminal)的一般问题……有望成为心理学的重大问题之一，甚至可能是最大的问题。''阈下'(subliminal)一词常与'无意识'(unconscious)互换使用，但不应该如此。它指的是恰恰在意识阈值之下的心理过程，往往能被感受到却未完全浮现。只是一丝暗示，一个转瞬即逝的'也许'，就是我们得到的全部，但往往足以算作某种我们在至少一个瞬间所知晓的东西。这些经验的擦身之击位于James《宗教经验之种种》的核心——它们以多种形态出现，确实如此之多，以至于其存在不能被置之不理。

When James began his psychical research, he was well ensconced in the field of physiology. However, the anatomist's factual, objective method missed something in its understanding of human nature. For James, something important was lost: the sense that a human being is more than just a bundle of perceptions and nervous reactions, and more than just a body that could disappear without a trace. He hoped that there was something ethereal, transcendent – something even ghostly – that was free from the constraints of our physical lives. And at many times throughout his life, he suggested that one can occasionally feel this 'something' haunting the fringe of consciousness. As late as 1901, James remarked: 'I seriously believe that the general problem of the subliminal … promises to be one of the great problems, possibly even the greatest problem, of psychology.' 'Subliminal' is often used interchangeably with 'unconscious', but it shouldn't be. It refers, instead, to mental processes just below the threshold of consciousness that can often be felt without fully emerging. Just a hint, a fleeting 'maybe', is all we get, but it is often enough to qualify as something that we know, at least for a moment. These glancing blows of experience stand at the centre of James's Varieties – they come in many forms, indeed so many that their existence cannot be pushed aside.

美国法学家Oliver Wendell Holmes曾打趣说，James会把房间的灯光调暗，好让奇迹发生。我认为这话有一定道理。这有点像美国自助读物作者Wayne Dyer反复说的那句话：'奇迹降临于瞬间。要准备好，要愿意。'James确实总是准备好了，也总是愿意的。当你调暗灯光，瞳孔会放大以便更多光线进入。你不能为此责怪James。也许我们会为自己能看到的东西感到惊讶。也许那本身就是奇迹。'奇迹不是在水上行走，'佛教僧侣一行禅师(Thich Nhat Hanh)坚持说。'奇迹是在当下走在绿色的土地上，欣赏此刻即可获得的和平与美丽。'对于世俗的怀疑论者来说，在宗教经验方面，这可能就是他们愿意走到的最远处：深深地栖居，在当下'活出一点'。然而，James在《宗教经验之种种》中又往前走了一点点，往深处探了一点点。

The American jurist Oliver Wendell Holmes once joked that James would turn down the lights in a room so that the miracles could happen. I think that there is some truth in this. It is something like the American self-help author Wayne Dyer's oft-repeated quote: 'Miracles come in moments. Be ready and willing.' James was definitely always ready and willing. When you turn the lights down, your pupils dilate so that more light can get in. You can't blame James for this. Maybe we surprise ourselves with what we can see. And maybe that is miracle enough. 'The miracle is not to walk on water,' insists the Buddhist monk Thich Nhat Hanh. 'The miracle is to walk on the green earth in the present moment, to appreciate the peace and beauty that are available now.' For secular skeptics, this might be as far as they are ever willing to go when it comes to religious experience: to dwell deeply, to 'live a little' in the present. James, however, goes just a little farther, a little deeper, in the Varieties.

有时候，当你把灯光调得非常暗，你反而能把事物看得更清楚。James描述了这样一种现象，他声称这是唯一可被称为真正'神秘的'(mystical)现象。在复述一位牧师的'狂喜时刻'时，James写道：'夜晚完美的寂静被一种更庄严的沉默所震颤。黑暗中存在着一种临在，因为它未被看见而更加被感受到。我无法再怀疑他在那里，正如我无法怀疑我在这里。事实上，我感到自己——如果可能的话——是两者中较不真实的那个。'

Sometimes, when you turn the lights very low, you can see things more clearly. James describes such a phenomenon, the only one, he claimed, that could be called genuinely 'mystical'. Recounting the 'hour of rapture' of a clergyman, James writes: 'The perfect stillness of the night was thrilled by a more solemn silence. The darkness held a presence that was all the more felt because it was not seen. I could not any more have doubted that He was there than that I was. Indeed, I felt myself to be, if possible, the less real of the two.'

据这位牧师所说，那个'他'(He)无疑是犹太-基督教的上帝，但如何称呼这种临在对James来说几乎无关紧要。'他'是一个非常古老的词，比性别和性更古老，意思是'这个在这里'。'这个在这里'是存在的，因为它未被看见而更加被感受到。对James而言，对他的同道神秘主义者如Blood而言，这个故事中有一种持久的慰藉。正如德国神秘主义者Novalis所写：'我们与不可见之物的联系比与可见之物的联系更为紧密。'这也是一种可能性，而James式的实用主义者乐于考虑它。

The 'He', according to the clergyman, was undoubtedly the Judeo-Christian God, but what we call this presence scarcely mattered to James. 'He' is a very old word, older than gender and sex, meaning 'this here'. 'This here' was present, all the more felt, because it wasn't seen. For James, for his fellow mystics such as Blood, there was a sustained comfort in this story. As the German mystic Novalis wrote: 'We are more closely connected to the invisible than to the visible.' This too is a possibility, and the Jamesian pragmatist is happy to entertain it.

在布鲁克林大桥建成之前，渡船载着乘客从河的一边到另一边。Walt Whitman常常混迹于人群之中。这位美国诗人是James长久的英雄之一，是他在《宗教经验之种种》中描述的那种心胸开阔的'健康心灵'的化身。James偶尔在阿迪朗达克山脉的徒步中或在神秘主义者的见证中感受到崇高或宗教性，但Whitman能在日常生活中触及它，甚至在一次肮脏的渡船之旅中——大多数人会认为那不过是一段相当烦人的通勤。对Whitman来说并不烦人。在他的诗《横渡布鲁克林渡船》(1855)中，他描述了那番景象——对自然的体验和对人潮的体验。两者都是不可解释的，充满希望的，也是共享的。

Before the Brooklyn Bridge was built, a ferry carried passengers from one side of the river to the other. Walt Whitman was often among the crowd. The American poet was one of James's longstanding heroes, the embodiment of the capacious 'healthy mind' he describes in the Varieties. James occasionally sensed the sublime or the religious on his hikes in the Adirondacks or in the testament of mystics, but Whitman could tap into it on a routine basis, even on a dirty ferry ride, which most people would regard as a rather annoying commute. It wasn't annoying for Whitman. In his poem 'Crossing Brooklyn Ferry' (1855), he described the spectacle – the experience of nature and the experience of the human throng. Both were inexplicable and hopeful and shared.

James一遍又一遍地阅读这首诗。这就是惊奇，而且绰绰有余。事实证明，人们也许可以把心灵研究学会中那些疯狂的部分搁置一旁，而仍然保留一种Whitman式的对世界的体验——一次再平凡不过的渡船之旅中的神圣内在性(numinous immanence)。至少，这是James的希望。用James的话说，Whitman的视野足以'激发我们的好奇心、希望和疑心'。世界并不总是，或者说从来不是，它表面看起来的那个样子。一次肮脏的渡船之旅也许不仅仅是一次肮脏的渡船之旅。还有更多的东西——至少这是可能的。Whitman的体验是一种宗教体验——而且与大多数人体验世界的方式截然不同。在反思《横渡布鲁克林渡船》时，James解释道：'当一个普通的布鲁克林人或纽约人，过着过于奢侈的生活，或者因个人事务所疲倦烦恼，乘坐渡船或走在百老汇大街上时，他的想象力不会像Whitman那样"飞入日落的色彩"之中，他也不会在内心里真正意识到这样一个不容置疑的事实：这个世界在任何地方、任何时代所包含的本质神性或永恒意义，从未超过他双眼如此漫不经心地掠过的那片视野中所蕴含的。'

James read and reread this poem. This was wonder, and there was enough of it to go around. It turns out that one can probably set aside the nuttier aspects of the Society for Psychical Research and still retain a Whitman-esque experience of the world, the numinous immanence of an all-too-human ferry ride. That, at least, was James's hope. Whitman's vision, in James's words, was sufficient 'to prompt our curiosities and hopes and suspicions'. The world is not always, or ever, exactly as it seems. A dirty ferry ride might be more than just a dirty ferry ride. There is something more – at least it is possible. Whitman's was a type of religious experience – and so very different from the way that most people experience the world. Reflecting on 'Crossing Brooklyn Ferry', James explained: 'When your ordinary Brooklynite or New Yorker, leading a life replete with too much luxury, or tired and careworn, about his personal affairs, crosses the ferry or goes up Broadway, his fancy does not thus "soar away into the colours of the sunset" as did Whitman's, nor does he inwardly realise at all the indisputable fact that this world never did anywhere or at any time contain more of essential divinity, or of eternal meaning, than is embodied in the fields of vision over which his eyes so carelessly pass.'

然而，人不必漫不经心。值得庆幸的是，还有其他方式度过时光，还有其他时光可以消逝。潮涨与潮落继续涌来退去。James暗示，即使对一个实用主义者来说，也有可能偶尔感受到其水流令人安心的循环。在这些时刻，人有机会以James所定义的'宗教的'方式存在，进入'一种为宗教人士所知、其他人所不知的心境，在这种心境中，坚持自我、维护自身的意志已被一种意愿所取代——闭上嘴巴，在上帝的洪水和水柱中化为虚无。在这种心境中，我们最惧怕的东西成了我们安全的居所……'

However, one does not have to be careless. Thankfully there are other ways to pass the time and other times to pass away. The flood and the ebb continue to go out and come in. And James suggests that it is possible, even for a pragmatist, to occasionally feel the reassuring cycle of its flow. At these moments, one has a chance to be 'religious' in James's sense of the word, to enter 'a state of mind, known to religious men, but to no others, in which the will to assert ourselves and hold our own has been displaced by a willingness to close our mouths and be as nothing in the floods and waterspouts of God. In this state of mind, what we most dreaded has become the habitation of our safety …'

我再次望向自由女神像，然后低头望向下方的水面。太阳确实正在落下去，我试着让自己观看它——正如Whitman和James所希望的那样——仿佛过了许多分钟。刚好足够长，让我庆幸自己仍然拥有这个机会。

I looked out to the Statue of Liberty again, and back down into the water below. The sun was indeed setting, and I tried to let myself watch it, as Whitman and James hoped we would, for what seemed like many minutes. Just long enough to be glad that I still had the chance.
