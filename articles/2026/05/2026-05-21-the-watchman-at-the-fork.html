岔路口的守望者

AI Parable (inspired by Amanda Askell)·每日精选·2026年5月21日·5分钟阅读

群山之间曾有一道狭窄的峡谷，贯穿其中的唯一一条道路连接着谷中六座村庄与外面的世界。在峡谷最深处，道路分成了两岔。左边的岔路笔直延伸约四分之一里后，通向谷中最大市镇的中央集场，那里每天清晨都有一百来人熙来攘往。右边的岔路在一面花岗岩壁肩处急转，尽头是一座小采石场，大多数日子里，只有一个名叫哈尔多（Haldor）的老石匠坐在那里敲凿石灰石，几十年的锤击使他半耳失聪，对下一锤之外的世界毫无知觉。

There was once a narrow gorge carved through the mountains, and through it ran the only road connecting the valley's six villages to the rest of the world. At the gorge's deepest point, the path split. The left branch ran straight and true for a quarter mile before arriving at the great market square of the valley's largest town, where a hundred people bustled on any given morning. The right branch curved sharply around a granite shoulder and ended at a small quarry where, on most days, a single old stonecutter named Haldor sat chipping away at blocks of limestone, half deaf from decades of hammering and wholly unaware of anything beyond the next strike of his chisel.

岔路口由路边一间石砌小屋中的一根拉杆控制。沉重的铁舌扳向一侧时，所有车辆行人都会被导向左边的岔路；扳向另一侧时，则将一切——马车、马匹、行人——送入弯道，直抵采石场。在所有人的记忆中，拉杆始终被锁在向左的位置，右边的路轨上早已长满了野百里香和三叶草。

The fork was controlled by a lever housed in a small stone hut beside the road. A heavy iron tongue, when thrown to one side, guided all traffic onto the left branch; when thrown to the other, it sent everything — carts, horses, people — around the curve and into the quarry. For as long as anyone could remember, the lever had been kept locked in the leftward position, and the rightward track had been allowed to grow over with wild thyme and clover.

看守拉杆的是一位名叫桑娜（Sanna）的女子。她担任此职已有二十三年，其间从未将铁舌从原位移开过一次。她理解的职责是守望岔路口，确保通往集场的道路始终畅通。她保持小屋整洁，拉杆润滑，火盆上的茶水始终温热。旅人有时会停下来歇脚，她会递上一杯茶和一块硬面包，他们会告诉她峡谷那边各村的新闻。她心满意足。她相信自己做的一切不伤人、不害物，还有些许微善。

The keeper of the lever was a woman named Sanna. She had held the post for twenty-three years, and in all that time she had never once moved the iron tongue from its resting place. Her job, as she understood it, was to watch the fork and ensure that the road remained open to the market town. She kept the hut swept, the lever oiled, and a pot of tea always warm on the brazier. Travelers would sometimes stop to rest, and she would offer them a cup and a piece of hard bread, and they would tell her news of the villages beyond the gorge. She was content. She was, she believed, doing no harm and some modest good.

深秋的一个清晨，一辆满载铁矿石的马车从峡谷上方的一支矿队处脱缰。车夫跳车逃开，但马车在陡坡上越冲越快，铁箍车轮在峡谷石壁间发出刺耳的尖啸。等到桑娜听到那声音——像雷声卡在喉咙里——已经来不及了，马车根本无法停住，正对着岔路口直冲而来。

On a morning in late autumn, a cart loaded with iron ore broke free from a mining team higher up the gorge. The driver leapt clear, but the cart gathered speed as it plunged down the steep incline, its iron-rimmed wheels screaming against the stone walls of the gorge. By the time Sanna heard it — a sound like thunder trapped in a throat — it was already too close to stop, and it was aimed squarely at the fork.

桑娜走出小屋，看见了那辆马车。她也看见铁舌仍锁在向左的位置。马车将沿着左边的岔路冲下去，以全速冲入集场。它会杀人——不是一个两个，而是许多。她站在那里看不见集场，但她能听到远处早晨人群的嗡嗡声，她以一种仿佛巨石落入胃中的确信知道，此刻那里有几十个人在买面包、在为羊毛的价格争辩、把孩子扛到肩上去看赶集日才来的杂耍艺人。

Sanna stepped out of her hut and saw it. She saw, too, that the iron tongue was still locked leftward. The cart would follow the left branch. It would enter the market square at full speed. It would kill people — not one or two, but many. She could not see the square from where she stood, but she could hear the distant murmur of the morning crowd, and she knew with a certainty that felt like a stone dropped into her stomach that dozens of people were there right now, buying bread, arguing over the price of wool, lifting children onto their shoulders to see the juggler who came on market days.

她看了看拉杆。她可以把它扳向右侧。马车会绕过花岗岩壁肩，冲入采石场。集场上的人会平安无事。但是哈尔多——年迈的、失聪的、沉浸在自己世界里的哈尔多，他从未伤害过任何人，他听不见马车朝他冲来，正如他听不见一句耳语——哈尔多会死。

She looked at the lever. She could throw it to the right. The cart would swing around the granite shoulder and plunge into the quarry. The people in the market square would be safe. But Haldor — old, deaf, absorbed Haldor, who had never done harm to anyone and who could not hear a cart bearing down on him any more than he could hear a whisper — Haldor would die.

她大约有四秒钟的时间。马车已经近到她能看见矿砂在车斗中滑动，能听见一颗颗石子撞击木壁的声响。铁舌虽重却并非扳不动——毕竟她一直在给它上油。她需要双手和全身的重量才能将它扳过去。

She had perhaps four seconds. The cart was close enough now that she could see the ore shifting in its bed, could hear the individual stones clattering against the wooden sides. The iron tongue was stiff but not immovable — she had kept it oiled, after all. She would need both hands and the full weight of her body to throw it.

桑娜没有动。

Sanna did not move.

马车轰鸣着掠过她的小屋，沿着左边的路轨消失了，冲向集场。她听到了撞击声。她听到了尖叫声。她双手垂在身体两侧，一动不动地站着，把一切都听在了耳中。

The cart thundered past her hut, followed the leftward track, and disappeared toward the market square. She heard the impact. She heard the screaming. She stood perfectly still with her hands at her sides, and she heard all of it.

治安官来的时候，问她为什么没有扳动拉杆。她说了实话：她考虑过。她知道扳动拉杆能拯救许多人的性命。她知道不扳就会酿成大祸。然后她选择了不扳。

When the magistrates came, they asked her why she had not thrown the lever. She told them the truth: she had considered it. She had known that throwing the lever would save many lives. She had known that not throwing it would mean catastrophe. And she had chosen not to throw it.

他们问她为什么。

They asked her why.

她说：「因为那辆马车不是由我改变方向的。我没有放开缰绳，我没有将它对准任何人。在我知道它存在之前，它就已经朝着那些人冲去了。如果我扳动了拉杆，我就会夺走一条性命——哈尔多的性命——而那条性命本来从未受到马车的威胁。我会把我没有造成的悲剧变成一次蓄意的杀戮。我会选择了谁该死。而我没有权利做出这个选择。」

She said: "Because the cart was not mine to redirect. I did not loose it. I did not aim it. It was already traveling toward those people before I ever knew it existed. If I had thrown the lever, I would have taken a life — Haldor's life — that was never in danger from the cart. I would have turned a tragedy I did not cause into a deliberate act of killing. I would have chosen who died. And I did not have the right to make that choice."

治安官们并不满意。整个山谷也不满意。而桑娜，如果在此后的岁月里她对自身诚实的话，她自己也不满意。

The magistrates were not satisfied. Neither was the valley. Neither, if she was honest with herself in the years that followed, was Sanna.

但她在那四秒中面对的问题，不是满意或不满意所能回答的。这是一个关于「作为」与「不作为」意味着什么的问题。如果你将致命的力量从许多人身上引向一个人，你是在拯救生命，还是在夺走生命？如果你在灾难发生时袖手旁观、什么也不做，你因为你不是灾难的起因而无辜，还是因为你本可以阻止它而成了共犯？杀人与任其死亡之间存在道德区别吗？如果存在，需要有多少生命面临威胁，这个区别才会崩塌？

But the question she had faced in those four seconds could not be answered by satisfaction or dissatisfaction. It was a question about what it means to act versus what it means to allow. If you redirect a deadly force from many people onto one person, have you saved lives or taken a life? If you stand aside and do nothing while disaster unfolds, are you innocent because you did not cause it, or are you complicit because you could have prevented it? Is there a moral difference between killing and letting die? And if there is, how many lives must be at stake before that difference collapses?

整个山谷就桑娜的决定争论了多年。有人称她为懦夫。有人称她为哲人。少数人称她为杀人者——不是因为她的所作所为，而是因为她的不作为。还有人说她是谷中唯一诚实的人，因为她拒绝将一个可怕的选择粉饰为英雄之举。哈尔多本人得知事情原委后，前去探望她。他坐在她的小屋里，喝着她的茶，沉默了很久。然后他问：「如果你扳了拉杆，你能与自己和解吗？」桑娜说：「我不知道。」哈尔多点了点头。「我也不知道，」他说，「但我很高兴我不用去知道。」

The valley argued about Sanna's decision for years. Some called her a coward. Some called her a philosopher. A few called her a murderer — not for what she had done, but for what she had not done. Others said she was the only honest person in the valley, because she had refused to dress up a terrible choice as heroism. Haldor himself, when he learned what had happened, went to visit her. He sat in her hut, drank her tea, and said nothing for a long time. Then he asked: "If you had thrown the lever, would you have been able to live with yourself?" Sanna said, "I don't know." Haldor nodded. "I don't know either," he said. "But I'm glad I don't have to find out."

多年以后，首都大学的一位哲学家听说了这个故事并将其记录下来，只改动了细节：失控的马车变成了一辆失控的电车（tram），峡谷中的岔路变成了轨道的道岔，拉杆变成了切换开关。他不再将这当作一个关于桑娜的故事来讲述，而是作为一个问题：你站在一个开关旁。一辆失控的电车正冲向被绑在主轨道上的五个人。你可以扳动开关，将电车引向只有一个人被绑的侧轨。你扳不扳？他将其称为「电车难题」（Trolley Problem），它后来成为现代道德哲学中被讨论最广泛的思想实验之一，由菲利帕·福特（Philippa Foot）于1967年提出，后由朱迪斯·贾维斯·汤姆森（Judith Jarvis Thomson）进一步拓展。

Years later, a philosopher from the university in the capital heard the story and wrote it down, changing only the details: a runaway cart became a runaway tram, the fork in the gorge became a fork in the tracks, the lever became a switch. He posed it not as a story about Sanna but as a question: you are standing at a switch. A runaway tram is heading toward five people tied to the main track. You can throw the switch, diverting the tram onto a side track where only one person is tied. Do you throw it? He called it the Trolley Problem, and it became one of the most widely discussed thought experiments in modern moral philosophy, introduced by Philippa Foot in 1967 and later expanded by Judith Jarvis Thomson.

电车难题实际上并非关于电车。它关乎两种强大道德直觉之间的张力。第一种直觉是功利主义（utilitarian）的：我们应当将伤害降至最低，以一条命换五条命意味着净增四条命——任何理性的人都应该扳动开关。第二种直觉是义务论（deontological）的：任由伤害发生与主动造成伤害之间存在根本区别，把一个人当作拯救他人的手段，是将那个人仅仅当作了工具而非目的本身。令人困惑的是，几乎所有人都会同时感受到这两种直觉，而它们指向截然相反的方向。

The Trolley Problem is not really about trolleys. It is about the tension between two powerful moral intuitions. The first intuition is utilitarian: we should minimize harm, and saving five lives at the cost of one is a net gain of four lives — any rational person should throw the switch. The second intuition is deontological: there is a categorical difference between allowing harm to happen and actively causing it, and using one person as a means to save others treats that person as a mere instrument rather than an end in themselves. The puzzle is that almost everyone feels both intuitions simultaneously, and they point in opposite directions.

该问题的各种变体探究的是我们的直觉在哪里断裂。在「天桥」版本中，你必须将一个大个子从桥上推下去挡住电车——大多数人会拒绝，尽管算术结果完全相同。在「环线」版本中，侧轨会绕回主轨，那一个人的身体才是真正挡住电车的东西——人们变得犹豫。在「器官移植」版本中，外科医生可以摘取一个健康人的器官来拯救五名患者——几乎所有人都会退缩。数字始终不变：一比五。但道德质感会随着你的参与程度、你是在转移已有的威胁还是在制造新的威胁、以及那一个人的死亡是副作用还是被蓄意选择的手段而发生变化。

Variations of the problem probe where our intuitions break. In the "footbridge" version, you must push a large person off a bridge to stop the tram — most people refuse, even though the arithmetic is identical. In the "loop" version, the side track loops back to the main track, and the one person's body is what stops the tram — people become hesitant. In the "transplant" version, a surgeon could harvest one healthy person's organs to save five patients — nearly everyone recoils. The numbers are always the same: one versus five. But the moral texture changes depending on how directly you are involved, whether you are diverting an existing threat or creating a new one, and whether the one person's death is a side effect or a deliberately chosen means.

电车难题最终揭示的不是正确答案，而是我们道德推理本身的形状。它表明我们并不只凭后果来评估行为——如果是的话，每个版本都会同样一目了然。它表明我们也不只凭意图来评估行为——如果是的话，作为与不作为之间的区别将毫无分量。两者兼而有之，纠缠在一起，而那些萦绕在我们心头、挥之不去的案例，恰恰是两种评估方法产生了不可调和之结论的情形。桑娜在岔路口站了四秒钟，而我们其余的人则要站一辈子。

What the Trolley Problem ultimately reveals is not the correct answer but the shape of our moral reasoning itself. It shows that we do not evaluate actions solely by their consequences — if we did, every version would be equally obvious. It shows that we do not evaluate actions solely by their intentions — if we did, the distinction between acting and omitting would carry no weight. We do both, tangled together, and the cases that haunt us are precisely the ones where the two methods of evaluation produce irreconcilable verdicts. Sanna stood at the fork for four seconds, but the rest of us stand there for a lifetime.
