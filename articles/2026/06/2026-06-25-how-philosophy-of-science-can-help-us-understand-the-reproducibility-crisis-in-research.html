科学哲学如何帮助我们理解研究的可重复性危机

Hasok Chang·The Conversation·2026年6月25日·3分钟阅读·原文

近十年来，科学界面临一个令人不安的现实：大量发表在顶级期刊上的研究发现，当其他研究者试图重复这些实验时，却无法得到相同的结果。从心理学到医学，从生物学到社会科学，这一现象如此广泛，以至于被冠以"可重复性危机"的名称。很多人最初将此归咎于统计方法的滥用——p值操纵、样本量不足、选择性报告数据。但如果我们追问更深层的问题：为什么科学家会系统地倾向于产生不可重复的发现？答案指向的不是单纯的技术失误，而是关于我们如何理解证实、证据和科学方法论的哲学问题。

Over the past decade, the scientific community has faced an unsettling reality: a large proportion of research findings published in top-tier journals cannot be replicated when other researchers attempt to reproduce the same experiments. From psychology to medicine, from biology to social science, this phenomenon has been so widespread that it has earned the name "reproducibility crisis." Many initially blamed statistical abuse—p-hacking, inadequate sample sizes, selective data reporting. But if we ask the deeper question: why do scientists systematically tend to produce findings that cannot be replicated? The answer points not to mere technical failures, but to philosophical questions about how we understand confirmation, evidence, and scientific methodology.

科学哲学——这个听起来似乎与实验室毫无关系的领域——恰恰为我们理解可重复性危机提供了最犀利的工具。哲学不只是在抽象层面上讨论"真理"和"知识"，它也在追问科学实践的基础假设：什么算作证据？一次观察能否证实一个理论？什么样的方法论能够最大限度地减少偏见？这些问题不是科学本身能够回答的，因为它们涉及科学赖以运作的前提。

Philosophy of science—a field that might seem utterly disconnected from the laboratory—actually provides the sharpest tools for understanding the reproducibility crisis. Philosophy does not merely discuss "truth" and "knowledge" at an abstract level; it interrogates the foundational assumptions of scientific practice: what counts as evidence? Can a single observation confirm a theory? What kind of methodology can minimize bias to the greatest extent? These are questions that science itself cannot answer, because they concern the premises on which science operates.

卡尔·波普尔的证伪主义是科学哲学对可重复性危机最直接的回应之一。波普尔提出，科学理论的标志不在于它能被多少次证实，而在于它是否可以被证伪——即是否存在一种可能的观察结果，如果出现，将推翻该理论。一个理论越是精确、越是敢于承担被证伪的风险，它就越具有科学价值。换言之，好的科学不是去寻找支持自己假设的证据，而是主动寻找可能推翻自己假设的证据。

Karl Popper's falsificationism is one of philosophy of science's most direct responses to the reproducibility crisis. Popper proposed that the hallmark of a scientific theory is not how many times it can be confirmed, but whether it can be falsified—that is, whether there exists a possible observation that, if it occurred, would overturn the theory. The more precise a theory and the more willing it is to risk falsification, the greater its scientific value. In other words, good science does not seek evidence supporting one's hypothesis; it actively seeks evidence that might推翻 it.

然而，可重复性危机恰恰暴露了科学实践中对波普尔原则的系统性背离。研究者往往不是试图证伪自己的假设，而是寻找证实它的证据——这种行为被称为"确证偏见"。确证偏见不只是一种心理缺陷，它更是科学文化的结构性产物：期刊偏好发表"新颖"和"显著"的结果，资助机构青睐有正面发现的项目，学者的职业生涯取决于发表高影响因子论文的数量。在这样的激励机制下，科学家自然而然地倾向于追求证实而非证伪。

Yet the reproducibility crisis precisely exposes a systematic departure from Popper's principle in scientific practice. Researchers often do not try to falsify their own hypotheses; instead, they seek evidence that confirms them—a behavior termed "confirmation bias." Confirmation bias is not merely a psychological flaw; it is a structural product of scientific culture: journals prefer to publish "novel" and "significant" results, funding agencies favor projects with positive findings, and scholars' careers depend on the number of high-impact-factor publications they produce. Under such incentive structures, scientists naturally gravitate toward seeking confirmation rather than falsification.

确证偏见的问题远比"科学家不够客观"这一描述所暗示的更深。它揭示了一个哲学层面的事实：证实与证伪之间的不对称性。波普尔指出，无论多少次正面观察都无法最终证实一个普遍性命题，但一次反面观察就足以证伪它。一千次实验支持某个理论，不意味着该理论为真；但一次实验与理论预测相矛盾，就意味着该理论至少在某个方面是错误的。这种不对称性是逻辑的基本结构，但科学实践却常常无视它。

The problem of confirmation bias goes far deeper than the description "scientists are not objective enough" would suggest. It reveals a philosophical fact: the asymmetry between confirmation and falsification. Popper pointed out that no matter how many positive observations accumulate, they cannot definitively confirm a universal proposition, but a single negative observation is sufficient to falsify it. A thousand experiments supporting a theory do not mean the theory is true; but one experiment contradicting the theory's prediction means the theory is wrong in at least some respect. This asymmetry is a basic structure of logic, yet scientific practice often ignores it.

更深层的问题在于"不充分决定性"——同一组数据可以被多种不同理论所解释。这意味着即使一项实验结果看起来"证实"了某个假设，它实际上也可能同样"证实"若干其他假设。科学家选择了其中一个理论来解释数据，这种选择并非纯粹由数据驱动，其中还涉及理论的美学偏好、研究传统、甚至社会因素。不充分决定性提醒我们：科学发现不是从数据到理论的唯一路径，而是从数据到理论的多条可能路径中的一条。

A deeper issue lies in "underdetermination"—the same set of data can be explained by multiple different theories. This means that even when an experimental result appears to "confirm" a hypothesis, it may equally "confirm" several other hypotheses. Scientists choose one theory among these to explain the data, and this choice is not purely data-driven; it also involves aesthetic preferences for theories, research traditions, and even social factors. Underdetermination reminds us: a scientific finding is not the unique path from data to theory, but merely one of several possible paths from data to theory.

可重复性危机也暴露了"证实"概念的模糊性。在日常语言中，"证实"似乎意味着"证明为真"，但在科学哲学中，证实是一个远比这更复杂的概念。亨佩尔的乌鸦悖论揭示了这一点："所有乌鸦都是黑色的"这一命题，在逻辑上被任何黑色的乌鸦所"证实"，但也被任何非黑色的非乌鸦所"证实"——比如一个白色的鞋子也"证实"了"所有乌鸦都是黑色的"。这种荒谬的结果表明，我们对"证实"的直觉理解存在根本性的问题。

The reproducibility crisis also exposes the ambiguity of the concept of "confirmation." In everyday language, "confirm" seems to mean "prove true," but in philosophy of science, confirmation is a far more complex concept. Hempel's raven paradox reveals this: the proposition "all ravens are black" is logically "confirmed" by any black raven, but also by any non-black non-raven—a white shoe, for instance, also "confirms" that "all ravens are black." This absurd result shows that our intuitive understanding of "confirmation" has fundamental problems.

面对可重复性危机，科学界的应对主要集中在技术层面：更大样本量、 preregistration（预先登记研究方案）、更严格的统计标准。这些措施固然必要，但它们只解决了问题的表层。科学哲学提醒我们，真正的改进需要触及更深层面：改变科学的激励机制，让证伪与证实获得同等的学术价值；承认科学知识的不确定性，而不是将每一个"显著结果"等同于真理的发现；鼓励方法论多元主义，让不同的研究路径能够相互检验。

In response to the reproducibility crisis, the scientific community's remedies have focused mainly on the technical level: larger sample sizes, preregistration of research protocols, stricter statistical standards. These measures are necessary, but they address only the surface of the problem. Philosophy of science reminds us that genuine improvement must reach deeper: changing science's incentive structures so that falsification receives equal academic value to confirmation; acknowledging the uncertainty of scientific knowledge rather than treating every "significant result" as the discovery of truth; encouraging methodological pluralism so that different research paths can cross-check one another.

剑桥大学科学哲学家张夏硕提出了"活跃科学"的概念，主张科学家不应将不可重复视为失败，而应将其视为新发现的起点。当实验无法重复时，这意味着我们遗漏了某些变量、某些条件、某些机制——这些遗漏本身就是知识的缺口。与其简单地宣布原始研究"错误"，不如追问：为什么在不同条件下结果不同？这种追问往往能揭示更深层、更复杂的科学真相。

Hasok Chang, a philosopher of science at the University of Cambridge, has proposed the concept of "active science," arguing that scientists should not treat non-reproducibility as failure but rather as the starting point for new discovery. When an experiment cannot be replicated, it means we have missed certain variables, conditions, or mechanisms—and these omissions are themselves gaps in knowledge. Rather than simply declaring the original study "wrong," we should ask: why do results differ under different conditions? This kind of questioning often reveals deeper, more complex scientific truths.

科学哲学最终提供的不是一种解决方案，而是一种反思的姿态。它要求我们在面对"科学发现"时保持一种哲学性的警觉：任何单一实验都不是真理的终审法庭，任何统计显著性都不是知识的最终担保。可重复性危机之所以令人不安，恰恰因为它动摇了我们对科学的朴素信任——但科学哲学告诉我们，这种动摇本身可能是科学走向成熟的标志。真正的科学精神不在于确信自己已经找到真理，而在于始终保持对自身方法论的批判性反思。

Philosophy of science ultimately provides not a solution but a stance of reflection. It demands that we maintain a philosophical vigilance when confronting "scientific findings": no single experiment is the final court of truth, and no statistical significance is the ultimate guarantee of knowledge. The reproducibility crisis is unsettling precisely because it shakes our naïve trust in science—but philosophy of science tells us that this shaking may itself be a sign of science maturing. The true spirit of science lies not in the conviction that one has found truth, but in maintaining critical reflection on one's own methodology at all times.
