哲学/教育学

「你想谈谈一个充满谎言的世界？」——在监狱中教授哲学

Jay Miller·The Guardian·2026年6月5日·13分钟阅读·原文

早上八点半整，一辆白色面包车驶到北卡罗来纳州（North Carolina）的大学校园，局外人（Outsiders）们穿着他们的黑色衬衫聚在一起，睡眼惺忪但精神不错。他们迅速上车，知道要赶一个紧凑的日程。从校园出发十分钟后，面包车停在一座 topped with razor wire 的大型金属拱门下。

At 8.30am sharp, a white van pulls up to the North Carolina college campus where the Outsiders are huddled in their black shirts, sleepy-faced but in good spirits. They pile in quickly, knowing there is a tight schedule to stick to. A 10-minute drive from campus, then the van pulls up under the arch of a large metal gate crowned with razor wire.

到八点四十五分，局外人站成一排，把随身物品放进塑料箱，等待不苟言笑的狱警拍身搜查和翻检物品。他们在检查：所有手机是否都安全锁在车里？司机是否已在前台交了钥匙？局外人对这套流程了然于心。他们知道服装应该中性、得体。他们知道身份证和访客卡应该拿出来备查，包要打开接受检查。金属探测器每响一声，所有人都会紧张，事情就会慢下来。局外人知道一切必须顺利进行，这样才能在九点整准时到达主楼的209室，另一位不苟言笑的狱警正在不耐烦地等着为我们开门。和他在一起的是一群穿着各种深浅蓝色制服的女性。我们称她们为内部人（Insiders）。在这里，她们被称为「offenders」。

By 8.45am, the Outsiders are standing in line, placing their possessions in plastic bins and waiting for the no-nonsense guards to pat them down and rifle through their things. They're checking: are all cellphones securely locked in the van? Has the driver checked in their keys at the front desk? The Outsiders know the drill. They know that their clothing should be neutral and moderate. They know that IDs and visitor cards should be out and ready, bags open for inspection. Every beep of the metal detector makes everyone go tense, and slows things down. The Outsiders know that everything needs to go smoothly so that at 9am sharp we can make it to Room 209 of the main building where another no-nonsense guard is waiting impatiently to let us in. With him is a group of women in uniforms of various shades of blue. We know them as the Insiders. In here, they are known as the "offenders".

内部人和局外人混坐在一起。课程开始。

Insiders and Outsiders intermingle and take their seats. Class begins.

我们已经进入这个名为「哲学导论」、听起来平平无奇的学期课程好几周了。这门课每周五上午在大学附近的一所女子矫正机构上三个小时，是美国全国性的内部-外部监狱交流项目（Inside-Out Prison Exchange Program）的一部分。课程共有20名学生。一半是「外部」学生——主要是我任教的那所小型文理学院里19到20岁的住宿生。另一半是「内部」学生，年龄、背景和生活经历的范围广泛得多。今天有17人出席。我们得到一个悲喜交加的消息：肖娜（Shauna）已提前获释。黛比（Debbie）来不了，因为她的牢房正在被搜查违禁品。迈克尔（Michael）得了流感。

We are several weeks into the semester-long course, innocuously titled Introduction to Philosophy. The class, held each Friday morning for three hours at a women's correctional facility near the college, is part of the US national Inside-Out Prison Exchange Program. There are 20 students on the course. Half are "Outside" students – that is, mostly 19- to 20-year-old residential students at the small liberal arts college where I teach. The other half are "Inside" students with a much broader range of age, background and life experience. Today, 17 are in attendance. We get the sad-but-happy news that Shauna has been released early. Debbie can't make it because her cell is being searched for contraband. Michael has flu.

从一开始，每次课都以一轮「傻袜子」开场。这个主意是学生们自己想出来的。有趣的是，你可以费尽心思在这里营造公平感，承认和应对那些定义这个空间的明显且不可避免的差异。内部人在这里没有手机、没有笔记本电脑、没有互联网，所以局外人也不带。局外人有答疑时间和辅导等资源，内部人也应该有。这些印有内部-外部监狱交流项目标志的黑色T恤，一部分作为局外人的制服以表达与内部人的团结，一部分也是满足监狱着装要求的简单方式。但真正把我们团结在一起的是傻袜子——一个由学生发起的每周仪式，展示你最大声、最傻、最疯狂的袜子，让学生们做回学生。这周，距离完成因驾车过失杀人而被判15年刑期还差一年的詹恩（Jenn），凭借她自己编织的饰有小猫脸的亮色袜子获得了最佳展示奖。

Since the beginning, each class has started with a round of "silly socks". The students came up with this on their own. It's funny, you can try so hard to achieve a sense of equity here, to acknowledge and address the obvious and unavoidable differences that define this space. Insiders have no phones, no laptops, no internet service here, so Outsiders don't either. Outsiders have access to resources such as office hours and tutoring, so Insiders should too. These black T-shirts with the Inside-Out Prison Exchange Program logo are worn in part as Outsider uniforms in solidarity with Insiders, in part as a simple way of satisfying the dress-code requirements within the prison. But silly socks is what really brings us together – a student-led weekly ritual of showing off the loudest, silliest, craziest socks you've got, which lets the students be students. This week, Jenn, who is one year shy of completing a 15-year sentence for vehicular homicide, is Best in Show for the bright socks embellished with kitten faces she knitted herself.

上周，我们开始讨论柏拉图（Plato）的《理想国》（Republic）——尽管学生们当时并不知道。事实上，就在上周这个时候，许多人甚至从未听说过柏拉图，更不用说洞穴比喻（Allegory of the Cave）了。在洞穴比喻中，柏拉图将我们比作被铁链锁住脖子、困在洞穴深处的囚徒，他们尚不理解现实不仅仅是投射在墙上的影子。或者说，教育是一种解放的力量，使我们能够感知影子之外的现实——尽管真理的光芒起初可能令人痛苦。

Last week, we began discussing Plato's Republic – though the students didn't realise it at the time. In fact, this time last week, many had never even heard of Plato, let alone the Allegory of the Cave. In the Allegory of the Cave, Plato compares us to captives who are chained by the neck deep within a cave, who do not yet understand that there is more to reality than the shadows they see projected on the wall. Or that education is the liberating force that allows us to perceive a reality beyond the shadows – painful though the light of truth may be at first.

作为一项原则，在任何文本、讲授或作业出现之前，我们先做哲学。我们总是从讨论开始，讨论总是从一个简单的问题开始。上周的问题是：「如果你所知道的一切都是谎言呢？」在没有提及任何听起来吓人的词汇如「形而上学」或「认识论」的情况下，学生们已经在做哲学了。局外人抱怨说，媒体尤其是社交媒体扭曲了一切，让一切显得不真实。一些内部人既好奇又困惑，她们听说过Facebook、Instagram和TikTok但从未用过。几位学生进一步展开这一思路，认为其他东西也在扭曲真相，无论是历史、资本主义，还是他们在各种大学课程中最近学到的其他主题。

As a matter of principle, before any text, lecture or assignment enters the picture, we start doing philosophy. We always start with discussion, and discussion always begins with a simple question. Last week, the question was: "What if everything you ever knew was a lie?" Without any mention of any scary-sounding words such as "metaphysics" or "epistemology", the students were doing philosophy. The Outsiders complained that the media, especially social media, twists everything, makes everything seem not real. Some of the Insiders were intrigued and perplexed, having heard of Facebook, Instagram and TikTok but never used them. Several students expanded on this thinking, arguing that other things twisted the truth too, whether it was history, or capitalism, or other subjects they'd recently learned about in various college classes.

「哦嗬嗬嗬，你想谈谈一个充满谎言的世界？让我来告诉你什么是谎言，」杰斯（Jess）插话道。「试试被一个警察诬告重罪。」这样的讨论持续了一个多小时，每位学生都在分享自己对洞穴里到底发生了什么的版本。到了正午，囚犯被叫去食堂。内部学生匆忙赶上狭窄的午餐时间窗口。我们离开时一致认为谎言无处不在，但仍然不确定这是否意味着一切都是谎言。

"Ohhhh-ho-ho, you want to talk about a world of lies? Let me tell you about lies," Jess jumps in. "Try getting stuck with a felony charge against a cop." It goes on like this for more than an hour, each student sharing their own version of what the hell is happening in the cave. At noon sharp, the inmates are called to the mess hall. Inside students scramble to catch the narrow lunchtime window. We depart, agreeing that lies are everywhere, but still not sure if that means everything is a lie.

这周，我们已经读了柏拉图《理想国》第七卷，准备好讨论了。在过去的几周里，我观察到内部学生提高了课程准备的标杆。她们每周带着我前一周留给她们的印刷文本出现，认真做笔记，而且总是把写作作业拿出来准备讨论（其中许多是手写的，因为对内部学生来说打字和打印作业可能很费事）。我注意到外部学生也努力跟上她们的步伐，以一种在我所见证的大学课堂中超越一切的方式做好了准备，营造出信任和相互问责的氛围。

This week, we've read Book 7 of Plato's Republic and are ready for discussion. In the past few weeks, I've observed how the Inside students have raised the bar for class preparation. They show up each week with the printed texts I left with them the week before, they take good notes, and they always have their writing assignments out and ready to discuss (many of which are hand-written, given how onerous it can be for the Inside students to type and print their work). I've noticed how Outside students have stepped up to match them, showing up prepared in a way that creates a sense of trust and mutual accountability well beyond anything I've witnessed in the college classroom.

我给她们今天的写作提示是对上周讨论主题的变体：如果你知道这一切都是谎言呢？你会做出什么不同的选择？

The writing prompt I've given them for today's class is a spin on the topic of last week's discussion: what if you knew it was all a lie? What would you do differently?

学生们紧张地翻阅自己的回答。我们课堂讨论的一个原则是每个人都有话可说，这在实践中意味着每个人都必须说点什么。不过到了现在，我们知道被大家叫做「Queenie」的卡桑德拉（Cassandra）——她有一种在不同意某事时总是提出问题的非凡天赋——会以她标志性的滑稽怀疑论开启讨论。

Students anxiously look over their responses. A principle we've adopted for class discussion is that everyone has something to say, which in practice means everyone has to say something. By now, though, we know that Cassandra, who everyone calls "Queenie", and who has the remarkable gift of always asking questions when she doesn't understand something, will kick off the discussion with her trademark brand of comical scepticism.

「那个洞里到底在搞什么鬼？」她脱口而出，引来一阵笑声。「你能画个图什么的吗，因为我真不知道那个洞里到底在搞什么鬼。那些影子什么的……」我站起来去找粉笔。但就在我要在教室后面那块空白黑板上写字之前，平时课堂上讨论中相当矜持的谢尔比（Shelby），兴奋地建议来一次现场重演。原来她是戏剧专业的学生，突然指挥所有人站起来走到教室前面，急切而坚定地要即兴排演洞穴比喻。

"What the hell is going on in that cave?" she blurts out, followed by a round of laughter. "Can you draw a picture or something, because I do not know what the hell is going on in that cave. All those shadows and shit …" I stand and go in search of some chalk. But just before I mark up the blank slate of chalkboard at the back of the room, Shelby, typically quite reserved in class discussion, excitedly suggests a live re-enactment. Turns out she's a theatre major and is suddenly directing everyone to stand up and move to the front of the room, eager and determined to stage an impromptu performance of the Allegory of the Cave.

有人开玩笑说现在是局外人该「服刑」的时候了。「没错，」杰辛达（Jacynda）大声说。「你们来被关起来怎么样！或者等等，他是怎么说的来着？」她低头看着桌子，手指在面前的文本上移动，「……把你的『腿和脖子锁住』，让你连头都不能转。那才叫糟糕！」大家都很高兴看到笼罩在这一幕上的浓厚反讽是好的那种——那种我们可以一笑而过的反讽。

Someone jokes that now it's time for the Outsiders to do some time. "Yeah," Jacynda shouts. "How about y'all get locked up! Or wait, how's he put it?" she asks, looking down at the desk and moving her finger over the text in front of her "… have your 'legs and necks fettered' so you can't even move your head. That's messed up!" Everyone is glad to see that the thick air of irony hanging over this scene is the good kind, the kind of irony we can laugh at.

但当外部学生按照谢尔比的戏剧指令走到教室前面就位时，几位内部学生冲上去加入了扮演囚犯的行列，这是一个自发的团结姿态，同时也揭示了学生们在过去几周里建立的深厚纽带。

But as the Outside students go to take their place at the front of the room, following Shelby's dramaturgical orders, several Inside students rush to join the play-acting prisoners, a playful gesture of spontaneous solidarity that also reveals the meaningful bonds that have developed between the students over the past few weeks.

一位狱警透过狭窄的玻璃窗探头查看我们这边的喧闹。

A guard peeks in through the narrow glass window to check on the ruckus we have brewing.

我自己的看法是，苏格拉底（Socrates）会对这个场景非常满意。我认为这正是哲学应有的样子。大声而喧闹。每个人都参与其中。每个人都投入其中。笑话、故事、轶事和哲学洞见的万花筒般的交汇，足以让任何人忘记所谓的「人文学科危机」。但这不仅仅是更友好和有趣。过去几周涌现了一些我所见过的最高质量的哲学思考。它们突出了许多大学课堂中经常缺失的东西、许多学术会议的沉闷程序中缺失的东西，以及当然，那些被用来保护人文学科传统的官僚修辞中缺失的东西。

My own view is that Socrates would be quite pleased with this scene. I think this is what philosophy should look like. Loud and rowdy. Everyone involved. Everyone engaged. A kaleidoscopic assortment of jokes, stories, anecdotes and philosophical insights powerful enough to make anyone forget about the so-called "crisis in the humanities". But it's not just more friendly and fun. These past few weeks have turned up some of the highest-quality philosophising I've ever witnessed. They highlight what's often missing from many college classrooms, from the stale proceedings of many academic conferences, and certainly from bureaucratic rhetoric charged with protecting a tradition of humanistic studies.

「所以那个洞里就是这样的情况，」在我们热闹的重演结束时我说道，此时我们坐回到棕色长方形桌子的马蹄形排列旁。「但这个洞穴到底是一种什么样的比喻？」我问。「为什么这些想象中的囚徒认为这些阴影形状是真实的？既然他们已经解开了锁链，他们应该做什么？」

"So that's what the hell is going on in the cave," I say at the close of our lively re-enactment, as we take our seats at the horseshoe arrangement of brown, rectangular tables. "But what the hell kind of an allegory is this cave?" I ask. "Why do these imagined prisoners think these shadowy shapes are real? What are they supposed to do now that they're unchained?"

「解开束缚！」Queenie大声说。

"Un_fettered_!" Queenie shouts.

经常讲述孙女们故事的维多利亚（Victoria）迅速回应：「一开始，我是那种，『这到底是什么？』我记得你说这是，比如，几千年前的，然后我开始读的时候，我就想，是啊，听起来就像！」这引发了一阵大笑。「但后来我开始想，」她继续说道，语气变得更为严肃，「这就像在这里发生的事情。你在这里待够了就开始觉得这就是全部了。你忘了外面的世界。所以谁说了什么、谁做了什么就成了你唯一关心的。这就像你的现实，你忘了外面不是这样的。」

Victoria, who often has stories to tell about her granddaughters, quickly responds: "At first, I was like, 'What the hell is this?' I remember you said it was, like, thousands of years old and, when I started reading it, I was like, yeah, it sounds like it!" This gets a good laugh. "But then I started thinking," she continues, striking a more serious tone, "it's kinda like what happens in here. You spend enough time in here and you start to think this is all there is. You forget the world out there. So like who said what and who did what is all you start to care about. It's like your reality, and you forget it's not like that on the outside."

「除了这就是你的现实，」詹恩附和道。「你是一个囚犯，你在监狱里待了15年，因为你决定在烂醉如泥的时候上了你的车然后碾过了一个人。这就是现实。」所有人沉默了一会儿，沉浸在对两种解释都必须正确的认识中。

"Except this is your reality," Jenn chimes in. "You are a prisoner, and you've been in prison 15 years because you decided to get into your car when you were too fucked up and you ran over somebody. That's reality." Everyone is silent for a moment, sitting with the realisation that both interpretations must be right.

「但那些错误信息呢，」开创了傻袜子传统的班级开心果大卫（David）问道。「那些疯狂的 QAnon 阴谋论什么的。反疫苗人士和地平论者。另类右翼的那套东西？」

"But what about all the misinformation," asks David, the class clown who started the silly socks tradition. "All the crazy QAnon conspiracy theories and stuff. The anti-vaxxers and flat-earthers. The alt-right pipeline stuff?"

内部人面露困惑，不确定年轻人口中的这种陌生新方言。在这种时刻可以清楚地看到，除了种族、代际、社会经济和经历差异之外，有时候仅仅是一道语言障碍就将一个现实与另一个现实分隔开来。但这也在她们之间创造了沟通的契机。局外人告诉内部人什么是「深度伪造（deepfakes）」和「取消文化（cancel culture）」，内部人告诉局外人什么是「Bo-Bos」（监狱鞋）和「dry snitching」（暗地告密）——正如Queenie生动地解释的那样，就是某人不点名地告某人的密。

The Insiders look confused, unsure of this strange new dialect spoken among young people. At times like this it's clear that, on top of racial, generational, socioeconomic and experiential differences, it's sometimes just a language barrier that separates one reality from another. But this also creates openings between them. Outsiders tell Insiders about "deepfakes" and "cancel culture", and the Insiders tell Outsiders about "Bo-Bos" (prison shoes) and "dry snitching", which, as Queenie explains animatedly, is when somebody rats somebody out without naming names.

最年轻的内部学生杰辛达毫不怯于展示她敏捷出众的口才，来阐述为什么柏拉图就是想让我们思考应该信任谁：「这边这些人把我们关起来、给我们投影子，还是那边这些人进来告诉我们现在自由了，尽管你的眼睛被强光刺得生疼也跟我们走？这些家伙到底是谁？让我猜猜——他们应该是那些哲学家？」

Jacynda, the youngest Insider, is not afraid to flex her glorious gift for whip-smart oratory in laying out why Plato is just trying to get us to think about who we should trust: "These people over here locking us up and throwing shadows at us, or these people over here coming in here telling us we're free now, just come along with us even though your eyes are burning from the bright light? Who are these motherfuckers, anyway? Oh lemme guess – they're supposed to be the philosophers?"

「没错，」我承认。「那些哲学家。」大家都笑了。

"That's right," I admit. "The philosophers." And everyone has a good laugh.

哲学与监狱的关系自柏拉图时代以来已经演变，从类比变成了现实。许多伟大的哲学著作都是在监禁中写就的——例如马丁·路德·金（Martin Luther King Jr.）常被引用的《伯明翰监狱来信》（Letter from Birmingham Jail），他于1963年4月因反对种族隔离的非法抗议指控而被关押在那里。再往前回溯近一千五百年，我们发现《哲学的慰藉》（On the Consolation of Philosophy），由晚期罗马哲学家波爱修斯（Boethius）在监狱中等待以莫须有的叛国罪被处决期间写成。还有伯特兰·罗素（Bertrand Russell）的《数学哲学导论》（Introduction to Mathematical Philosophy），写于1918年在布里克斯顿（Brixton）监狱服刑期间，因为他发表了反对美国加入第一次世界大战的和平主义观点。与此同时，路德维希·维特根斯坦（Ludwig Wittgenstein）作为战俘整理了后来成为《逻辑哲学论》（Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus，1921年）的笔记。维克多·弗兰克尔（Viktor Frankl）、伊曼纽尔·列维纳斯（Emmanuel Levinas）和让-保罗·萨特（Jean-Paul Sartre）等思想家在纳粹监禁下写下了开创性的著作，意大利马克思主义思想家安东尼奥·葛兰西（Antonio Gramsci）在意大利法西斯监禁期间写下了他的狱中笔记。

Philosophy's relationship to the prison has evolved since Plato's time, from analogy to reality. So many great works of philosophy were written in incarceration – for example, Martin Luther King Jr's oft-quoted Letter from Birmingham Jail, where he was held in April 1963 on charges of unlawful protest against racial segregation. Back up nearly one and a half millennia, and we find On the Consolation of Philosophy, written by the late-Roman philosopher Boethius while in prison awaiting execution on trumped-up charges of treason. There is Bertrand Russell's Introduction to Mathematical Philosophy, written during a stint in Brixton prison in 1918 for voicing pacifist views against the US joining the first world war. Meanwhile, Ludwig Wittgenstein was a prisoner of war while he compiled the notes that became the Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus (1921). Thinkers such as Viktor Frankl, Emmanuel Levinas and Jean-Paul Sartre scrawled groundbreaking works under Nazi imprisonment, and the Italian Marxist thinker Antonio Gramsci wrote his prison notebooks while imprisoned by Italian fascists.

最终，一些哲学家也开始对监狱本身进行哲学思考。让一位英国功利主义者来为最臭名昭著的监禁惩罚概念之一制定智识和建筑蓝图吧。诚然，杰里米·边沁（Jeremy Bentham）对声名狼藉的全景监狱（panopticon）的讨论只是他洋洋洒洒的论著《惩罚的理由》（The Rationale of Punishment，1830年）的一个方面，这是一个以国家授权暴力维护法律社会秩序的功利主义论证。同样诚然，边沁的监狱计划之所以获得如此巨大的学术关注，很大程度上归功于法国后结构主义者米歇尔·福柯（Michel Foucault）的戏剧化分析。在《规训与惩罚：监狱的诞生》（Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison，1975年）中，福柯描述了通过持续的、匿名的监视来实现强制改造的理念，认为这是全景监狱这个「残酷而精巧的牢笼」的核心。这种通过单向观察实现绝对权力的概念本身已成为监狱哲学中一个有影响力的隐喻，为监视的概念语言提供了基础，这种语言已渗入对数字追踪、数据收集等方面的较新批评。

Eventually, some philosophers came around to philosophising about prisons too. Leave it to a British utilitarian to draw up both the intellectual and the architectural blueprints for one of the most notorious conceptions of carceral punishment. Granted, Jeremy Bentham's discussion of the infamous panopticon is but one aspect of his sprawling treatise The Rationale of Punishment (1830), a utilitarian justification of state-sanctioned force to uphold a social order of laws. Granted also that the outsized scholarly airtime that Bentham's prison plans have received is due largely to the dramatised analysis offered by the French poststructuralist Michel Foucault. In Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison (1975), Foucault describes the idea of enforced reform through constant, anonymous surveillance as central to the "cruel, ingenious cage" of the panopticon. This notion of absolute power through one-directional observation has itself proved an influential metaphor in the philosophy of prisons, furnishing a conceptual language of surveillance that has found its way into more recent critiques of digital tracking, data collection and the like.

不过，还是给边沁一些肯定吧。即使在不祥的不确定、匿名监视的威胁下，他将监狱想象为一个学习的场所。在单独囚禁的漩涡中应该有一个空间，囚犯可以通过教育在那里找到修复。「监狱应该是他们的学校，」边沁说，他是普及教育的坚定倡导者。然而遗憾的是，边沁心目中的是矫正性教育——一种强制的道德化影响，其价值仅在于它能「极大地帮助改变思维习惯」和「提升」自尊。

Give Bentham some credit, though. Even under the sinister threat of uncertain, anonymous surveillance, he imagined the prison as a place of learning. Somewhere within the vortex of solitary cells would be a space in which prisoners could find restoration through education. "The prison should be their school," says Bentham, a dedicated advocate of universal education. Alas, what Bentham has in mind is corrective education, an enforced moralising influence valued only for its capacity to offer "great assistance in changing the habits of the mind" and of "elevating" self-esteem.

也许教育确实能为被监禁者提供这些及类似的好处。但当然，那不完全是我们在课堂上追求的东西。那么，以监狱教育的名义，我们到底在做什么好事？

Perhaps education has these and similar benefits to offer the incarcerated. But, of course, that's not exactly what we're going for in this class. What good, then, are we doing in the name of prison education?

我们在监狱哲学方面已经走过了漫长的路。在我们继续关于监狱的哲学思考的同时，在监狱中做哲学的努力也有了可喜的增长。我强烈推荐安迪·韦斯特（Andy West）的《内在生命》（The Life Inside，2022年），一本美好的回忆录，展示了这些丰富的讨论对那些可能看似不太可能是哲学实践受益者的男性所产生的影响（另见韦斯特在Aeon发表的文章 Inside Ambiguity）。柯丝汀·希夫里斯（Kirstine Szifris）的著作《铁栏背后的哲学》（Philosophy Behind Bars，2021年）借鉴了她教授监狱课程的经验，为这种教育对被监禁者的成长和发展的价值提出了有力的论证。2005年至2013年间，《卫报》（The Guardian）刊登了一系列关于监狱中哲学的文章，其中大部分由资深监狱教育工作者艾伦·史密斯（Alan Smith）撰写。此外，虽然严格来说并非哲学文本，阿赫梅特·阿尔坦（Ahmet Altan）的自传《我将永远不再看到这个世界》（I Will Never See the World Again，2018年），关于他在土耳其（Turkey）被政治监禁的经历，充满了哲学洞见。

We've come a long way in prison philosophy. While we continue to philosophise about prison, there has been a welcome increase in efforts to philosophise in prison. I highly recommend Andy West's The Life Inside (2022), a lovely memoir that illustrates the impact of these rich discussions on men who may seem unlikely beneficiaries of philosophical practice (see also West's Aeon essay Inside Ambiguity). Kirstine Szifris's book Philosophy Behind Bars (2021) draws on her experience teaching in prison to make a compelling case for the value of this kind of education for the growth and development of incarcerated individuals. Between 2005 and 2013, the Guardian ran a series of articles on philosophy in prisons, the bulk of them written by a veteran prison educator, Alan Smith. And, while not strictly speaking a philosophical text, Ahmet Altan's autobiography I Will Never See the World Again (2018), about his political imprisonment in Turkey, is rich with philosophical insight.

与此同时，近年来涌现了大量监狱教育计划，其中许多是在高等教育的框架下。除了内部-外部监狱交流项目外，这些计划还包括位于华盛顿特区的乔治城大学（Georgetown University）的监狱与司法计划（Prisons and Justice Initiative），以及位于纽约的巴德监狱计划（Bard Prison Initiative）等。其中少数甚至是专门针对哲学的，例如英国的「监狱中的哲学」慈善机构（Philosophy in Prison），包括与上原牛津研究所（Uehiro Oxford Institute）等中心的合作推广。

Meanwhile, a host of prison education schemes have cropped up in recent years, many of them in the context of higher education. In addition to the Inside-Out Prison Exchange Program, these initiatives include the Prisons and Justice Initiative at Georgetown University in Washington DC and the Bard Prison Initiative in New York, among others. A few of these are even philosophy-specific, such as the Philosophy in Prison charity in the UK, which includes outreach collaborations with centres such as the Uehiro Oxford Institute.

当然，对柏拉图来说，不仅仅是关于逃离洞穴。同样重要的是获得自由的人返回洞穴、致力于解放他人的责任。这些及类似计划可以被视为一种现代的努力，以实现将教育用于解放目的的责任，特别是对那些被系统性地剥夺了此类机会的人。我认为这是最近被称为「公共哲学（public philosophy）」的一个关键组成部分，尤其是当它关注于一个经常被排除在哲学和社会的「公众」概念之外的群体时。与自由主义传统将「公众」抽象地构想为无差别的权利承担者群体形成鲜明对比的是，与被监禁群体接触的哲学实践的一个主要价值在于，它从一个非理想的「公众」概念出发来推进公共哲学——旨在将那些经常被排除在公共生活中心、被剥夺某些权利和机会、被切断政治和社会参与益处的人纳入其中。换言之，在监狱中教授哲学是做公共哲学的最佳方式之一。

Of course, for Plato, it isn't only about escaping the cave. Equally important is the responsibility of the liberated to return to the cave to see to the liberation of others. These and similar programmes can be seen as a modern-day effort to make good on the responsibility to put education toward emancipatory ends, particularly for those who have been systematically deprived of such opportunities. This is, I think, a crucial component of what has recently been billed as "public philosophy", especially insofar as it is focused on a population often excluded from both philosophical and social conceptions of "the public". In notable contrast to the liberalist tradition of conceiving "the public" abstractly, as an undifferentiated mass of rights-bearing individuals, one of the chief values of a philosophical practice that engages with incarcerated communities is that it proceeds from a non-ideal notion of "the public" in the name of public philosophy – one that aims to include those often excluded from the centre of public life, denied certain rights and opportunities, and cut off from the benefits of political and social participation. Put differently, teaching philosophy in prisons is one of the best ways of doing public philosophy.

然而请注意，此类计划的价值几乎总是以哲学赋予被监禁者的好处来框定的。在某个实践层面上，这必须如此，因为使我们能坐在一起讨论柏拉图《理想国》的这类计划依赖于外部的、有时不确定的资金来源，而这些资金反过来取决于说服正确的人相信监狱人口是此类计划的直接受益者。但这种思维方式在当前关于监狱中哲学价值的学术研究中也非常普遍。例如，在邓肯·普里查德（Duncan Pritchard）关于在监狱中做哲学的一篇文章中，我们了解到有实证证据表明哲学可以改善「学生的智识美德，从而改善其智识品格」以及「重要的个人和人际技能」。类似地，另有人声称监狱中的哲学「可以赋予被边缘化的人以声音，照亮不公正，揭示社会问题的根源，并赋予他人寻求解决方案的力量」。

Notice, however, that the value of such initiatives is pretty much always framed in terms of the good that philosophy affords the incarcerated. At a certain practical level, this must be so, given that programmes such as the one that make it possible for us to sit in a room and discuss Plato's Republic depend on external, sometimes uncertain sources of funding, which in turn depend on convincing the right people that the prison population is the direct beneficiary of such programmes. But this way of thinking is also very much present in current academic research on the value of philosophy in prisons. In an essay by Duncan Pritchard on doing philosophy in prisons, for example, we learn there is empirical evidence that philosophy can improve "the intellectual virtues, and thereby the intellectual character, of the students" as well as "important personal and interpersonal skills". Similarly, others have claimed that philosophy in prisons "can give voice to those who are marginalised, shine a light on injustices, expose the root of social problems and empower others to seek solutions".

假设这一切都是正确的。假设在监狱中做哲学的好处是丰富的，益处是真实的。但问题仍然是，这种益处是否以及在多大程度上是相互的。假设哲学确实能为监狱人口做大事，我们也可以问：监狱中的哲学能为哲学做什么？

Suppose all of this is right. Suppose the virtues of doing philosophy in prisons are plentiful and the benefits are real. Still, the question remains whether and to what extent the benefit is mutual. Assuming philosophy can and does do great things for the prison population, we might also ask: what can philosophy in prisons do for philosophy?

需要澄清的是——这里的问题与其说是「我，哲学家，从中得到了什么？」当然，我确实认为哲学家可以从被监禁学生经常提供的深刻洞见中学到很多。但如果我们讨论的是教授哲学的价值——即帮助学生批判性地思考当下——那么把学生放在监狱中会收获很多。学生们也同意这一点。

To clarify – the question here is not so much, "What do I, the philosopher, get out of it?" Of course, I do think philosophers can and do learn a great deal from the striking insights that incarcerated students regularly offer. But if we're discussing the value of teaching philosophy – that is, helping students to think critically about the present – much is to be gained by putting students in prisons. And the students agree.

要理解原因，让我们从对我们Z世代学生通常做出的一些较为正面的概括开始。这些包括：适应力、数字素养、政治行动主义（特别是在多样性、平等和社会正义的名义下）、在面对令人震惊的全球灾难时保持乐观的显著能力，等等。然而，并不清楚进行不同观点的建设性交流的能力是否属于这些优点。让我先说，我完全了解保守派和右翼媒体经常描绘的关于大学校园里发生了什么的荒诞漫画（看看对所谓「安全空间（safe spaces）」的歇斯底里就知道了）。但我听到许多教育界同行如今做出的一个概括，表达了对学生中某些人是否愿意倾听不同观点、并在哲学家威尔弗里德·塞拉斯（Wilfrid Sellars）所说的「理由空间（the space of reasons）」中善意地寻求共同点的真正担忧。

To understand why, let's begin with some of the more positive generalisations commonly made about our gen Z students. These include: adaptability, digital literacy, political activism (particularly in the name of diversity, equality and social justice), a striking ability to remain optimistic in the face of staggering global calamity, and plenty more. It's not clear, however, that an aptitude for a productive exchange of differing views belongs with these virtues. Let me say first that I'm fully aware of the absurd caricature often drawn by reactionaries and rightwing media of what happens on college campuses (look no further than the fit-throwing over so-called "safe spaces"). But a generalisation I hear many of my co-educators making today expresses genuine concern about the willingness of some students to hear out different perspectives and make good-faith efforts to seek common ground within what the philosopher Wilfrid Sellars called "the space of reasons".

据我所见，毫无疑问我们学生的心是在正确的地方的。但任何目光清醒的教育者都能告诉你，正在发生一种价值转变——对学生们来说重要的是别人看到他们的美德，而不是我们有效地推广这些品质，或者真正成功地改变人心和思想。这对哲学家来说尤其令人不安，因为他们的事业就是给出理由和索取理由。确实，有时学生的发言听起来更像是在寻求同伴们一轮赞许的手指响声的宣言，而不是对课堂讨论的真正贡献。但更令人担忧的是，与多种多样视角的参与度的下降，威胁着把课堂变成一个回音室——一个你不再信任另一边的人的地方。

So far as I can see, there's little doubt that our students' hearts are in the right place. But as any clear-eyed educator can tell you, there's something of a value shift going on, where what matters to the students is that other people see their virtue, not that we effectively promote these qualities, or actually succeed in changing hearts and minds. This should be particularly disconcerting for philosophers, who are in the business of giving and asking for reasons. It's true that sometimes students' comments sound more like proclamations looking for a round of approving finger snaps from peers than genuine contributions to class discussion. More worrying, however, is that diminishing engagement with a range of diverse perspectives threatens to turn the classroom into an echo chamber – a place where you no longer trust people from the other side.

所有这些在我学生踏入监狱的那一刻就消失了。没有预先批准的话术炫耀。没有语言警察。没有迫在眉睫的被取消威胁。在除了尊重交流的标准期望之外没有任何更多指导的情况下，课堂立刻——仿佛是魔法般地——培养出了一种倾听而非评判的风气，塑造了我们共同经历的整个体验。内部学生毫不费力地说清楚了这个独特机会的价值。对一些人来说，这是一种与外部世界联系的方式，有助于为重新回归社会做准备。对另一些人来说，这是在简历上好看的大专学分。对还有一些人来说，这是从监禁的枯燥中获得的有意义的消遣。然而它对外部学生的价值却更难表述。

All of this disappeared the moment my students stepped into a prison. No flexing of pre-approved talking points. No language policing. No impending threats of cancellation. With no more guidance from me than the standard expectations of respectful discourse, the class immediately cultivated, as if by magic, an ethos of listening-not-judging that shaped the entirety of our experience together. Inside students had no trouble making clear the value of this unique opportunity. For some, it was a way to connect with the outside world that helped prepare for re-entry. For others, it was college credit that would look good on a résumé. For others still, it was a meaningful distraction from the monotony of incarceration. The value it held for Outside students, however, is harder to articulate.

让我举个例子。在编写这门课的教学大纲时，我犹豫是否要加入一个关于监狱哲学的章节。一方面，我看到的都是地雷。外部学生会愿意分享关于监狱的想法吗，有人愿意让他们分享吗？内部学生想分享关于监狱的什么样的想法？如果课堂上在监狱里掀起了一场监狱废奴运动怎么办？也许我们还是只讨论监狱比喻算了，我想。

Let me give an example. When putting together the syllabus for this course, I debated whether or not to include a section on the philosophy of prisons. On one hand, I saw only landmines. Would Outside students want to share any thoughts about prisons, and would anyone want them to? What kinds of thought would the Inside students want to share about prison? What if the class spawned a prison abolitionist movement within a prison? Maybe we should just stick to prison analogies, I thought.

另一方面，如果安吉拉·戴维斯（Angela Davis）的著作能告诉我们什么的话，那就是要运用你的想象力，冒一些风险，尝试一些不同的东西。而且恰好汤米·谢尔比（Tommie Shelby）的精彩专著《监狱废奴的理念》（The Idea of Prison Abolition，2022年）刚刚出版：其与戴维斯的广泛对话在为监狱改革辩护方面做得极为出色，成为戴维斯的重磅之作《监狱过时了吗？》（Are Prisons Obsolete?，2003年）的绝佳伴侣。于是我冒了个险，想象事情会顺利。

On the other hand, if the writings of Angela Davis tell us anything, it is to use your imagination, take some risks and try something different. It also happened that Tommie Shelby's superb monograph The Idea of Prison Abolition (2022) had just been published: with its extensive engagement with Davis in making the case for prison reform, it made an excellent companion to Davis's bombshell Are Prisons Obsolete? (2003). So I took a risk and imagined it would go well.

这堂课的引子是一个简单的问题：「假设监狱不完美，我们应该试图修复它们，还是应该试图废除它们？快，不要想——举手：左手修复，右手废除。数到三，预备……」

The primer to this, however, was a simple question: "Assuming prisons aren't perfect, should we try to fix them, or should we try to get rid of them? Quick, without thinking – show of hands: left hand fix, right hand abolish. On the count of three, ready …"

整整10只右手举了起来——每一只都是外部学生的——选择废除，而所有举起来选择修复的9只左手都是内部学生的。（黛比在本周早些时候已被转移。）

Exactly 10 right hands were raised – every single one the hand of an Outside student – to abolish, while all the nine left hands raised to fix were of an Inside student. (Debbie had been transferred earlier in the week.)

这场壮观的意见分歧之后，课堂变得如此喧闹，以至于一位狱警走过来在窗户上敲了敲他的指关节。

Things got so loud and rowdy after this spectacular show of difference that a guard stopped by and tapped his knuckles on the window.

接下来的周五早上，就在傻袜子之后，我要求同样的举手表决。这一次，没有人举手。这是因为，他们每人都准备好解释——文本和笔记已备好——事实证明改革者和废奴主义者并没有那么不同。所有人都在等着Queenie，她只是说：「哦，我现在爱安吉拉了。」（她们已经直呼其名了。）

The following Friday morning, just after silly socks, I ask for the same show of hands. This time, no one raises a hand. This is because, as each of them is ready to explain, with texts and notes at the ready, it turns out that reformers and abolitionists aren't that different after all. Everyone is waiting for Queenie, who just says: "Oh, I love Angela now." (They're on first-name terms.)

但这周，开启讨论的是另一个人。西蒙（Simone）——在课堂上一直很少说话，直到上周为监狱废奴辩护时才开口——早早跳出来发言，说汤米基本上同意安吉拉，因为他们都在谈论相同的监狱改革：社会服务、戒毒治疗、教育。

But this week, it's someone else who kicks things off. Simone, who had said very little in class until last week when it was time to advocate for prison abolition, jumps in early to say that Tommie pretty much agrees with Angela because they're both talking about the same reforms to prison: social services, drug treatment, education.

「没错，」Queenie表示同意，并解释说安吉拉真正想说的是「与其只是把人扔进监狱因为他们做了不该做的事，他们应该用用想象力。冒一些风险。尝试一些不同的东西。」

"Yeah," Queenie concurs, and explains that what Angela is really trying to say is that "instead of just throwing folks in prison for doing something they shouldn't, they should just use their imagination. Take some risks. Try something different."

一声喇叭的噼啪声打断了讨论，接着是一连串的蜂鸣声，一个比一个令人不安。「封锁，」几位内部人异口同声地说。「我们要在这里待一会儿了，」杰斯补充道。就这样，我们安然地回到讨论中。

A crackle of loudspeaker interrupts, followed by a sequence of beeps, each more disconcerting than the last. "Lockdown," say several Insiders in unison. "We're gonna be here for a while," Jess adds. And with that, we settle right back into discussion.
