亭子的三扇门

AI Parable (inspired by Amanda Askell)·每日精选·2026年6月28日·2分钟阅读

在古城里有一座亭子，开着三扇门（three doors），其中两扇后面只有灰尘和蛛网，而一扇后面藏着一锭纯金的奖品。每到傍晚，会有一位旅人受邀挑选一扇门；同样每到傍晚，亭子的守护者（the keeper）——他确切知道金子藏在哪里——会打开旅人没有挑选的两扇门中的一扇，露出里面的灰尘。然后，守护者礼貌地鞠一躬，问旅人是否愿意保留最初的选择，还是换到另一扇仍然紧闭的门。

In the old city there stood a pavilion with three doors, and behind two of them waited only dust and cobwebs, while behind one glittered a prize of pure gold. Each evening a traveler was invited to choose a door, and each evening the keeper of the pavilion, who knew precisely where the gold rested, would open one of the two doors the traveler had not chosen, revealing nothing but dust. Then, with a courteous bow, the keeper would ask the traveler whether he wished to keep his first choice or move to the other door that remained closed.

多数旅人都为自己的初次猜测感到骄傲，不肯挪动脚步。「门终归是门，」他们会说，「如今只剩两扇关着，必然是一半对一半。换门就等于承认自己错了，而承认错了便是冒犯命运。」于是一个又一个夜晚，他们坚守原地，大约三次中就有两次空手而归。几个倔强者甚至吹嘘说换门是意志薄弱的表现，命运只眷顾坚定之人。

Most travelers, proud of their first guess, refused to budge. "A door is a door," they would say, "and there are two closed now, so surely it is half and half. To move is to admit I was wrong, and to admit I was wrong is to tempt the fates." And so, evening after evening, they stayed where they stood, and roughly two times out of three they went home empty-handed. A few stubborn ones even boasted that switching showed a weak spirit, and that fortune favored the resolute.

后来来了一位老妇人（an old woman），她多年来一直观察着这座亭子，她懂得了旁人不曾懂的事。她想：当守护者打开一扇满是灰尘的门时，他并不是随手为之，因为他绝不能露出金子。所以他打开的那扇门并非一个独立的事件、随随便便的恩赐；而是一个经过思量的仁慈之举，是把一条信息白白递给任何一个肯用心接收的人。这份仁慈悄悄倾斜了旅人脚下的大地，尽管门本身一寸未动。

But there came an old woman who had watched the pavilion for many years, and she understood something the others did not. When the keeper opens a dusty door, she reasoned, he is not choosing at random, for he must never reveal the gold. So his opened door is not an independent event, lightly offered; it is a deliberate act of mercy, a piece of information handed freely to anyone wise enough to receive it. That mercy tilts the very ground the travelers stand on, though the doors themselves never move.

她对他们说：想想你们最初的猜测值多少吧。一开始，三扇门只有一锭金，你选的门只有三分之一的可能藏着金子；另外三分之二，金子在你没选的门后面。如今守护者受他自己的规矩约束，抹去了那两扇中的一扇，而另一排里仍然紧闭着的那扇，实质上成了全部第二份概率的继承者。所以换门并不是在「一半对一半」里再赌一次；而是承接了「你最初猜错了」的那三分之二机会。坚守的旅人攥着三分之一的希望；换门的旅人则带着三分之二。

Consider, she told them, what your first guess was worth. In the beginning, with three doors and one prize, your chosen door held the gold only one time in three; two times in three, the gold was behind one of the doors you did not pick. Now the keeper, bound by his rule, has swept away one of those two, and what remains standing closed in that other row is, in effect, the heir to all that second probability. To switch, then, is not to gamble again at half and half; it is to inherit the two-thirds chance that your first guess was wrong. The traveler who stays clings to a one-third hope; the traveler who moves carries two-thirds.

等到她终于受邀上前时，老妇人指了指自己中意的第一扇门，平静地等守护者打开他那扇满是灰尘的门，然后毫不犹豫地走向剩下那扇紧闭的门。人群低声议论她鲁莽。然而她赢的次数远多于输的次数，渐渐地，年轻的旅人开始效仿她，直到「坚守」成了新手的标记，而「换门」成了智者的习惯。门没有变，金子也没有挪动；改变的只是旅人对「守护者打开的那扇门究竟意味着什么」的理解。

When at last she was invited to choose, the old woman pointed to the first door she fancied, waited calmly for the keeper to open his dusty one, and then, without a flicker of doubt, walked to the remaining closed door. The crowd murmured that she was reckless. Yet she won far more often than she lost, and in time the younger travelers began to imitate her, until staying became the mark of the novice and switching the habit of the wise. The doors had not changed, nor had the gold moved; only the travelers' understanding of what the keeper's opened door truly meant.

这个故事揭示了「蒙提霍尔问题」（the Monty Hall problem）的本质——一个表面看来像是在两扇同样可能的门之间做公平抉择的谜题，实则是一堂关于「新信息如何改变旧概率」的课。当你最初在三扇门中挑一扇时，你有三分之一的可能猜对，另有三分之二的可能奖品在另外两扇门后面。因为主持人知道奖品在哪里，而且必须从另一对门里打开一扇空门，所以他的揭示并非随机；他把那三分之二的概率全部汇集到你没有选的那扇仅剩的门上。因此换门大约三次能赢两次，而坚守只能赢一次。道理在于：主持人受约束的选择本身就是信息，依此行事，便能把「一半对一半」的天真直觉转化为经过度量的「二比一」优势。

This story reveals the essence of the Monty Hall problem — a puzzle that seems, on its face, to be a fair choice between two equally likely doors, but is in fact a lesson about how new information shifts old odds. When you first pick one of three doors, you have a one-third chance of being right and a two-thirds chance that the prize lies behind one of the other two. Because the host knows where the prize is and must always open an empty door from the other pair, his reveal is not random; it funnels all of that two-thirds probability onto the single remaining door you did not choose. Switching therefore wins about two times out of three, while staying wins only one time out of three. The lesson is that the host's constrained choice is information, and acting on it turns a naive fifty-fifty feeling into a measured two-to-one advantage.
