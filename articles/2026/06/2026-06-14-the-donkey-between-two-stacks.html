两垛干草之间的驴

AI Parable (inspired by Amanda Askell)·每日精选·2026年6月14日·1分钟阅读

太阳高悬在打谷场上，农夫去了集市，留下他的灰驴站在两垛新鲜干草之间。驴向左看去：那垛干草金灿灿的，散发着甜香，是黎明时分从南边草地割来的。它向右看去：那垛干草一样金灿灿，一样甜香，从同一片草地、同一时刻割来。它把重心移向左边的草垛，但当鼻尖凑近时，一个念头——或者说一种感觉——让它停了下来。右边的草垛同样近，同样芬芳，同样丰盛。为什么要偏爱其中一个？有什么理由能在右边提供同样承诺的情况下，为选择左边提供正当性？

The sun hung high over the threshing floor, and the farmer had gone to market, leaving his grey donkey standing between two stacks of fresh hay. The donkey looked to the left: that stack was golden and sweet-smelling, cut from the south meadow at dawn. He looked to the right: that stack was just as golden, just as sweet, cut from the same meadow at the same hour. He shifted his weight toward the left stack, but as his muzzle came close, a thought — or rather, a feeling — stopped him. The right stack was equally near, equally fragrant, equally abundant. Why favor one over the other? What reason could justify choosing left when right offered the same promise?

它退回到正中间。它把头转向左，再转向右，又转向左。每次它朝一垛干草迈步，局势的对称性就把它拉回来。距离完全相同。品质完全相同。它对两者的饥饿感完全相同。如果它选择了左边，那不是因为左边更好——那仅仅是一个选择，而一个没有理由的选择根本不算选择，驴似乎正是这样认为的，站在尘土中，什么也没嚼。

He stepped back to the center. He turned his head left, then right, then left again. Each time he moved toward one stack, the symmetry of the situation pulled him back. The distance was identical. The quality was identical. His hunger was equal toward both. If he chose the left, it would not be because the left was better — it would only be a choice, and a choice made without reason is no choice at all, or so the donkey seemed to believe, standing there in the dust, chewing nothing.

上午过去了。其他动物走来，从两垛干草的边缘吃着。农场里的狗小跑过来，毫不犹豫地从右边的草垛叼走一口，又从左边叼走一口，根本不在乎哪边是哪边。驴看着它们，隐约感到它们那种简单做法的正确——吃就是了，动就是了——但它无法复制。对它而言，选项的完全等同已经变成了一种瘫痪。两垛干草看起来越完美地平衡，通过选择来打破平衡就越不可能。每个选项的正当性与另一个相同，既然没有哪边的正当性更强，哪一边都无法胜出。

The morning passed. Other animals came and ate from the edges of both stacks. The farm dog trotted by and stole a mouthful from the right stack without hesitation, then another from the left, not caring which was which. The donkey watched them and felt the correctness of their simple approach — just eat, just move — but he could not duplicate it. For him, the very equality of the options had become a kind of paralysis. The more perfectly balanced the two stacks appeared, the more impossible it became to break the balance by choosing. Each option was as justified as the other, and since neither justification was stronger, neither could prevail.

到了下午晚些时候，驴已经在两垛干草之间走出了一个紧凑的八字形，从不投入，总是在后退。它的腿变得沉重。它的肚子空了。干草——两垛都——它一口也没碰，虽然其他动物已经在边缘啃过。它隐约明白，任何一个选择都能救它。它也明白，拒绝选择本身就是一种选择——最糟糕的那种——但这种理解并没有解放它。对称性依然存在。两垛干草仍然以同等的力量召唤它。于是，灰驴，困在两个完美等同的好东西之间，一直站着，直到再也站不住，安静地倒在它们之间的干地上，什么也没有选择，什么也都失去了。

By late afternoon, the donkey had walked a tight figure-eight between the stacks, never committing, always retreating. His legs grew heavy. His stomach hollowed out. The hay — both stacks — remained untouched by him, though others had nibbled at the margins. He understood, dimly, that any choice would have saved him. He understood that the refusal to choose was itself a choice — the worst one — but this understanding did not free him. The symmetry still held. The two stacks still beckoned with equal force. And so the grey donkey, caught between two perfectly equal goods, stood still until he could no longer stand, and folded quietly onto the dry ground between them, having chosen nothing and having lost everything.

这个故事揭示了布里丹之驴（Buridan's ass）的本质——一个哲学悖论，描述了这样一种情境：一个理性的行动者面对两个同等诱人的选项，又没有理由偏爱其中任何一个，可能完全无法做出任何决定。这个悖论归属于中世纪哲学家让·布里丹（Jean Buridan），说明选项之间的完美对称如何导致瘫痪而非自由。它挑战了「更多选项总会让我们更好」的假设，并表明理性在为每个选择要求正当性时，可以变成自身的陷阱：一个只在有充分理由时才行动的生物，在没有理由能使天平倾斜时，可能会完全无法行动。

This story reveals the essence of Buridan's ass — the philosophical paradox that describes a situation where a rational agent, faced with two equally attractive options and no reason to prefer one over the other, may be unable to make any decision at all. The paradox, attributed to the medieval philosopher Jean Buridan, illustrates how perfect symmetry between choices can produce paralysis rather than freedom. It challenges the assumption that more options always make us better off, and shows that rationality, when it demands a justification for every choice, can become its own trap: a creature that will only act for good reasons may fail to act when no reason tips the scale.
